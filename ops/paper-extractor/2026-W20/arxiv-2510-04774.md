### One-line summary

A SoNS-equipped robot swarm, when its decentralized behavior gets stuck, automatically queries an external LLM for replacement code and executes it across the swarm, completing the mission in 85% of trials across 6-robot hardware and >30-robot simulation runs.

## Pass 2 — Content grasp

### Format note

This is a 3-page IROS 2025 workshop short paper (MACRAI: Multi-Agent Cooperative Systems and Swarm Robotics in the Era of Generative AI), not a full-length archival paper. The paper has an Abstract, Introduction, a combined Results-and-Discussion section, two algorithm listings (Methods pseudocode; example LLM-returned code), and a 2-figure / 8-reference footprint. Pass 2 reflects that compressed scope; what would be a methods section in a full paper is here a single pseudocode listing.

### 1. Main argument and supporting evidence

The paper argues that the authors' previously-introduced **self-organizing nervous system (SoNS)** — a middleware that lets a robot swarm self-organize a *temporarily centralized* dynamic hierarchy with an interchangeable "brain" robot at the root — supplies precisely the two properties an online LLM-based code-generation loop needs in order to be tractable for a swarm: (i) **ease of behavior design**, because SoNS's hierarchical separation of global from local actuation means the LLM can be asked to write a *global* behavior directly rather than a local rule whose group-level emergent effect must be reverse-engineered; and (ii) **global estimation of the swarm and its environment**, because sensor information is forwarded upstream through the SoNS network to culminate at the brain robot, giving any single robot (the current brain) an aggregated view to hand the LLM. The supporting evidence is a proof-of-concept demonstration in which a SoNS-equipped swarm, charged with moving forward while maintaining a square formation and given no a priori obstacle-avoidance code, gets physically stuck against unknown obstacles; the current brain robot then opens a generic-web-API conversation with DeepSeek R1 over OpenRouter, sends the SoNS context plus its current sensor information plus the open-ended prompt "There seems to be something wrong, can you check what happened and improve my code?", receives Lua code, swaps it into the MISSION program, and propagates the new program through the SoNS hierarchy so all robots execute it. Across 1 hardware run with 6 robots and 20 simulation runs with 34 robots (25 ground + 9 aerial), 17/20 simulated missions completed — the headline 85% success rate. The argument's load-bearing move is the framing that SoNS's pre-existing properties are *enabling conditions* for the loop rather than new contributions; the new contribution is the runtime solicit-generate-execute integration plus the success-rate evidence.

### 2. The Big Question

How can fully self-organized swarm behaviors — long acknowledged in the field (Dorigo & Theraulaz 2020; Hamann 2018; Brambilla et al. 2013) to be analytically intractable and slow to transfer from simulation to real robots — be designed, repaired, or extended *at runtime* without abandoning the scalability, flexibility, and fault-tolerance benefits that motivated self-organization in the first place? The paper is positioned as one move in this larger problem: rather than improving offline automatic design (the Francesca & Birattari 2016 / Hasselmann et al. 2021 lineage) or making local robots self-diagnose for LLM consumption (the Strobel et al. LLM2Swarm 2024 lineage), it argues that a self-organizing *hierarchy* is the missing structural ingredient that makes LLM code generation tractable for a collective.

### 3. Specific design claims being defended

This is a systems paper without explicit hypothesis-testing protocol, so the relevant unit is *design claims*. The paper defends:

- **DC1.** SoNS's separation of global from local actuation makes it tractable for an LLM to write a *global* swarm behavior directly, sidestepping the analytic intractability of designing a local rule whose emergent effect produces a desired group behavior.
- **DC2.** SoNS's upstream sensor-aggregation gives the current brain robot an estimate of the global swarm-and-environment configuration (with bounded staleness — "a maximum update delay of n+1 steps, where n is the depth of the rooted graph"), which is the global context an LLM needs to write useful code for the swarm.
- **DC3.** Because the SoNS-rooted swarm can be programmed *as if* it were a single robot with a reconfigurable body, the LLM request can be framed as a single-robot request — "much more straightforward than what would be required in robot swarms with flat (i.e., single-level and fully decentralized) system architectures."
- **DC4.** The propagation step — the brain swapping in the LLM-returned MISSION program and sending it to its children, who recursively send it to theirs — is sufficient to update all robots in the swarm in a self-organized manner.
- **DC5.** The combined loop is empirically operational: 85% mission success across 20 simulation trials, plus a successful hardware demonstration, supports the claim that the integration works in proof-of-concept.

Note: the paper does *not* defend a claim that the loop is reliable, safe, or production-ready — the framing is "proof-of-concept demonstrations" throughout, and the discussion section opens explicit threads about safety, code-vs-update-able separation, and request principle as future work.

### 4. Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss for a non-specialist reader

- **Self-organizing nervous system (SoNS)** — the authors' prior construct (Zhu, Oğuz, Heinrich et al., *Science Robotics* 2024, ref [7]): a middleware that lets a robot swarm self-assemble a temporary, dynamic, rooted-graph hierarchy among peers. Robots take parent/child roles in the graph; one robot at the root is the "brain." The hierarchy is reconfigurable — any robot can become brain, and the structure heals as robots join/leave — so the swarm gains *temporarily centralized* coordination without giving up the fault-tolerance of decentralization.
- **SoNS-brain / brain robot** — the robot currently occupying the root of the SoNS rooted graph. It is the aggregation point for upstream sensor info and the source of downstream actuation commands (and, in this paper, the robot that talks to the LLM).
- **Global vs local actuation (v_GLOBAL, w_GLOBAL vs v_LOCAL, w_LOCAL)** — SoNS's velocity components: GLOBAL components are commanded top-down through the hierarchy (formation-level motion); LOCAL components are the individual robot's own corrections (e.g., obstacle avoidance applied per-robot). The LLM-generated code in this paper writes to both, but the separation is what makes the global behavior LLM-writable.
- **Rooted graph / depth n** — the SoNS hierarchy is a directed rooted tree; "depth n" is the longest path from root to leaf. The "maximum update delay of n+1 steps" is the worst-case latency for a sensor reading at a leaf to reach the brain.
- **DeepSeek R1** — the reasoning-tuned LLM (Guo et al., *Nature* 2025, ref [8]) used as the external code generator, accessed via the OpenRouter API.
- **OpenRouter API** — a unified web API for routing requests to multiple LLM providers; the paper uses it as a single endpoint so the LLM is interchangeable.
- **Lua** — the lightweight scripting language the code is requested in (small interpreter, easy to hot-swap into a running robot program).
- **Tethered aerial robots** — in the hardware demo, the 2 aerial robots are physically tethered to the ceiling (the paper notes this is the only deviation from the setup in ref [7]). Implication: the aerial robots provide overhead perspective but are not flying freely.
- **Square formation shape** — the swarm's default mission constraint: move forward while maintaining a square shape. Provides a measurable group-level state for "stuck" detection (robots stop progressing).
- **Differential drive** — the ground-robot drivetrain (two independently driven wheels). Relevant because Example 1's LLM-returned code explicitly adjusts the local velocity component "due to using differential drive robots" (Alg. 2, line 8 comment) — i.e., the LLM produced code that is aware of the actuator geometry.
- **LLM2Swarm (ref [6], Strobel et al. 2024)** — the prior work the paper distinguishes itself from: that work has *individual* robots in a swarm self-diagnose and locally validate LLM-generated code; this paper argues that approach still leaves open the problem of designing individual behaviors that produce a desired *group* behavior, which is exactly what the SoNS hierarchy resolves by letting the LLM write the global behavior directly.

### 5. Figure-by-figure analysis

**Fig. 1 — Demo keyframes (hardware + simulation).**
- (a) Real-robot demo: 4 ground robots + 2 tethered aerial robots in an arena with 2 unknown obstacles. Four keyframes show: (i) robots get stuck and request help; (ii) robots use newly generated code to avoid the first obstacle; (iii) robots avoid the second obstacle; (iv) robots surpass obstacles and complete the mission.
- (b) Simulation trial: 25 ground + 9 aerial robots, 15 obstacles. Two abstracted top-down keyframes show the same stuck → request → execute → avoid pattern at scale, with the SoNS hierarchy drawn as edges between robots.
- *Takeaway:* the figure carries the qualitative existence-proof of the loop end-to-end. Sample size for the hardware is n=1 demonstration run; the simulation example is one of the 17 successful runs. Axes/error bars are not applicable (these are keyframes, not measurements).
- *Caveat for the reader:* the figure asserts the loop works; it does not quantify *how* the swarm avoids obstacles in detail — that has to be read off the algorithm listings.

**Fig. 2 — Task duration histogram, 20 simulation trials.**
- Histogram of elapsed time (s) for the 17 successful trials, binned in 5-s buckets from 325 to 409 s, plus a single red bar labeled "Failed trials" with count 3.
- Successful-trial distribution peaks at 340–349 s (4 trials) with a tail out to 400–409 s. The cluster is tight (most trials within ~325–375 s) but the right tail shows code-variation effects — the paper notes "with some strategies enabling the robots to complete the task more quickly."
- *Takeaway:* the headline 85% is 17/20; the failures are visible as a small but non-zero count. No statistical comparison is drawn against a baseline (no non-SoNS or no-LLM condition in this histogram).
- *Quality of the figure as evidence:* honest — it shows the failure count rather than hiding it, and it doesn't fit a curve or claim significance.

**Algorithm 1 — Methods pseudocode** (not a figure, but the paper's load-bearing methodological artifact).
- Three functions: PREPARECONVERSATION (declares "I am a leader of a swarm, can you write some Lua program to control the swarm for me?" plus SoNS context: velocity components, robot capabilities including differential drive, relative coordinate frame, sensing capabilities, known environment components incl. dimensions of robots and potential obstacles, declared safety distance, mission goal, functions needed, overall format); REQUESTHELP (appends positional info of all detected obstacles + robots to the conversation string and the canonical prompt *"There seems to be something wrong, can you check what happened and improve my code?"*, then POSTs to OpenRouter); MAIN (initiates SoNS, calls PREPARECONVERSATION once, then loops MISSION).
- The MISSION function defines the stuck-detection trigger explicitly (lines 16–20): if robot r_i has stopped moving forward, increment a TIMER; *if TIMER > θ_1, call REQUESTHELP*. After receiving a reply, extract code, update MISSION, and send the updated program to neighbor robots. The complementary branch (lines 27–29): if a new MISSION program is received from a neighbor, update and forward — this is the propagation mechanism that spreads the LLM's code through the SoNS graph.
- *Takeaway:* the LLM is invoked exactly when the per-robot stuck-timer exceeds threshold θ_1, and the state sent to the LLM is the SoNS-aggregated obstacle and robot positions plus the static SoNS context. The trigger is per-robot but, because of the SoNS hierarchy, the actor that talks to the LLM is the brain (the paper says in §II: "one robot sends its available information"; and "the SoNS-brain then updates its mission-specific program with the generated code returned by the LLM and sends the updated program to the robots it is directly connected to").

**Algorithm 2 — Two example LLM-returned algorithms.**
- *Example 1:* For each detected obstacle, if distance < θ_2, generate a local-velocity adjustment v_LOCAL, w_LOCAL away from the obstacle; explicitly correct for differential-drive geometry (line 8 comment). If the robot is the current SoNSBRAIN, call MOVEFORWARD with v_GLOBAL, w_GLOBAL and propagate to children. *Interpretation:* the LLM produced code that uses both layers of the SoNS velocity stack — local for obstacle deflection, global for sustained forward motion.
- *Example 2:* Same local-deflection pattern for nearby obstacles; the brain's branch adds a non-trivial step (lines 22–29): if there is no obstacle within detected distance θ_3, call MOVEFORWARD with current global velocity; *else*, generate a new global velocity *towards the side with fewer obstacles* and call MOVEFORWARD at reduced speed. *Interpretation:* this is structurally more sophisticated — it routes the whole swarm around dense-obstacle regions at the global layer rather than only deflecting locally.
- *Takeaway:* the two examples illustrate genuine variation in the LLM's strategies across trials ("there was some variety in the code the LLM returned") — the loop is not deterministic, and the histogram tail in Fig. 2 is the runtime cost of that variation.

### 7. Confusions / unresolved points

- **The 15% failure cases are characterized only at a single-sentence level of granularity.** The paper's only direct statement on what went wrong (§II, Results and Discussion): *"In the three trials that we consider unsuccessful (see Fig. 2), the LLM returned code that removed mechanisms that enabled the current SoNS-brain to detect when other robots in the swarm were stuck, and thus some robots were left behind while the rest completed the mission."* So: in all 3 failures, the LLM's returned code *broke the stuck-detection sensing pathway*, the swarm split, and a sub-group was left behind. The paper does not say whether the 3 failures were the same kind of broken-stuck-detection bug or three different variants, whether the brain identified itself as stuck and could have re-requested code, or whether the failure was recoverable. No statistical analysis is offered; the n=3/20 is reported flatly.
- **The hardware result is n=1 ("1 demonstration with 6 real robots").** The 85% number is from simulation only. The hardware run is qualitative evidence the loop runs end-to-end on physical robots, not statistical evidence of reliability on hardware. The abstract's "in a demonstration with 6 real robots and simulation trials with >30 robots" reads as if the 85% applies across both — it does not; the 85% is the simulation number alone.
- **When the LLM is invoked is per-robot, but only one robot talks to it.** Algorithm 1 line 20 triggers REQUESTHELP whenever any robot's stuck-timer exceeds θ_1, but §II says "one robot sends its available information" and identifies that robot as "the current SoNS-brain." The reconciliation is implicit (presumably brain promotion concentrates the request) but not spelled out for a non-specialist reader who hasn't read ref [7].
- **The request prompt is open-ended by design.** The paper acknowledges this explicitly ("the request they made to the LLM was fairly open-ended") and flags it as a limitation: "in this paper, robots requested help anytime they got stuck and the behaviors the LLM returned were simple. Future work could study more principled approaches to requests for help and more complicated tasks." The 85% number therefore characterizes a *generic-prompt + simple-task* regime; it should not be read as a general capability claim.
- **No separation between static code and update-able code is enforced.** Future-work item: "Future work could investigate more stringent separation of static code and update-able code, especially when the safety requirements are more complicated than the simple safety distances used in this paper." Read as: the LLM currently has the latitude to overwrite *any* part of MISSION, including (as the failure analysis shows) the stuck-detection logic itself. This is a structural fragility the authors flag but do not address.
- **What state exactly is sent to the LLM.** PREPARECONVERSATION (Alg. 1, lines 2–4) lists "the SoNS context (v_LOCAL, w_LOCAL, v_GLOBAL, w_GLOBAL velocity components), robot capabilities (differential drive, relative coordinate frame, sensing capabilities, known environment components (dimensions of the robots and potential obstacles), declared safety distance to an obstacle), mission goal (move forward at a desired speed), functions needed and example of overall format." REQUESTHELP (line 6) adds "positional information of all obstacles and all robots detected by robot r_i to String." So the state is: *static* SoNS/robot/mission context (sent once, at startup) + *dynamic* positions of detected obstacles and robots at the moment of stuck (sent at request time). The brain's "global estimate" is therefore concretely instantiated as obstacle-and-robot positions aggregated upstream through SoNS.
- **No comparison or ablation.** There is no non-SoNS baseline (e.g., flat decentralized swarm with the same LLM loop), no no-LLM baseline (e.g., SoNS with hand-coded obstacle avoidance), and no alternate-LLM comparison (e.g., a non-reasoning model). The 85% is reported in isolation; whether SoNS-the-hierarchy or DeepSeek-R1-the-reasoner is doing the heavy lifting is not separable from these results.
- **Hedging calibration check.** The paper's hedging is well-calibrated: "proof-of-concept demonstrations" (§II opening), "can automatically solicit and run code" (abstract — capability claim grounded in the n=17/20), "we propose that ... can greatly simplify" (Introduction — proposed, not proven), "we propose that the SoNS is useful for online LLM-based generation of code ... in at least the following two ways" (Introduction — open-ended list, not closed). No instances of "demonstrates" or "establishes" being applied to claims the evidence cannot bear. The "85% success rate" is reported as a number, not characterized as "high" or "reliable."
