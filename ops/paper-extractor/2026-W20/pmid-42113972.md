### The Big Question

Does participatory systems-modeling — specifically Forrester/Sterman-lineage SD GMB conducted with non-modeler community stakeholders — actually transfer systems-thinking competence to those stakeholders, or does it merely *use* their input to build a model that the modelers then own? This is a long-standing tension in the participatory-SD literature: proponents have asserted capacity-building as a co-benefit of GMB engagement for decades, but measured cognitive outcomes among lay participants are rare. The paper sits inside that debate as one of the more direct empirical tests of the capacity-transfer claim.

### Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss

- **System Dynamics (SD).** Forrester-lineage modeling tradition centered on stocks, flows, feedback loops, and delays; computational simulation of accumulation dynamics in social/biological/economic systems. (Sterman's *Business Dynamics* is the canonical textbook.)
- **Group Model Building (GMB).** A participatory variant of SD developed primarily in the Netherlands/US in the 1990s in which a facilitation team (modeler, facilitator, recorder, "gatekeeper") leads stakeholders through scripted exercises to co-produce a shared system model — typically a causal loop diagram (CLD) and sometimes a stock-and-flow diagram. (Andersen, Richardson, Vennix lineage.)
- **CBPR (community-based participatory research).** Research conducted *with* rather than *on* community members, with shared decision-making over research questions, methods, and use of findings.
- **Systems thinking.** The cognitive disposition / competence to perceive feedback, accumulation, delay, and emergence; typically operationalized via instruments like the Systems Thinking Scale (Moore et al.) or the systems-thinking inventory.
- **Lived experience** (in CBPR usage). First-person experience of the condition or community context the research addresses — distinct from professional or academic expertise.
- **Capacity building.** Strengthening of skills, knowledge, or organizational capability among community partners; a stated CBPR co-benefit.
