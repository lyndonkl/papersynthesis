### Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences)

The paper's load-bearing argument is that a single algebraic modification to bifurcation analysis — solving b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) = d<sub>n</sub>(x) instead of b<sub>n</sub>(x) = d<sub>n</sub>(x), Eq. 3 — recovers critical sizes that the standard analysis misses, and that this modification follows directly from the exact stationary distribution of any birth–death Markov chain (Eq. 2: π<sub>n</sub>(x) = π<sub>n</sub>(0)·∏<sub>y=1</sub><sup>x</sup> b<sub>n</sub>(y-1)/d<sub>n</sub>(y)). The evidence is four worked models — Föllmer–Kirman ant recruitment with critical numerosity n<sub>c</sub> ≈ r/s + 1 (Fig. 3 shows n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 50 for r=1, s=0.02); Beekman–Ratnieks–Sumpter pharaoh-ant trail formation with n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 2√(s/r) - q/r ≈ 50 for the simulation parameters (Fig. 4); Amé et al. cockroach shelter selection with n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 2√k (Fig. 5 in the cited literature, re-derived here); and Pacala–Gordon–Godfray task allocation with n<sub>c</sub> ≈ (s/min{f<sub>1</sub>,f<sub>2</sub>})<sup>1/α</sup>, the only case where standard bifurcation analysis fails outright (Fig. 5, n<sub>c</sub> = 50). The unifying claim — that all four cases share "competing feedbacks scaling differently with n" — is supported by inspecting each model's rate functions: in FK the spontaneous-switching feedback scales as O(1) while recruitment scales as O(n)/(n-1), so their balance shifts with n; analogous decompositions are given for BRS and the cockroach model. The methodological consequences are demonstrated empirically: Fig. 6 plots the absolute error |μ̂<sub>n</sub> - μ<sub>n</sub>| between ODE prediction and stochastic mean for the Pacala model and shows a sharp non-monotonic spike at n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 47, refuting any monotone "ODE gets better as n grows" intuition, and Fig. 7 shows two literature models of the same pavement-ant experiment predicting *opposite* transitions (parallel→alternate vs alternate→parallel) at numerosities around n ≈ 200–250.

### The Big Question

The Big Question is *emergence in finite systems*: when "more is different" (Anderson 1972 [2]), how much more, and why? The field has two incompatible intuitions — (a) collective behaviors depend critically on group size (Beekman 2001 [3], Chandra 2021 [4], Tunstrøm 2013 [5], Jhawar 2020 [6]) and (b) collective behaviors are robust to loss of individuals because they emerge from leaderless interactions (Ouellette & Gordon 2021 [20], also assumed by swarm-robotics practice [28–30]). The paper frames its contribution as the start of a "systematic study of how number influences collective behavior" — i.e., to convert the size-dependence/robustness tension into a tractable analytical question via the critical-numerosity construct.

### Specific hypotheses / design claims tested

The paper does not test empirical hypotheses; it defends a definition and a method. The design claims being defended:

1. **Definition adequacy:** 𝒩<sub>c</sub> = {n ≥ 2 : P(n) ≠ P(n-1)}, where P(n) counts stable equilibria of b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) - d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 on [1, n-1], captures the right notion of "critical numerosity" across the chosen model class. Defended by showing it agrees with simulation in FK, BRS, and cockroach examples, and by showing the parity artifact of the naive "π<sub>n-1</sub> vs π<sub>n</sub> have different numbers of extrema" definition is avoided (§2.1 footnote).
2. **Mechanism claim:** in all four worked cases, the critical numerosity arises from feedbacks that scale differently with n. Defended by algebraic decomposition of each rate function.
3. **Failure-mode claim:** standard bifurcation analysis applied to the ODE limit can miss critical numerosities entirely (demonstrated definitively for the Pacala task-allocation model, where the ODE has one equilibrium for every n while the modified analysis reveals n<sub>c</sub>).
4. **ODE-accuracy claim:** for stochastic models with a critical numerosity n<sub>c</sub>, the error of ODE approximation does not decrease monotonically in n; it can spike at n<sub>c</sub> (Fig. 6).
5. **Model-disambiguation claim:** competing models of the same phenomenon can be differentiated by their predicted critical-numerosity behavior (Fig. 7, pavement-ant foraging).

### Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss

- **Numerosity** — number of constituents in a collective; borrowed from Ladyman & Wiesner [8]; chosen to avoid conflation with size/scale/spatial extent. (Footnote 1, p. 2.)
- **Critical numerosity (n<sub>c</sub>)** — an integer n at which the number of stable equilibria of the modified equation b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) - d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 changes (Eq. 4, p. 7).
- **Birth–death Markov chain** — a continuous-time Markov chain on {0,…,n} where only transitions x→x+1 (rate b<sub>n</sub>(x)) and x→x-1 (rate d<sub>n</sub>(x)) are possible.
- **Discreteness-induced transition** — Togashi–Kaneko [11]; a behavioral bistability that appears only when state variables are integer-valued, vanishing in the continuous-density limit.
- **Noise-induced transition** — Horsthemke & Lefèvre [33]; a behavioral transition driven by stochastic fluctuations that has no counterpart in the deterministic ODE.
- **Föllmer–Kirman (FK) model** — 1993 model of ants choosing between two identical paths via spontaneous switching (rate s) plus pairwise recruitment (rate r); also: noisy voter model [46,47], Moran model with bidirectional mutation [48], Togashi–Kaneko chemical reaction [49]. Eq. 1.
- **P-bifurcation (phenomenological stochastic bifurcation)** — change in the number of modes of the stationary distribution as a parameter varies; Arnold & Boxler [37,38]. Cited as a near-but-imperfect cousin of the paper's definition.
- **Master equation** — ODE for the time evolution of a probability distribution over discrete states.
- **Fokker–Planck equation** — PDE diffusion approximation to a master equation; accuracy depends on n [17].
- **System-size expansion (van Kampen)** — perturbative expansion of a master equation in inverse system size; standard tool for connecting stochastic and ODE models [13].
- **Keizer's paradox** — observation that stochastic and deterministic models of a population with rare extinction predict different steady states even in the infinite-volume limit [65,66]. Used in §4.1 to justify the finite-n failure mode.
- **Complex-balanced chemical reaction networks** — CRN class for which a product-form stationary distribution exists (Anderson–Craciun–Kurtz [73]); cited as a possible higher-dimensional generalization route.

### Confusions / unresolved points

1. **Choice of stability criterion is implicit, not derived.** The paper defines P(n) as the number of *stable* equilibria of b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) - d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 on [1, n-1], but the stability condition (sign of d/dx [b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) - d<sub>n</sub>(x)]) is justified only by analogy to ODE stability. The connection to actual peaks of π<sub>n</sub>(x) is asserted at the start of §2.3 and used in Appendix A.1, but a clean theorem statement "extrema of π<sub>n</sub> are approximately the stable solutions of Eq. 3 with these errors" is absent. This is the most load-bearing assumption and it is *defended by example*, not proven.

2. **The "competing feedbacks scaling differently with n" claim is descriptive, not predictive.** In each worked example the paper exhibits the decomposition, but there is no general theorem saying "any rate pair (b<sub>n</sub>, d<sub>n</sub>) with this property has a critical numerosity at n<sub>c</sub> ≈ f(parameters)." It is a unifying *interpretation* of four cases, not a proven mechanism. The paper hedges this in the abstract ("commonly arise") — preserve that hedge in any downstream synthesis.

3. **The figure-6 error spike is shown for one parameter set.** The text says "for somewhat different values of the parameters, the critical numerosity appears in this range, and the error spikes above it" — but only one parameter combination is plotted. How sharp the spike is, and how reliably it appears, is left to the reader's imagination.

4. **Fig. 7 lacks experimental ground truth.** The paper is candid (footnote 2) that no experiment confirms which of the three pavement-ant models is right. The model-disambiguation claim is therefore *that critical numerosities could disambiguate models given an experiment*, not that they *have* disambiguated one. This is appropriate hedging; just note it.

5. **Higher-dimensional generalization is gestured at, not delivered.** §5 lists three candidate routes (exact distributions for complex-balanced CRNs; Fokker–Planck; van Kampen) with caveats on each. The schooling-fish example from Fig. 1 — which the introduction uses to motivate the whole question — is not actually analyzed by the paper's machinery. Pass 3 candidates: does the modified bifurcation analysis extend at all to vector-valued x? If yes, how?

6. **Threshold-N claim — operator focus item.** The paper does *not* give a single threshold N where emergence "kicks in" universally; that would contradict its argument. Instead it gives *system-specific* formulas for n<sub>c</sub>:
   - Föllmer–Kirman ant path selection: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ r/s + 1 (Fig. 3 uses r=1, s=0.02 → n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 51).
   - Beekman–Ratnieks–Sumpter pharaoh-ant trails: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 2√(s/r) - q/r (Fig. 4 simulation → n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 50).
   - Amé et al. cockroach shelter selection: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 2√k.
   - Pacala–Gordon–Godfray task allocation: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ (s/min{f<sub>1</sub>,f<sub>2</sub>})<sup>1/α</sup> (Fig. 5 → n<sub>c</sub> = 50; Fig. 6 → n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 47).
   - Pasteels et al. / Deneubourg et al. pavement-ant foraging: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 200–230 from Fig. 7 (numerical, not given by closed form).
   - Ice nucleation (introduction, Moberg et al. [7], not derived here): "roughly 90 or more" water molecules.
   - Pharaoh-ant pheromone trails (Beekman 2001 [3], not derived here): 700 ants forage vs ≤600 do not.
   - Fire-ant rafts (Ko et al. [68], introduction): "10 or more" stably form a raft.
   - The scaling regime: critical numerosities sit in the tens-to-low-hundreds range for the worked insect models, and the *formulas* show n<sub>c</sub> is set by ratios of competing rates (recruitment/switching, attractive/repulsive, on-trail/off-trail), with exponents of 1, 1/2, or 1/α depending on how the feedbacks scale.

Hedging preserved throughout: the paper consistently says "approximately," "roughly," "commonly arise," "could generalize," and never claims a universal threshold.
