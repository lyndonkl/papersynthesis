### Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences)
The paper argues that **what specifically distinguishes human social collective intelligence (CI) from other forms of CI (insect swarms, neural networks, markets, evolutionary ecological networks) is Theory of Mind — the capacity of agents to model the *hidden cognitive variables* (beliefs, preferences, constraints, in the Gintis "BPC" sense) of *other* agents and to act on those models to reshape the network of interactions**, and that AIs endowed with analogous ToM-like capacities can therefore enhance hybrid human–AI CI on the same mechanism rather than by accident of architecture. Supporting evidence is *not* new empirical data; it is (i) a literature synthesis of prior empirical results — chiefly Woolley et al.'s "c-factor" finding that group performance correlates with the average score on the Reading-the-Mind-in-the-Eyes ToM test and with social-sensitivity / contribution-evenness rather than with average or maximum individual IQ; Crandall et al.'s S# cooperation algorithm; the Meta-FAIR Diplomacy Cicero system; and Engel et al.'s online-vs-face-to-face replication that ToM predicts c equally well in either setting — and (ii) a constructive *theoretical* contribution: a 3-agent minimal model (Section 4) in which one agent (A₁) holding a second-order ToM of two prisoner's-dilemma-trapped agents (A₂, A₃) manipulates an information-carrying signal to flip their game from PD (Nash equilibrium = defect-defect) to Harmony (Nash equilibrium = cooperate-cooperate), and a Section 5 unified objective integrating RL, ToM-induced belief inference, and Cicero-style piKL anchoring as a candidate cognitive architecture. The argument is hedged appropriately for a position paper — the claims are framed as "we hypothesise," "we posit," "can," "suggests," with the strongest assertion reserved for the *conceptual* claim that ToM is "crucial in distinguishing human social collective intelligence from more general forms," and softer language ("can enhance," "could assist," "facilitated by") used for the AI-side claim.

### The Big Question
What is the *mechanism* by which collective intelligence arises specifically in social (psychologically mediated) collectives, as opposed to non-social collectives like ant colonies or neural networks — and given pervasive agential AI, what kind of cognitive capacity would an AI need to be an additive participant in human collective intelligence rather than a tool or a disruptor? The paper situates itself within a broader move (Watson and Levin's "collective intelligence of evolution and development"; Solé et al.'s liquid/solid brains; Friston et al.'s ecosystems-of-intelligence) toward treating CI as a unifying framework spanning biology, society, economics, and AI, and asks what the *psychological* layer adds to that picture.

### Specific hypotheses tested / design claims defended
This is a position/synthesis paper — there are no empirical hypotheses tested by the authors themselves. The defended claims are:
1. (H1, conceptual / load-bearing) Human social CI is improved by ToM, and ToM is **crucial** in distinguishing human social CI from more general (non-social) CI. Word "crucial" used in the abstract; argued in §§1.3, 2.3, 6.
2. (H2, predictive / hedged) AIs equipped with ToM-like capacity **will** enhance collective intelligence "in ways similar to human contributions" (abstract verb is "will"; body verb is "can"). Argued in §§1.2, 4, 5, 6.
3. (H3, framing) AI should be considered as "agential actors in a 'social ecology' rather than as mere technological tools" (abstract). Argued via the machine-behaviour [Rahwan et al., 36] and human–AI co-evolution [60] literatures.
4. (H4, mechanistic, constructive) ToM lets a single goal-directed agent **causally** reconfigure a network of interactions — via "niche choice, niche conformance, and niche construction" — to improve collective output, on *behavioural* (lifetime-of-an-agent) rather than evolutionary time scales. Argued by the Section 4 PD-to-Harmony model.
5. (H5, operational, constructive) A cognitive architecture combining RL (raw goal-seeking) + Bayesian belief inference over others' latent cognitive states θᵢ (ToM) + language as noisy channel + piKL dual regularisation (anchor-piKL toward human-like policies + ToM-piKL toward belief-updated policies) is a candidate unifying model for ToM-equipped AI. Argued in Section 5.

### Operational definition of ToM-in-AI (operator focus #1)
The paper offers a layered operational definition, narrowing from the general psychological construct down to a specific implementable model:

1. **General (Frith & Frith [51]):** ToM = explaining other people's behaviour on the basis of their internal cognitive states — knowledge, beliefs, desires.
2. **Restricted-for-this-paper (Gintis BPC [57, §2.2]):** ToM is restricted to "the subset of the beliefs, preferences, and constraints of other agents in the sense of incentivised decisions." Operationally: ToM = a model of the *hidden* (cognitive) variables of another agent that structure their observable behaviours. "Critically, people use and improve upon this information carrying interactions in their social networks ... they need to have ... a *social* world model of how agents are influenced by their interactions with other agents."
3. **Hierarchy (Lombard & Gärdenfors [90]):** Zeroth-order (reflexes / direct perception) — First-order (self-attribution of states) — Second-order (A attributes hidden cognitive states to B and uses them to explain B's behaviour; "lowest level at which A attributes hidden (cognitive) variables to B in order to explain the causes of behaviour") — Third-order (A attributes to B an understanding of A's internal states) — Fourth+ order (awareness of one's own and another's mental states; recursive). The paper's minimal model puts A₁ at second-order ToM and A₂, A₃ at first-order.
4. **Algorithmic instantiation candidates the paper endorses:**
   - **Inverse Reinforcement Learning (Jara-Ettinger [83]; Ruiz-Serra & Harré [84]):** "agents modelling other agents' mental states is equivalent to inferring an unobserved world model the other agent uses in its decision-making, as well as their reward function." Acknowledged limitation: IRL cannot disentangle beliefs from desires under the all-agents-identical assumption.
   - **Bayesian belief inference (the paper's Section 5 formalism):** Each agent i has a latent cognitive state θᵢ ∈ Θ encoding beliefs/goals/preferences; agent i emits a message m via a noisy channel P(m | θᵢ); agent j updates b_j(θᵢ | m) = [P(m|θᵢ)·P(θᵢ)] / Σ_θᵢ' P(m|θᵢ')·P(θᵢ'); j then takes a ToM-enhanced policy π_j^ToM(a | s, m) = E_{θᵢ ~ b_j(θᵢ|m)} [π_j(a | s, θᵢ)].
   - **Game-theoretic ToM (Yoshida, Dolan & Friston [56]):** ToM as inference over the strategic decision rules of other players.
   - **Cicero-style piKL (Meta-FAIR [44]):** Two readings — anchor-piKL (D = KL[π_i ‖ π_anchor]) regularises toward human-like policy, and ToM-piKL (D = KL[π^RL ‖ π^ToM]) regularises toward belief-updated policy. The paper's Section 5.6 proposes a unified objective L_total = E[R(s,a)] − λ₁·KL[π_i‖π_anchor] − λ₂·KL[π^RL‖π^ToM].

The operational definition is therefore: **ToM in AI = a Bayesian inference module over other agents' latent BPC variables, coupled to a policy that takes expectations over those inferred states and (optionally) regularised toward human-like behaviour via piKL.** This is constructive enough to be implementable in principle but is not implemented or evaluated in the paper.

### Collective-intelligence metric (operator focus #2)
The paper proposes and *applies in one toy example* a specific information-theoretic measure of CI. From §1.3 and §3.1:

- **Definition (Eq. 1):** φ(X; τ) ≜ I(X_t; X_{t−τ}) − Σᵢ I(X^i_t; X^i_{t−τ}), where X is the joint stochastic variable over all agents, I is time-delayed mutual information (TDMI) with delay τ. The first term is the predictive information the *whole* system carries about its own past (Whole); the second is the sum of each agent's predictive information about its own past (Σ Parts). The difference is the **"excess TDMI"** — the extent to which "the whole computational process is greater than the sum of the independent parts," interpretable as the inter-agent information flow that single-agent computation cannot account for.
- **Stated relation to IIT:** "loosely based on the information theory form of Integrated Information Theory (IIT) [29], where we are neutral to the interpretation of IIT as a measure of consciousness, and will not be carrying out any optimisation over binary partitions of state variables." Acknowledged as "not the same as IIT."
- **Worked Section 3.1 result (monkey-vs-computer matching-pennies; Lee et al. [110]; computed with JIDT [112]):** Joint TDMI, Monkey TDMI, Computer TDMI, and Excess TDMI tabulated for three algorithms (0,1,2) at τ ∈ {1,2,3} (Table 1). Excess TDMI is highest under Algorithm 0 (naïve uniform random) at φ ≈ 0.1047 bits (τ=3) and lowest under Algorithm 2 (most sophisticated) — at τ=3 the monkey TDMI (0.0030) is nearly twice the τ=1 value (0.0005), interpreted as the monkey "decoupling itself" from the computer as the computer's strategy becomes more sophisticated.
- **Worked Section 4.2 result (3-agent PD→Harmony minimal model):** CI of the first scenario (no ToM, no signal) = 0 bits; CI of the second scenario (A₁ uses second-order ToM to flip the game) = 1 bit "when s^t ∈ {−1, +1} is uniformly distributed."
- **Anchor concept the metric is operationalising:** Woolley et al.'s "c-factor" of group intelligence [54] — the paper treats its φ measure as an information-theoretic *proxy* for c, motivated by Bettencourt's information-aggregation framework [111] and consistent with Moore et al.'s information-theoretic CI in biology [27]. Quote: "To illustrate how we will use information theory as a proxy for Woolley et al.'s c intelligence, we use a dataset that was previously studied [109]..." (§3.1).

So the CI metric the paper argues ToM enhances is **excess time-delayed mutual information φ(X; τ) — operationally an information-theoretic proxy for Woolley's c-factor of group intelligence, defined as Whole-system predictive information minus the sum of single-agent predictive information**. The paper's claim is *not* that ToM trivially maximises φ; it is that ToM allows a single goal-directed agent to *causally* change φ at the lifetime time scale (rather than evolutionary time scale), with the toy model showing a jump from 0 bits to 1 bit when ToM is introduced.

### Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss
- **Theory of Mind (ToM)** — capacity to attribute hidden mental states (beliefs, preferences, intentions, knowledge) to other agents and use those attributions to explain or predict their behaviour. The paper restricts ToM to the Gintis "BPC" subset (beliefs / preferences / constraints) for tractability.
- **BPC model (Beliefs, Preferences, Constraints) [57]** — Herbert Gintis's framework treating socio-cognitive decision-making as parametric variation within structural constraints; the load-bearing latent variables in the paper's ToM definition.
- **Collective intelligence (c-factor) [54]** — Woolley et al.'s group-level analogue of individual g; predicted by ToM-test performance and contribution-evenness, not by mean/max individual IQ.
- **Liquid brain / solid brain [7]** — Solé et al.'s classification: solid = spatially fixed agents with persistent connections (neurons, spin glasses); liquid = mobile agents with short-lived interactions (ants, immune cells); human social networks are "liquid brains made of solid brains."
- **Cognitive morphospace [70, 7]** — geometric space parameterised by traits (e.g., autonomic, computational, social complexity; solid–liquid dimension; degree of ToM) used to compare types of CI as in a body-shape morphospace.
- **Niche construction / niche choice / niche conformance [120, 121]** — three modes by which an organism integrates into an environment: construction (modifying the environment), choice (selecting a fitting environment), conformance (adjusting phenotype to the environment). The paper extends these to *social* niche construction [146] — agents modifying the social context to influence their own social evolution.
- **piKL / Cicero [44]** — Meta-FAIR's algorithm in Diplomacy that uses a KL-divergence regulariser between the agent's policy and a human-anchor policy to keep behaviour human-interpretable while still optimising payoff.
- **Time-delayed mutual information (TDMI) / excess TDMI φ [Eq. 1, 2]** — TDMI: I(X_t; X_{t−τ}); excess TDMI: whole-system TDMI minus the sum of single-agent TDMI; the paper's CI metric.
- **Integrated Information Theory (IIT) [29]** — Tononi's measure of integration in a system; the paper's φ is acknowledged as "loosely based on" the IIT information-theoretic form but is not IIT proper (no binary-partition optimisation, no claim about consciousness).
- **Game Theory of Mind [56]** — Yoshida/Dolan/Friston's framework: ToM modelled as recursive Bayesian inference over strategic decision rules.
- **Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) [83]** — recovering an agent's reward function from its behaviour; proposed as one algorithmic instantiation of ToM.
- **Trait-Mediated Indirect Interaction (TMII) [115]** — hypergraph-style ecological interactions where one species' trait alters the interaction between two others (e.g., uninfested plant emitting volatiles to attract predators away from infested neighbour); supports the paper's hyper-graph formulation.
- **Reading the Mind in the Eyes test [107, 108]** — Baron-Cohen et al.'s ToM diagnostic from photographs of eye regions; the load-bearing measurement instrument behind Woolley's c-factor result.

1. **"Will" vs "can" — title-register inconsistency between v1 and v2.** The arXiv listing still records the v1 title "Will Enhance"; the v2 PDF reads "Can Enhance." The abstract preserves the strong form ("we hypothesise that AIs equipped with a theory of mind will enhance collective intelligence in ways similar to human contributions"), and the body almost everywhere uses the softer "can." This is the cleanest example in the paper of an asymmetry between the rhetorical register of the abstract and the hedged register of the body. The clarity audit flags this: a reader pulling only the abstract will overestimate the paper's commitment.

2. **The c-factor ↔ φ identification is asserted, not validated.** Section 3 introduces φ as a "proxy for Woolley et al.'s c intelligence," but no correlation, regression, or calibration between c and φ is shown. The two are theoretically related (both target Whole > Σ Parts) but the identification is a methodological choice, not an established equivalence. This matters because the *evidence* the paper draws on (Woolley's empirical c-factor finding) and the *metric* it computes (φ) are connected only by analogy.

3. **The §4 model demonstrates that A₁'s second-order ToM *can* lift φ from 0 to 1 bit, but does not establish that ToM is *necessary*.** A simpler mechanism — say, A₁ being a deterministic relay of s^t without any cognitive state at all — could in principle yield the same lift. The paper treats the "A₁ understands the BPC of A₂, A₃" framing as ToM, but the model machinery is equally consistent with "A₁ has a hard-coded if-then policy." The clarity audit flags a missing premise: the necessity of cognitive (vs. mechanical) signal-relaying is not derived.

4. **IRL/Bayesian-ToM is presented as the algorithmic instantiation, but its known limitations are noted in passing and not resolved.** Jara-Ettinger's "difficulty of recovering an agent's beliefs and desires even while assuming that all agents are identical in their choice-making" is acknowledged ([83] cite, p. 6), but the §5 unified objective assumes the belief-inference step works as written. Whether the unified objective survives the IRL identification problem is not discussed.

5. **The cognitive-status hierarchy (§2.2) is invoked but not used to gate the claims about AI.** The paper enumerates zeroth- through fourth-order ToM and notes that "there is no sharp line that unambiguously distinguishes between stimulus-response, conscious, and self-conscious agents." It then proceeds to talk about AIs needing ToM without committing to a level. Cicero is described as having "an elementary form of modelling the cognitive state of other players" without an explicit order assignment. This is a deliberate choice (the paper hedges on whether AIs *have* ToM vs. *behave as if they do*) but it leaves the operational threshold for "AI ToM" undefined.

6. **The §5 unified piKL objective is presented in closed form but with no example evaluation.** It is unclear whether the two regularisation terms (anchor-piKL toward human policy + ToM-piKL toward belief-updated policy) are jointly satisfiable in practice, or whether they trade off. λ₁ and λ₂ are introduced as "control the influence of human-alignment and social-inference regularisation, respectively" but no setting is recommended.

7. **The §3.1 monkey-vs-computer worked example is illustrative of the *measure* φ but does not contain ToM.** The paper says so explicitly ("we use a dataset that was previously studied [109] to measure the information flow in an iterated economic game experiment between monkeys and computers"), but the example sits in a section nominally about ToM-mediated CI. A reader could conflate "φ measures CI" with "φ measures ToM-enhanced CI" — they don't, the §3.1 example contains neither agent doing second-order ToM.

8. **"AI Theory of Mind" is used in two registers without explicit demarcation.** Sometimes as "AI that has ToM" (e.g., Cicero, §1.2), sometimes as "ToM-style cognitive tool an AI uses to model humans" (§5, §6). The two are different — an AI that models human ToM is not the same as an AI that itself has ToM in the philosophical sense — and the paper does not always signal which register it is in. This is the "Pass 1 flag" issue from the prior pass: position papers commonly conflate ToM-like behavioural outputs with ToM possession, and this paper inherits that ambiguity by design rather than by accident.
