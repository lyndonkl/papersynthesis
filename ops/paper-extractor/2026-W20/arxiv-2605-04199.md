## Pass 2 — Content grasp

### Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences)

The review argues that when a system is genuinely multiplex — i.e., the same set of units interacts simultaneously through M qualitatively distinct relation types, each one a layer — its collective dynamics cannot in general be recovered from any single layer in isolation or from the aggregated weighted graph that flattens the layers; the multiplex structure produces qualitatively new collective phenomena. The supporting evidence is a systematic walk through six dynamical-process families (percolation, diffusion / reaction-diffusion, synchronization, epidemic / social spreading, opinion dynamics, evolutionary games), in each of which a concrete mechanism, equation, and limit-case result demonstrate a "truly multiplex" phenomenon: discontinuous cascading failures across coupled layers (Buldyrev-type interdependent percolation with a hybrid first-order transition at ⟨p_c⟩⟨k⟩≃2.45541 for Poisson layers, vs continuous transitions in single-layer counterparts); the emergence of a "multiplex fast diffusion" regime above an inter-layer coupling threshold D* where diffusion runs faster than on any constituent layer; Turing-pattern formation in multiplexes even when neither layer alone could host the instability; relay / inter-layer / chimera synchronization patterns absent in single-layer systems; the disappearance or shift of epidemic thresholds when contagion couples with awareness in a parallel information layer; and structurally-induced cooperation enhancement in multiplex prisoner's dilemma games. The review consolidates this evidence under a three-mechanism taxonomy (structural inter-layer correlations; dynamical interplay between inter- and intra-layer interactions; dynamically correlated heterogeneous processes across layers) stated explicitly in the Conclusions (Section XII). The argument is calibrated: the authors say the framework "identifies" and "presents" mechanisms rather than "proves" them universal — preserve that hedging.

### The Big Question

How does the *architecture* of interaction in a complex system of many similar units (humans, neurons, species, agents, infrastructures) — specifically the existence of *multiple qualitatively distinct types of interaction running over the same set of units* — determine the collective behaviors that emerge? More sharply: which collective phenomena are genuinely multiplex (cannot be obtained from any aggregation or any single layer in isolation), and what structural or dynamical signatures of the layered architecture are responsible for them? This sits in the network-science tradition of "structure determines behavior" but pushes past the single-layer formulation that dominated the field's first two decades.

### Specific hypotheses / design claims defended

The review is not testing a single hypothesis; it organizes the literature around the defense of four claims:

1. *Existence claim.* There exist collective phenomena that emerge on multiplex networks and do *not* emerge on (a) any layer of the multiplex considered in isolation, or (b) the weighted aggregated single-layer graph obtained by collapsing the layers. (Examples given throughout: multiplex fast diffusion, Buldyrev cascading collapse, multiplex Turing patterns, relay synchronization, multiplex topological enslavement in games.)
2. *Mechanism-1 claim — structural inter-layer correlations.* Patterns of correlation between layers (edge overlap; inter-layer degree correlations; community alignment across layers) systematically alter dynamical outcomes — e.g., link overlap enables catastrophic cascading percolation [ref 41]; degree assortativity / disassortativity between layers controls cooperation in games [ref 516]; community-structure similarity across layers smooths the percolation transition [ref 208].
3. *Mechanism-2 claim — dynamical interplay between inter- and intra-layer interactions.* Tuning the *relative weight* of inter-layer vs intra-layer couplings (a single parameter, often called D, w, or η depending on context) produces sharp transitions — superdiffusion above D* [ref 252]; new explosive synchronization transitions in duplex Kuramoto systems [refs 352-353]; layer-based localization in multimodal epidemic spreading [ref 387].
4. *Mechanism-3 claim — dynamically correlated heterogeneous processes.* When two *different* dynamics run simultaneously on two layers and couple (Kuramoto on one layer + biased random walk on another [ref 551]; epidemic + awareness UAU-SIS [ref 419]; voter + disease [ref 426]), the coupled system can exhibit phenomena (explosive transitions, hysteresis, multistability, cyclic cascade collapse) that neither dynamics shows in isolation.

### Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss for a non-specialist

- *Multiplex network* — a network with N nodes and M layers, where each layer is its own adjacency matrix A^[α] over the same node set, and nodes are matched one-to-one across layers ("replica nodes"). Inter-layer links connect a node to its own copy in another layer.
- *Aggregated network* — the single-layer projection obtained by summing or OR-ing the layers; the natural null comparator for any "truly multiplex" claim.
- *Edge overlap o* — fraction of layers on which a given pair (i,j) is connected; o=1/M means each edge appears in exactly one layer, o=1 means all edges in all layers.
- *Edge-colored graph* — special case of multiplex where each layer is just a different "color" of edge over the same physical nodes (replica nodes are the same entity); the natural model for social-agents-playing-different-games.
- *Mutually connected giant component (MCGC)* — the multiplex analogue of the giant component: a node is in the MCGC iff it has a path to the MCGC in *every* layer it is coupled to. Defined self-consistently; "replaces the notion of connectedness in inter-dependent multiplex systems."
- *Supra-Laplacian L* — block-matrix Laplacian over MN nodes (N nodes × M layers) governing diffusion across the multiplex; its second-smallest eigenvalue λ₂ controls the relaxation timescale.
- *Master Stability Function (MSF)* — single-layer technique extended to multiplex: separates dynamics from topology in linear stability analysis of the synchronous manifold, lets you compute whether a coupling configuration synchronizes without knowing the full Jacobian.
- *Reducibility* — the question of whether a multiplex description can be compressed by merging similar layers without losing essential dynamical information; computed via quantum-Jensen-Shannon divergence on layer Laplacians.
- *Topological enslavement* — emergent regime in games on heterogeneous multiplexes where, as M and inter-layer degree correlation grow, game outcomes are determined by network topology (hub aggregation across layers) rather than by game-parameter values [ref 516].
- *Explosive (first-order, discontinuous) transition* — abrupt jump in a macroscopic order parameter as a control parameter crosses a critical value, often with hysteresis; contrast continuous (second-order) transition. Multiplexity *induces* explosive transitions in several settings where single-layer counterparts are second-order.

### Figure-by-figure analysis (load-bearing figures only)

- **Fig. 1** (p.4) — Three real-world multiplex examples: London transportation (rail/metro/bus), ecological food-web + parasite layer, *C. elegans* connectome with four communication modalities. Anchors the empirical motivation: these are not toy systems.
- **Fig. 4** (p.7) — The four dynamical-process families illustrated as multiplex sketches: voter model, epidemic spreading, intertwined synchronization+random-walk, coevolution with rewiring. This is the visual table-of-contents for the dynamics half of the review.
- **Fig. 7** (p.17) — *Quantitative result for percolation.* Phase diagram for the mutually connected giant component σ̄⟨k⟩ on a two-layer Poisson multiplex: discontinuous transition at p_c⟨k⟩ ≃ 2.45541, with σ̄⟨k⟩ jumping from 0 to ~1.25643 at criticality. Compare single-layer Poisson percolation: continuous, threshold at ⟨k⟩=1. The doubling of the threshold and the discontinuity are the canonical "Buldyrev result" — the founding numerical signature of genuinely multiplex emergent behavior.
- **Fig. 8** (p.19) — Percolation on real multiplexes: (a) *C. elegans* connectome smoothly percolates (no sharp transition); (b) targeted-attack robustness Ω of real multiplexes correlates with cross-layer community NMI (r=0.68) — *empirical* confirmation that structural inter-layer correlations mitigate vulnerability; (c) US-airlines two-carrier multiplex with optimal-percolation attack nodes highlighted. Multiple solutions exist — the failure mode is not uniquely localized.
- **Fig. 9** (p.23) — *Quantitative result for diffusion.* λ₂ of the supra-Laplacian as a function of inter-layer diffusion D: monotonic increase with two regimes separated by D*. For D≫D*, λ₂ exceeds that of either single layer (=multiplex super-diffusion / "multiplex fast diffusion"). Heterogeneity in the per-node interlayer constants D_i drastically reduces λ₂ near D*; removing even a small fraction of interlayer edges drops λ₂ sharply.
- **Fig. 10** (p.26) — Turing-pattern amplitude ⟨A⟩ vs ⟨k²⟩ of inhibitor layer in a scale-free multiplex with r=D²/D¹=1 (instability impossible in single-layer reductions): pattern onset above ⟨k²⟩_c, demonstrating multiplex-induced Turing instability.
- **Fig. 11** (p.27) — Multiplex congestion / "slower is faster" paradox: average travel time T can *decrease* with increasing inter-layer speed-ratio γ when storage buffers B are bounded. Failure-mode signal: increasing B from 40 to 10³ *worsens* performance for some γ — a multiplex Braess paradox.
- **Fig. 13** (p.33) — *Synchronization regime diagram for a duplex of 500 Rössler oscillators in (g,w) plane:* three regions (A: only inter-layer; B: complete; C: only intra-layer). Inter-layer synchronization can be achieved with intra-layer coupling *below* the isolated-layer threshold — multiplex coupling can substitute for intra-layer coupling.
- **Fig. 16** (p.37) — Explosive synchronization induced by inhibitory layer: a duplex of excitatory + inhibitory phase oscillators where neither layer in isolation supports a first-order transition; inter-layer coupling w>0 flips the excitatory layer from second-order to first-order with hysteresis.
- **Fig. 17** (p.38) — Edge-colored Rössler MSF diagram: six regions in (g^[1], g^[2]) plane, including regions V/VI where *two unstable layers stabilize each other* into synchrony. Hard counterexample to "multiplex = aggregate" intuition.
- **Fig. 19** (p.42) — Multimodal contagion phase diagram: fraction infected ρ vs λ/μ for various inter-layer contagion ratios η. Two regimes — for η≪1, dynamics is dominated by the layer with larger leading eigenvalue (decoupled behavior); as η grows, secondary susceptibility peaks decay and a single epidemic onset emerges.
- **Fig. 21** (p.46) — UAU-SIS coupled awareness + disease model: epidemic threshold λ_c^[1] depends on awareness λ^[2] only above a *metacritical point* — there is a regime where contagion and awareness layers are dynamically decoupled at threshold even though they couple in the bulk.
- **Fig. 25** (p.54) — *Quantitative result for games.* In a multiplex weak prisoner's dilemma with M layers and ⟨k⟩=3, fraction of cooperators x_c remains substantial up to extremely large temptation T as M grows — opposite of the aggregated-graph behavior (which approaches well-mixed defection). The cooperator-fraction distribution P(x_c) across layers is heterogeneous: layers diverge.
- **Fig. 27** (p.55) — *Topological enslavement.* As M and inter-layer degree correlation rise, cooperation in heterogeneous-multiplex games becomes determined by topology, not by game payoffs — failure mode for "design the payoff to control behavior" strategies in layered systems.
- **Fig. 29** (p.57) — Public-goods game on multiplex: critical synergy R_c drops from 3.75 (isolated) to 3.25 (M=2 with maximal edge overlap). Without structural correlations (no overlap), no enhancement. Edge overlap is *required* for multiplex reciprocity.
- **Fig. 31** (p.59) — Intertwined Kuramoto (sync) + biased-random-walk (transport) dynamics on a two-layer multiplex: mutual coupling produces explosive sync on layer 1 *and* explosive heterogenization of walker distribution on layer 2 — bistability appears (white region in panel c). Coupled dynamics produces phenomena absent in either dynamics alone.
- **Fig. 32** (p.61) — Coevolving multiplex voter model: phase diagram in (p^[1], p^[2], q) parameter space shows consensus, active, *shattered*, *asymmetric*, and *dynamically active shattered* phases. The shattered phase emerges only when layers have asymmetric plasticity — a multiplex-specific failure mode of consensus dynamics.

### Confusions / unresolved points

1. *The "truly multiplex" criterion is partly definitional.* The review repeatedly contrasts multiplex phenomena against (a) any single layer in isolation and (b) the aggregated graph. But "aggregated" admits many constructions (binary OR, weighted sum, overlay, quotient — all listed on p.8). A phenomenon could survive a binary-OR aggregate but vanish under a weighted-sum aggregate. The review acknowledges this implicitly (multiple aggregate definitions on p.8) but the "cannot emerge on the aggregated network" claim in the Conclusions is not always pinned to a specific aggregate construction. Mild internal-consistency concern.
2. *Hedging is preserved at the abstract level but slightly stronger in the figure captions.* The Conclusion identifies the three mechanisms with the verb "can be identified" — calibrated. But individual figure discussions sometimes use "demonstrates" / "shows" for results that rest on a single simulation study with synthetic random layers (e.g., Fig. 25's M-dependence of cooperator fraction). The audit tagged this as mild overclaiming localized to figures, not the abstract.
3. *Reducibility (Sec. III.E) and the rest of the review pull in opposite directions.* Reducibility methods show many real-world multiplexes can be compressed without dynamical loss; the review's main thesis is that multiplex structure is essential. The tension is acknowledged but not resolved — when is a system "truly multiplex" enough that the review's mechanisms apply, vs reducible to a smaller representation? Practical question for downstream application.
4. *Failure-mode coverage is uneven.* Section IV (percolation) is rich in failure-mode results (Braess-like Fig. 11; multiple optimal-percolation solutions Fig. 8c). Section VIII (social dynamics) is rich in *consensus*-style results but sparse on hostile / adversarial dynamics — multiplex *poisoning*, *manipulation by a single adversarial layer*, etc., not covered. May limit utility for downstream multi-agent-orchestration synthesis.
5. *The boundary between Sec. X (intertwining dynamics) and Sec. XI (coevolution) is fuzzy.* Both involve mutual feedback across layers. The distinction the authors draw — Sec. X has fixed topology, Sec. XI has co-evolving topology — is logically clean but examples bleed across.
6. *Higher-order networks / hypergraphs (group interactions beyond dyadic) are flagged in the Conclusions as the missing generalization* — the review explicitly does not cover them and points to refs 541-542, 574-578 as future work. This is the load-bearing limit case of the entire framework: all results assume interactions are dyadic. Real multi-agent systems with group decisions (committees, quorums, voting blocs) fall outside the review's scope.
