### Pass 2 question set

**1. Main argument and supporting evidence (3–5 sentences).**
The paper argues that the unlabeled Erdős–Rényi canonical ensemble — the maximum-entropy distribution over *isomorphism classes* of graphs at fixed expected edge count — is qualitatively different from the labeled ER ensemble in the sparse regime, because the unlabeled microcanonical entropy `S_{nm} = log g_{nm}` is non-concave (Eq. 7: `2 S_{nm} < S_{n,m-1} + S_{n,m+1}` for `m ≤ log n`), confirmed both by Pólya-counting asymptotics from Wright (1971/1974) and by direct numerics up to `n=50` (Fig. 1). Non-concavity is the standard sufficient condition for ensemble inequivalence (Touchette 2015) and drives a first-order transition: the canonical Gibbs measure `p_{nm} ∝ g_{nm} q^m` always has a local maximum at `m=0` (because `g_{n0}=g_{n1}=1`, so `p_{n0} ≥ p_{n1}` for any `q<1`), and acquires a competing maximum at `m_* ≈ log(n!) ≈ n log n` in the dense regime; at the critical fugacity `q_c ≈ 2 log(n!)/n²` (equivalently `m_* = 2 m_c = 2 log(n!)`) the two maxima are equiprobable so the partition function is `Z_n ≈ 1 + e^{m_*}/n!`. Below `k_c` the canonical ensemble is therefore a statistical mixture: probability `P ≈ 1 − ⟨k⟩/(2 k_c) → 1` of an empty graph, and probability `1−P` of a graph with average degree `≈ 2 k_c` (Eq. 19), so any target intermediate degree `0 < ⟨k⟩ ≪ k_c` is *not realizable* in the thermodynamic limit. The same machinery applied to the rescaled variables `Q = q/q_c` and `M = ⟨m⟩/(2 m_c)` produces a step function `M(Q) → Q · 𝟙[Q>1]` (Eq. 24), the canonical signature of a first-order transition, and as a corollary the percolation transition at `k_p = 1` familiar from labeled ER graphs is *absent* in the unlabeled ensemble — bounded-degree unlabeled graphs simply do not exist as a thermodynamic phase. The `scientific-clarity-checker` audit flags one important place where hedging is *appropriate*: the authors explicitly write "we discover" and "is thus absent" for the unlabeled ensemble result (a thermodynamic-limit claim that they have derived analytically and confirmed numerically), but qualify their methodological prescription with "cannot be ignored" rather than "must always use unlabeled" — i.e., the choice depends on whether vertex identity carries empirical meaning, which is restated as an open application question rather than as a settled rule.

**2. The Big Question.**
Are the well-studied statistical-mechanics results for *labeled* random graphs — degree distributions, connectivity thresholds, percolation transitions, configuration-model nulls — the right tools to use as null models for *real-world* networks (molecules, atoms in material networks, biological tangles) whose nodes are physically indistinguishable, so that only the unlabeled isomorphism class is empirically meaningful? More broadly: when an ensemble is built from an indistinguishability symmetry (Gibbs-paradox-style), does it inherit the thermodynamic phases of the labeled construction, or does the symmetry quotient produce qualitatively new behavior?

**3. Specific hypotheses / load-bearing claims.**
- H1 (entropy non-concavity): For unlabeled ER on `n` vertices with `m` edges and `k = 2m/n`, `S_{nm} = log g_{nm}` is non-concave in `m` throughout `k ≤ log n`, with an inflection point at `k_c ≈ log n`; concave for `k > log n`. Quantitative form: Eq. (6) `g_{n,m+1}/g_{nm} ≈ μ_m / log μ_m` where `μ_m log μ_m = 2m` (Lambert-W), making the ratio monotonically *increasing* in `m`, hence Eq. (7) violates concavity.
- H2 (critical fugacity): The critical inverse temperature satisfies `q_c = e^{−β_c} ≈ 2 log(n!)/n²`, equivalently the critical `m_*` is `2 m_c = 2 log(n!) ≈ 2 n log n`.
- H3 (broken equivalence): For `q < q_c` (equivalently `⟨k⟩ < k_c`), the canonical distribution `p_{nm} ∝ g_{nm} q^m` is bimodal with peaks at `m=0` and `m ~ m_*`, so the canonical ensemble cannot reproduce a microcanonical ensemble fixed at any `⟨k⟩ ≪ k_c`. The relative entropy per node `s = D_KL/n → ∞`, the large-deviation criterion for inequivalence.
- H4 (thermodynamic limit / first-order): In rescaled variables `Q=q/q_c`, `M=⟨m⟩/(2 m_c)`, the limit `n→∞` gives `M(Q) → Q` for `Q>1` and `M(Q) → 0` for `Q<1` (Eq. 24) — a discontinuity of height 1 at the critical point, the defining signature of a first-order transition.
- H5 (percolation absence): The labeled-graph percolation transition at average degree `k_p=1` lies inside `[0, k_c]`, the forbidden region; therefore percolation has no analogue in the unlabeled canonical ensemble.
- H6 (symmetry connection): The transition point `k_c = log n` coincides exactly with Erdős–Rényi's asymmetry threshold for labeled graphs (Erdős–Rényi 1963; Cameron 2013) — graphs become asymmetric for `k ≫ log n` and possess nontrivial automorphisms for `k ≪ log n`. Mechanistic story: tilting from labeled to unlabeled distributions multiplies probabilities by `1/|Aut(G)|`, redistributing mass toward symmetric graphs; for `k ≪ k_c` the maximally symmetric graphs are the empty graphs (and graphs dominated by 0-degree "loners"), so the mass concentrates there.

**4. Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss for a non-specialist reader.**
- *Labeled vs unlabeled graphs:* A labeled graph distinguishes vertex identity (vertex 1 ≠ vertex 2); an unlabeled graph is the isomorphism class — only the structural pattern of connections matters. `n!` is the maximum number of labeled graphs corresponding to a single unlabeled graph (less for symmetric graphs).
- *Microcanonical ensemble:* Uniform distribution over labeled (or unlabeled) graphs with *exactly* `m` edges. *Canonical ensemble:* Maximum-entropy distribution over graphs with *expected* `m` edges; in labeled case each edge is independent Bernoulli(`p`).
- *Ensemble equivalence / inequivalence:* In standard thermodynamics, the canonical and microcanonical ensembles give the same thermodynamic predictions in `n→∞`. Inequivalence — known from long-range / gravitational / mean-field systems — happens when microcanonical entropy is non-concave; the canonical ensemble can no longer reproduce all microcanonical states.
- *Microcanonical entropy `S_{nm} = log g_{nm}`:* The (log) count of distinct graphs on `n` vertices with `m` edges; `g_{nm} = C(N, m)` with `N=n(n−1)/2` in the labeled case, considerably more complex in the unlabeled case (Pólya counting / cycle index).
- *Pólya counting theory / cycle index:* The combinatorial machinery for enumerating objects under group action — here used to count labeled graphs up to vertex permutation. Yields the generating function Eq. (2).
- *Graph symmetry phase transition:* The threshold `k_c = log n` at which a random ER graph transitions from having a nontrivial automorphism group (`k ≪ log n`) to being asymmetric with high probability (`k ≫ log n`).
- *Percolation transition:* In labeled ER at `k_p = 2m/n = 1`, a giant connected component containing `Θ(n)` vertices emerges; for `k<1` only `O(log n)` components exist.
- *Fugacity `q = e^{−β}`:* The Boltzmann factor; the canonical model's single parameter, controlling expected `⟨m⟩`. In labeled ER, `q = p/(1−p)` so `q ∈ [0, ∞)` parameterizes `p ∈ [0, 1)`.
- *Lambert W function:* The function `W₀(x)` defined by `W₀(x) e^{W₀(x)} = x`; appears in Eq. (3) governing the asymptotic ratio `g_{n,m+1}/g_{nm}`.
- *Asymmetric (labeled) graph:* A labeled graph whose only automorphism is the identity; equivalently, every vertex permutation produces a *distinct* labeled graph in its isomorphism class.
- *Loners / 0-degree nodes:* Isolated vertices. They contribute hyperexponentially to graph symmetry (any permutation among loners is an automorphism), which is why the unlabeled measure concentrates them in the empty phase below `k_c`.

### Mechanism (what the operator asked to capture)
- **Order parameter:** `M = ⟨m⟩/(2 m_c)`, the rescaled expected edge count (equivalently `⟨k⟩/(2 k_c)`, the rescaled expected average degree). It is `0` in the empty phase and `Q = q/q_c` (specifically `M = Q` for `Q>1`) in the log-degree phase.
- **Control parameter:** `Q = q/q_c`, the rescaled fugacity, equivalently the rescaled target average degree.
- **Phase-transition mechanism:** First-order; driven by non-concavity of the microcanonical entropy `S_{nm} = log g_{nm}` in the sparse regime `k ≤ log n`. The canonical Gibbs measure `p_{nm} ∝ g_{nm} q^m` develops two competing local maxima (at `m=0` and at `m = m_* ~ n log n`) that swap dominance at the critical fugacity `q_c ≈ 2 log(n!)/n²`. At the critical point both maxima contribute equally, so the partition function is `Z_n ≈ 1 + e^{m_*}/n!` (Eq. 14). In the thermodynamic limit `n→∞`, the rescaled order parameter `M(Q)` becomes a step function jumping by 1 at `Q=1`.
- **Why exactly at `k_c = log n`:** This is *the same point* as the labeled graph symmetry transition (Erdős–Rényi 1963; Cameron 2013). Mechanistic story: weighting labeled graphs by `1/|Aut(G)|` to obtain the unlabeled measure transfers probability mass from asymmetric graphs (`k ≫ log n`, where `|Aut|=1`) toward symmetric graphs (`k ≪ log n`). For very sparse graphs the maximally symmetric configurations are empty / near-empty graphs (every permutation among the `n` 0-degree "loners" is an automorphism, contributing a factor `n!` to `|Aut|`), so the unlabeled ensemble concentrates mass on the empty graph below `k_c`. Above `k_c` graphs are asymmetric, the `1/|Aut|` reweighting is trivial, and labeled and unlabeled ensembles coincide.
- **Where ensemble inequivalence shows up:** Below `k_c`. Two diagnostic signatures: (i) the canonical ensemble with expected degree `0 < ⟨k⟩ < k_c` cannot reproduce the microcanonical ensemble at *any* fixed degree `⟨k⟩` in that range — it is a *mixture* `p_{n0} ≈ 1 − ⟨k⟩/(2 k_c)` of the empty graph plus `1 − P` of a graph with average degree `≈ 2 k_c`; the canonical distribution has no single dominant `m`-value; (ii) the relative entropy per node `s = D_KL/n` between canonical and microcanonical diverges as `n→∞`, the large-deviation diagnostic.
- **Which configurations exhibit qualitatively different dynamics:** Specifically, *sparse* unlabeled ER configurations with target bounded average degree `0 < ⟨k⟩ ≪ log n`. These configurations *do not exist* as a thermodynamic phase — the canonical ensemble has no probability mass concentrated at any such `⟨k⟩`. Concretely: if you try to use the unlabeled ER model as a null for a real-world network with bounded average degree (which describes most sparse empirical networks — molecules, web of contacts, biological tangles), the model's "typical" graph will be empty with probability approaching 1, not a graph of the target average degree. The celebrated percolation transition at `k_p=1` falls inside this forbidden zone, so it has no unlabeled analogue. The high-density phase `k > k_c = log n` is well-behaved: ensembles agree, the typical graph is asymmetric, the standard labeled-ER intuitions apply.
