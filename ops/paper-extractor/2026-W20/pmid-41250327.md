### 1. Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences) — *(abstract-only; full text unavailable)*

The paper argues that asymmetry between communicators' propensities to speak and to listen — rather than opinion content, network topology, or strategic incentives — is itself sufficient to generate the social structures observed in online networks: local dominance, hierarchy, and the integration-vs-fragmentation polarity. The supporting mechanism, as stated in the abstract, is a three-element feedback loop coupling **speaking activity ↔ attention allocation ↔ memory**: a talker who attracts listeners receives more attention, which (via memory) reinforces future attention to that talker, which sustains their speaking, which further captures attention. This loop's gain — set by the parameters listener engagement, openness, and memory — determines whether the network locks into hub-dominated fragmentation or remains integrated. The evidence adduced is simulation output from a minimal agent-based model; the abstract reports two qualitative results as the main findings: (i) self-referential communication generates local dominance and hierarchy, and (ii) increased listening propensity, openness, and memory enhance cohesion and reduce fragmentation, with a corollary that enforcing minimum listening across all members suppresses excessive hub dominance. *Caveat: without the methods section, the precise functional forms coupling speaking/attention/memory, the order parameter used to measure "fragmentation," and the simulation regime (network size, time horizon, initial conditions) cannot be verified, and the "supporting evidence" claim rests entirely on the authors' self-description in the abstract.*

### 2. The Big Question

How does emergent social hierarchy and the integration-vs-fragmentation polarity of online communication networks arise from the interaction primitives of individual communicators — specifically, how much explanatory work can be done by asymmetry in communicative propensity (talking vs. listening) alone, without invoking opinion content, ideological alignment, or strategic optimization? This sits inside the broader sociophysics program on opinion dynamics and emergent structure (voter models, bounded-confidence models, the Sneppen-school "reinforced communication" line going back to the group's 2005-2008 papers on self-organized communication topology). The paper's specific move is to swap *what is said* for *who speaks vs. who listens* as the explanatory primitive.

### 4. Unfamiliar terms / concepts requiring gloss

- **Talker propensity / listener propensity** — per-agent rates or probabilities governing how often an agent broadcasts vs. attends. Per-agent asymmetry between the two is the modeling primitive. (Definition implicit; inferred from abstract.)
- **Attention allocation** — how an agent distributes finite listening capacity across neighbors. Likely the rate-limited resource that creates competition among talkers.
- **Memory** — temporal persistence of past attention or interaction; the mechanism by which a current bias toward a talker carries forward and reinforces future attention. The loop's hysteresis-like element.
- **Self-referential communication** — communication in which a talker's own past speech (mediated through listeners' attention and memory of that talker) reinforces their own future speech. The local positive-feedback motif.
- **Local dominance** — emergent property in which a small subset of agents capture a disproportionate share of attention from their neighborhood, becoming local hubs.
- **Listener engagement** — listener-side parameter that modulates how strongly attention couples to talker activity. High engagement maintains cohesion per H3.
- **Openness** — likely a parameter governing willingness to attend to non-incumbent talkers / new contacts (analog of bounded-confidence threshold or rewiring rate). Its named role: increased openness reduces fragmentation.
- **Integration vs. fragmentation** — the macroscopic order parameter the model is studied around: integration = single connected attention-mediated community; fragmentation = disjoint hub-clusters or echo chambers. Phase-structure language ("sets the balance") suggests a critical-transition framing rather than a smooth gradient. *(Whether the transition is sharp or smooth is not stated in available material.)*
- **Hub dominance** — the extreme of the local-dominance phenomenon at the network level: one or a few agents capture most attention globally.
