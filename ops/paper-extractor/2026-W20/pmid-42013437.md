### Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences)

The paper argues that adoption barriers to modern ML-based clinical decision support (ML-CDS) — most concretely, early warning scores (EWSs) — are inherently *dynamic* feedback phenomena (perception → use → trust → use, with delays and nonlinearities) that static facilitator/barrier checklists cannot capture, and that *group model building* (GMB) from system dynamics is the appropriate participatory method to elicit that dynamic structure from the people who live it. As empirical anchoring, the authors cite their own prior Duke study (Bedoya 2019 / O'Brien 2020) showing that integration of NEWS into the EHR at three Duke sites "did not change rates of mortality or unanticipated intensive care unit transfer, even when the accuracy of the ML score indicated it was performing well," with frontline nurses ignoring the alert 86% of the time — i.e., a strong validity signal but no behavioural uptake. The argument's procedural claim is that three sequential GMB sessions (variable elicitation → behaviour-over-time graphing → causal loop diagramming), administered to developers + implementers + users of the NEWS and Duke EWS across DUH and DRH, will yield a causal loop diagram (CLD) that the team will then *translate* into a stock-and-flow simulation in Vensim — with stocks (e.g., patient counts, "adoption of CDS") and flows defined by mathematical equations capturing "delays, nonlinearities, and feedback effects" — and that the simulation is what will quantify "how feedback loops influence variables over time" and let the team run scenario comparisons by perturbing parameters. The clarity-checker audit flags that the paper's central quantitative claim — that the simulation will let the team "test the internal logic of the causal loop diagram and refine its underlying assumptions" — is forward-looking ("we are translating", "we will define", "we anticipate generating") and that no concrete loops, stocks, or equations are reported in this protocol; the strongest *evidence-in-paper* is the qualitative CLD scaffold described in Methods (developed pre-Session 3 and validated post-session via a charge-nurse interview and a group interview with two non-participating nursing leaders) and the four-timepoint Weiner Acceptability/Appropriateness/Feasibility questionnaire administered to GMB participants.

### The Big Question

How can implementation scientists move beyond static lists of facilitators and barriers — which dominate dissemination-and-implementation research — to capture the *temporal* and *feedback-driven* structure of why evidence-based ML-CDS tools persistently fail to be adopted by frontline clinicians even when their predictive accuracy is established, and can a quantified system-dynamics simulation, co-built with hospital staff, serve as a "learning health system" infrastructure that exposes *intervenable leverage points* (Meadows lineage, invoked via the title's "levers") for changing that adoption trajectory? Adjacent sub-question: can GMB itself — as a co-design ritual — improve perceived acceptability, appropriateness, and feasibility of CDS among the developers, implementers, and users who participate?

### Specific hypotheses tested (design claims being defended, since this is a protocol)

Because no inferential statistics are pre-registered, these are the design / mechanism claims the protocol is set up to defend:

1. **Dynamic-structure claim:** EWS adoption is governed by feedback loops with delays and nonlinearities (not a one-shot decision), and a CLD produced from three sequential GMB sessions will recover that structure.
2. **Participatory-elicitation claim:** Bringing developers (data scientists), implementers, and end-users (charge nurses, staff nurses) into the same modelling sessions surfaces *adoption dynamics* that single-stakeholder usability studies miss — specifically because single-stakeholder studies "do not consider applied function within the context of typical clinical care workflows" (Discussion).
3. **Translation claim:** A qualitative CLD can be operationalised as a stock-and-flow simulation in Vensim, with each stock-flow relationship "defined with mathematical equations" capturing delays, nonlinearities, and feedback, such that time-series outputs (e.g., "patient counts, adoption of CDS") can be generated and scenario-compared.
4. **Acceptability-of-method claim:** Participants' Weiner-instrument scores for Acceptability of Intervention, Intervention Appropriateness, and Feasibility of Intervention measured at four timepoints (pre-session-1 + post each of three sessions) will collectively "act as a proxy for assessing commitment to adopting a new tool such as the EWS."
5. **Generalisability claim (forward-looking, hedged):** "These findings will have broader applicability beyond just EWSs" — future work will incorporate "multiple clinical use cases to fully capture multilevel factors that determine real-world adoption and sustainability of AI-CDS." The hedge "will have" is preserved; no evidence of generalisation is given in the protocol itself.
6. **Natural-experiment claim (about the setting, not the method):** The DUH–DRH contrast — same Epic EHR since 2013, but DUH replaced NEWS with a homegrown Duke EWS in 2019 while DRH continued NEWS — creates a natural variation that "allows us to assess how different conditions affect EWS adoption." The five concrete differences enumerated: (i) input variables (7 for NEWS vs 40+ for Duke EWS, with NEWS interpretable and Duke EWS opaque); (ii) workflow integration (Duke EWS = continuous firing real-time feedback; NEWS = discrete shift-change-only); (iii) target users (NEWS → staff nurses; Duke EWS → charge nurses); (iv) information displayed (NEWS = overall score; Duke EWS = 3-level red/yellow/green colour-coded risk category); (v) interpretability (NEWS has no specific probabilistic interpretation; Duke EWS thresholds chosen based on desired sensitivity/PPV).

### Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss for a non-specialist reader

- **Group model building (GMB)** — A participatory system-dynamics method (Vennix 1996; Hovmand 2014) in which stakeholders collectively build a system-dynamics model of a problem they live with, structured as facilitated "scripts" from a community resource called Scriptapedia.
- **Causal loop diagram (CLD)** — A qualitative system-dynamics artefact: nodes are variables, signed arrows show whether an increase in one variable causes an increase (+) or decrease (−) in another; closed cycles of arrows are *feedback loops* (reinforcing or balancing).
- **Stock-and-flow model / simulation** — The quantitative complement to a CLD. *Stocks* are accumulating quantities (e.g., "patient counts", "adoption of CDS"); *flows* are the rates at which stocks change (in/out). Each stock-flow relationship is given a mathematical equation; the resulting set of differential/difference equations can be simulated to produce time-series of the stocks. The paper uses **Vensim** (Ventana Systems) for this.
- **Levers / leverage points** — Title language; in Meadows / Forrester usage, structural locations in a system where a small intervention produces large change (e.g., feedback-loop gain, delay length, information flow). The protocol uses "intervenable levers" in the Discussion.
- **Early warning score (EWS)** — A bedside risk score using vital signs (and in ML versions, more variables) to predict deterioration / decompensation, typically over a 12-48 h horizon.
- **NEWS** — National Early Warning Score; a 7-variable point-based EWS (Smith 2013).
- **Duke EWS** — A locally trained, ML-based, 40+-variable EWS deployed at DUH from 2019 (O'Brien 2020); presented as red/yellow/green risk categories rather than a raw score.
- **Alert fatigue** — The phenomenon where clinicians ignore (49%-96% of) CDS alerts because too many fire (Ancker 2017; Johnson 2017).
- **Variable elicitation / Behaviour-over-time / CLD initiating-and-elaborating** — The three named Scriptapedia GMB scripts the sessions are built on (refs 26, 27, 28).
- **Weiner instruments — Acceptability of Intervention Measure (AIM), Intervention Appropriateness Measure (IAM), Feasibility of Intervention Measure (FIM)** — Validated 4-item implementation-science scales (Weiner 2017) that "collectively can act as a proxy for assessing commitment to adopting a new tool."
- **CDS lifecycle / IRRID** — *IRRID* is JMIR's International Registered Report Identifier (the protocol's registration handle, here DERR1-10.2196/8084). The "lifecycle framework" the paper cites is Sittig 2023 (eight stages necessary for patient-centred CDS).
- **Snowball sampling** — Recruitment method used here: an initial contact refers further stakeholders, used until at least one developer and implementer per EWS and at least two users per EWS were enrolled.

### Specific stocks, flows, and feedback loops the paper identifies — captured per the spawn request

This is the spawn brief's focus, and the honest finding is that the protocol *names the modelling apparatus but does not yet specify any concrete loop, stock, flow, or equation*. Capturing exactly what the paper does and does not commit to:

**Stocks and flows — what is named:**

- The paper defines the formalism generically: *"stocks represent quantities that accumulate or decrease, such as patient counts, while flows indicate the rates at which these stocks change"* (Analysis / System Dynamics Modeling section).
- Only two example stocks are named in the text: **"patient counts"** and **"adoption of CDS"** (the latter introduced as *"key variables, such as patient counts or adoption of CDS, change over time"*).
- No specific flows are named. No equations are given. The paper commits only to *"define each stock and flow relationship with mathematical equations and capture delays, nonlinearities, and feedback effects using Vensim (Ventana Systems) simulation software's built-in functions."*
- The simulation will have "adjustable parameters to explore what would happen to system behavior if 1 or more values change" — i.e., parameter-sweep scenario analysis is the planned use, but the parameters themselves are not enumerated.

**Feedback loops — what is named:**

- No specific feedback loops are described by name, polarity (reinforcing/balancing), or membership. The text speaks of feedback in the abstract: *"complex processes are modeled by specifying variables, feedback loops, and time delays"* (Objective) and the simulation will *"quantify how feedback loops influence variables over time"* (Methods/Analysis).
- One *implicit* candidate loop is described qualitatively in the Introduction's framing of the prior failure: clinicians perceive overabundance of alerts → develop alert fatigue → ignore alerts → CDS is operationally inert → patient outcomes do not improve → users perceive CDS as lacking utility → further disengagement. This is consistent with a *reinforcing disengagement loop*, but the paper does not draw it, sign it, or name it.
- A second *implicit* candidate loop, from the Use Case section: lack of transparency in the underlying ML score → user perceives the score as lacking validity/usefulness → user does not act on alerts → no observed clinical benefit → reinforces perception of lack of validity. Again, not drawn.
- One concrete causal *edge* is reported as elicited in Session 2: participants identified *"quality of data input as a factor affecting use of the score,"* with the facilitator probing how input quality changes during a patient's stay. This is a node-and-arrow fragment, not a loop, and is given as an example of session content, not as a finalised model element.

**Leverage points ("levers") — Meadows term, in the title:**

- The title and the Discussion both invoke "levers" — Discussion phrasing: *"identify intervenable levers to improve efficiency, reducing the reliance on costly real-world research usually needed to identify implementation barriers, facilitators, and strategies."*
- No specific leverage points are surfaced. The leverage-point identification is positioned as a *deliverable of the simulation*, not as a result of the protocol paper: *"The resulting interactive simulation model will provide an environment to test the implementation of AI-enabled CDS more generally across health systems and identify intervenable levers."*
- The paper does not cite Meadows; the "levers" framing is used in lay (not technical Meadows-typology) sense. There is no distinction made between, e.g., parameter-level levers (constants in the equations), feedback-loop strength levers, or paradigm-level levers.

**Net assessment for the spawn brief:** This protocol is *programmatic*, not *substantive*, on stocks/flows/loops/leverage points. It commits to the methodology that will produce them and names the software (Vensim) and the data sources (GMB session transcripts + the CLD itself) that will feed the translation, but the specific loops, stocks, flows, equations, and leverage points are deferred to a future results paper. The hedging ("we are translating", "we will define", "we anticipate generating", "the resulting interactive simulation model will provide") is consistent and should be preserved downstream.

### Confusions / unresolved points

1. **Where is the CLD?** The Results section says *"three GMB sessions were completed during that time"* and that the CLD is the qualitative deliverable, but the CLD itself is not reproduced in the protocol PDF — it is deferred to a future results paper. For a "protocol" publication whose stated empirical work is *already complete through 2022-2025* and whose CLD is described as fully synthesised and validated by independent charge-nurse / nursing-leadership interviews, the absence of the CLD in this paper is a content-strategy choice (split publication) rather than a constraint. Downstream readers wanting the actual model structure will need to wait for or hunt the companion results paper.
2. **Translation method underspecified.** The protocol says it will "translate" a qualitative CLD into a quantitative stock-and-flow model, but does not describe the translation procedure (e.g., how arrow polarities become equation signs; how variables become stocks vs. auxiliaries vs. flows; how delays elicited in Session 2's behaviour-over-time graphs become integration time-constants). The Discussion's phrase *"adjustable parameters to explore what would happen"* implies parameter calibration, but no calibration procedure or data source for parameter values is given. This is the single largest methodological gap the clarity-checker audit flags.
3. **"Identify intervenable levers" is asserted but not operationalised.** The "levers"/"leverage points" framing is in the title and recurs in the Discussion but is never tied to a specific analysis step. There is no sensitivity-analysis protocol, no Meadows-typology classification of leverage candidates, no decision rule for what counts as a lever vs. an uninfluential parameter. Pass 3 would interrogate this.
4. **Sample size and saturation.** Session 1 had 10 participants who worked with NEWS or Duke EWS. The recruitment target was "at least 1 developer and implementer for each or both of the 2 EWS scores and at least 2 users of each score" (i.e., minimum 6, with two EWS types). No saturation criterion or analytic justification for n=10 is offered, and the protocol notes only that DUH staff, DRH staff, and developers "met separately to facilitate identification of themes specific to their contexts" — implying within-stratum sample sizes smaller than 10.
5. **Adoption-of-CDS as a stock.** The paper proposes modelling "adoption of CDS" as a stock. This is conceptually unusual — adoption is more often modelled in diffusion-of-innovations system dynamics (Sterman, *Business Dynamics*, ch. 9) as a *flow* (the rate of new adopters per unit time) integrated into stocks of "potential adopters" and "adopters." The protocol's phrasing leaves this design choice unresolved, and the clarity-checker flags it as a place where the eventual paper will have to commit to a specific Bass-diffusion-style or alternative formulation.
6. **Implicit conflation of "adoption" with "appropriate use."** The Bedoya 2019 evidence is that nurses ignored the alert 86% of the time *even when accuracy was good*; the protocol treats "adoption" as the outcome variable but does not clarify whether "adoption" means (a) using the score at all, (b) acting on a high-risk alert appropriately, or (c) not over-acting on low-risk alerts. These have different feedback structures (e.g., (b) and (c) can trade off via alert fatigue) and need disambiguation before the simulation is built.
7. **Hedge calibration on generalisation.** Conclusions say *"these findings will have broader applicability beyond just EWSs"* — promoting future-tense certainty about generalisation from a single-site, two-hospital case study. The internal hedge in the body of the Discussion is softer (*"could offer value", "approach could improve"*), so the Conclusions overshoot the body's hedging on this single point. Preserve the softer "could" downstream when synthesising.
