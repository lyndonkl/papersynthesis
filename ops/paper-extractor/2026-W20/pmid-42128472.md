### 1. Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences)
The paper argues that Danish pig veterinarians have largely adapted their antimicrobial-use practices over time — 87% report doing so — and that the dominant adaptation driver has been *legislative restrictions* rather than autonomous professional change. It further argues that a stable set of on-farm practices enables further AMU reduction (gilt acclimatisation, all-in/all-out, feed quality, ventilation), but that progress is bounded by financial constraints, a paucity of effective AM alternatives, and structural housing limitations that veterinarians cannot themselves resolve. A specific empirical observation — that legislative requirements have *unintentionally* increased aminoglycoside and macrolide usage — is offered as evidence that top-down regulation produces system-level side effects detectable only with a systems-thinking lens. The overall conclusion (hedge preserved) is that veterinarians occupy a *pivotal* bridging role between regulatory frameworks and on-farm practice, and that future efforts should "focus on enhancing the feasibility and effectiveness of AMU reduction strategies, ensuring they are supported by robust scientific evidence and feasibility analysis." The argument's evidentiary base is a single mixed-methods survey (n=73, 58% response, Danish pig vets only); the load-bearing inferential step from survey responses to systems-map + iceberg-model + leverage-point claims is the methodology's primary analytic move and cannot be evaluated without the methods section. *(Main-argument body abstract-only; full text unavailable for evidence-chain audit.)*

### 2. The Big Question
How can livestock antimicrobial use be reduced sustainably — without compromising animal health or welfare — given that a decade-plus of top-down regulatory tools (Yellow Card scheme, VetStat, DDDvet caps) has captured the easy gains and the remaining barriers are structural, economic, and bound up in practitioner mental models? More broadly: how do you find *leverage points* in a tightly regulated socio-veterinary system where the front-line prescribers (veterinarians) have professional discretion but operate inside legislative, financial, and infrastructural constraints they did not design?

### 4. Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss
*(All glosses are general-field, not from the paper itself.)*
- **AMU / AMR** — antimicrobial use; antimicrobial resistance. AMU is the policy lever, AMR the public-health endpoint.
- **One Health** — framework treating human, animal, and environmental health as one interconnected system; the dominant rhetorical frame for livestock-AMU policy.
- **Yellow Card scheme** *(not in abstract; Danish-context background)* — Danish regulatory mechanism penalising pig farms whose AMU exceeds threshold levels (DDDvet/100 pigs); foundational to the "legislative restrictions" the paper cites.
- **VetStat** *(not in abstract; background)* — Danish national veterinary-prescription database; the data substrate for AMU monitoring.
- **DDDvet** *(background)* — Defined Daily Dose for veterinary medicine; standard unit for cross-comparable AMU measurement.
- **Gilt acclimatisation** — herd-management practice in which incoming young female breeding pigs (gilts) are exposed to the farm's existing pathogen environment under controlled conditions to build immunity before entering breeding; a disease-prevention substitute for prophylactic antimicrobials.
- **All-in/all-out** — batch-rotation system where a barn/section is fully populated and depopulated together, allowing cleaning and disinfection between batches; reduces transmission and the need for therapeutic AMU.
- **Grounded Theory** — qualitative-research methodology (Glaser & Strauss) for building theory inductively from coded textual data; appropriate for open-ended survey responses.
- **Systems thinking map** — visual representation of feedback loops and causal relationships in a complex system; here applied to the AMU prescribing system.
- **Iceberg model** *(Meadows / Senge lineage; central to the operator's focus)* — four-level analytic frame: (i) **events** (visible incidents — e.g., a prescription written); (ii) **patterns/trends** (recurring behaviours — e.g., 87% adjusted AMU over time); (iii) **systemic structures** (rules, incentives, infrastructure that generate the patterns — e.g., Yellow Card, housing constraints); (iv) **mental models** (beliefs and assumptions held by actors — e.g., veterinarian beliefs about AM alternatives). The paper produces an iceberg model; the abstract does not enumerate the four layers' specific contents.
- **Leverage points** *(Meadows term, operator-flagged)* — places within a complex system where a small shift produces large change. Meadows ranks 12 categories of leverage from weakest (parameter tuning) to strongest (transcending paradigms).
- **Aminoglycosides / macrolides** — two AM classes whose usage the paper reports has *unintentionally increased* under existing legislation; the substitution finding.
- **Eradication protocols** — herd-level disease-elimination programmes (e.g., for PRRS, Mycoplasma); proposed by respondents as a future leverage point.

### 7. Confusions and unresolved points (Pass 3 candidates)
- **Iceberg-layer contents — the operator's central focus is not directly answerable from the abstract.** The abstract names enabler practices (events / behavioural patterns level), barriers (which span structural and mental-model levels), and future strategies (proposed structural interventions), but does not declare which findings the authors assign to each of the four iceberg layers. *Without the figure of the iceberg model itself, the assignment is an inference rather than an extraction.* A best-guess mapping from the abstract content is below; treat it as a hypothesis for Pass 3 confirmation, not as an extraction:
  - *Events (surface):* individual AM prescription decisions; specific cases requiring treatment; the 87%-adjusted-practices finding can be read either here (as an aggregated event count) or one layer down (as a pattern).
  - *Patterns / trends:* the longitudinal AMU-reduction trajectory in Danish pig production; the unintended increase in aminoglycoside/macrolide usage; the cluster of enabling practices (gilt acclimatisation, all-in/all-out, feed quality, ventilation) becoming normalised.
  - *Systemic structures:* legislative restrictions (Yellow Card, VetStat); financial constraints on farmers; pig-housing infrastructure limitations; the absence of cost-effective vaccines and rapid diagnostics in the available toolkit.
  - *Mental models:* veterinarian beliefs about which alternatives to AMs are viable; assumptions about animal-welfare trade-offs in any reduction strategy; the implicit model of the veterinarian's professional role (bridging regulator and farmer).
- **Leverage points named for intervention — operator focus, partially answerable.** The abstract names three explicit future-strategy needs (in respondents' own framing): **cost-effective vaccines**, **better and faster diagnostic tools**, and **implementation of eradication protocols**. By Meadows' typology these sit roughly at the *parameter / information-flow* and *rules-of-the-system* layers — moderate-strength leverage, not paradigm-level. *Whether the authors themselves label these as "leverage points" with that taxonomy, or as a flatter list of stakeholder needs, is not determinable from the abstract.*
- **The aminoglycoside/macrolide unintended-consequence finding** — abstract states this as fact but gives no magnitude, no time series, no statistical test. The mechanism (substitution? therapeutic-class switching? selection inside the Yellow Card scoring formula?) is implicit. This is the most policy-actionable finding and the one most in need of Pass 3 examination if the operator escalates.
- **Quantitative-analysis output** — abstract states only that "work experience and continued education" were tested against AMU practices; the *direction* and *strength* of any relationship are not reported in the abstract.
