### One-line summary

LegalMALR is a Chinese statute retrieval framework that pairs a multi-agent query-understanding system with a zero-shot LLM reranker to handle implicit, multi-issue, colloquial legal queries that defeat dense-retriever + lightweight-reranker pipelines.

## Pass 2 — Content grasp

### Main argument and supporting evidence

The paper argues that single-query dense retrieval and lightweight cross-encoder rerankers are jointly insufficient for Chinese statute retrieval over colloquial, implicit, multi-issue queries, and that the gap can be closed by (a) replacing the single-query stage with a multi-agent query-understanding system (MAS) that iteratively produces complementary legal reformulations, (b) stabilising the MAS's stochastic LLM-driven action selection with Generalized Reinforcement Policy Optimization (GRPO) trained only with LoRA adapters against a trajectory-level MAS-stage-recall reward (Eq. 1-7), and (c) ranking the merged candidate pool with a zero-shot commercial LLM (Qwen-Max) used purely at inference. Support comes from two evaluations: on the public STARD benchmark (309 test queries, 1.76 gold statutes/query on average) LegalMALR reports Recall@10 = 0.8195 and MRR@10 = 0.7367, +6.16 / +6.31 points over the strongest in-house RAG baseline Qwen3-Original (Table 5), and on the authors' new CSAID benchmark (118 difficult queries, 7.16 gold statutes/query) Recall@10 = 0.6841, MRR@10 = 0.9161, nDCG@10 = 0.7126, HitRate@10 = 0.9915, +4.41 to +8.71 absolute points over Qwen3-Original (Table 6). Two ablations decompose the gain: MAS+GRPO improves MAS-stage Recall@14 from 0.8012 (zero-shot MAS) to 0.8396 on STARD and from 0.6969 to 0.7478 on CSAID at a matched ~60-embedding-call budget (Table 7), and swapping the lightweight Qwen3-Reranker for the Qwen-Max LLM reranker, holding the candidate set fixed, lifts Recall@10 from 0.7230 to 0.8101 on STARD with a dense retriever and 0.5531 to 0.6329 on CSAID (Table 8). The argument is internally consistent: the two ablation axes are orthogonal and each is shown to contribute, and the cost analysis (Sec 4.6) defends the budget-matching by showing the empirical 60.3-embedding-call mean for MAS matches the fixed 60-call baseline.

### The Big Question

How can retrieval-augmented generation pipelines be made to handle queries whose surface form omits the legal terminology, conditional structure, and multi-element doctrinal alignment that statutory applicability depends on — i.e., the gap between lay-language legal questions and the structured, multi-element form of statute law in civil-law systems. Cast more broadly: when query and corpus speak structurally different languages, can multi-perspective query understanding plus large-model reasoning at rerank time substitute for unavailable supervised relevance labels?

### Specific hypotheses / design claims tested

The paper is engineering-oriented, so these read as design claims, defended by the experiments rather than as pre-registered hypotheses:

1. **H1 — Multi-perspective beats single rewrite.** Iterating multiple specialised reformulation agents (six roles: Planner, Single-Element Rewrite, Supplementary-Element Rewrite, Multi-Element Decomposition, Supportive-Law Rewrite, Semantic-Abnormality Repair; Table 2 + Table 10 in App. B) over the same query produces a candidate pool with higher recall than a single dense-retrieval pass at matched embedding-call budget. Tested in Section 4.5 (Fig. 4: one-step MAS Recall@10 = 0.56 vs multi-step MAS Recall@14 = 0.70 vs MAS+GRPO = 0.75) and in the cost analysis (Sec 4.6).
2. **H2 — GRPO stabilises MAS.** Treating each MAS rollout as a trajectory and optimising the unified Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 policy with GRPO using terminal MAS-stage recall + per-step penalty + intermediate hit reward (Eqs. 1-7) raises mean recall toward the empirical upper-bound. Tested in Table 3 (eight-rollout per-query max/mean/min recall = 0.8725 / 0.8098 / 0.7511, indicating headroom) and Table 4 (categorical breakdown showing 186 + 78 unstable-but-recoverable queries that GRPO is designed to fix).
3. **H3 — Large-model reranking beats lightweight reranking on legally complex candidate sets.** A zero-shot Qwen-Max reranker, with no chain-of-thought generation (no-CoT, deterministic JSON output), produces better rankings than Qwen3-4B-Reranker on the same candidate pool. Tested in Table 8 (Qwen-R → LLM-R lifts MRR@10 from 0.6305 to 0.7546 on STARD-dense, 0.8171 to 0.9248 on CSAID-dense).
4. **H4 — Cross-domain generalisation.** A MAS policy trained only on STARD transfers to the harder CSAID distribution. Tested by training exclusively on STARD's 1,234 training queries and reporting CSAID as out-of-distribution (Table 6 — LegalMALR is best on every metric).

### Unfamiliar terms / concepts requiring gloss

- **GRPO (Generalized Reinforcement Policy Optimization)** — the optimisation procedure used to update the unified MAS policy. The paper's formulation (Eqs. 5-7) is a REINFORCE-style estimator with group-wise advantage normalisation (sample K=8 trajectories per query, normalise rewards by group mean/std, weight log-policy gradients accordingly); no value function or learned advantage estimator. Trained with LoRA only, backbone frozen, ~16h on 8x RTX 4090.
- **MAS (Multi-Agent Query Understanding System)** — the iterative reformulation loop. A Planner Agent inspects the current candidate-set coverage and picks one of six Rewrite Agents to run; that agent emits a reformulation (or several, for Multi-Element Decomposition) that triggers a dense retrieval + lightweight rerank; results are merged into a growing pool; Planner decides whether to loop again (max 4 reformulation rounds + 1 exit iteration).
- **STARD** — public Chinese statute-retrieval benchmark (Su et al. 2024, EMNLP); 1,234 train + 309 test queries, 1.76 gold statutes/query average, 55,348-article corpus.
- **CSAID (Chinese Statute Ambiguity and Implicitness Dataset)** — the paper's contributed benchmark. 118 anonymised queries, three sources: 60 from a commercial legal-platform retrieval log, 30 written by legal researchers around classical cases, 28 from documented retrieval failures; mean 39.66 chars/query, 7.16 gold statutes/query, 79,055-article corpus; quality-controlled by legal researchers then licensed attorneys.
- **Statutory applicability** — the legal-domain task definition: whether a statute's factual elements, conditional structures, exception clauses, and doctrinal coherence are satisfied by the factual scenario in the query. Distinct from semantic similarity.
- **No-CoT reranking** — the deliberate choice to have Qwen-Max output only a JSON index list without an explanatory rationale; rationale-generation gave "modest" gains but cost latency, format stability, and fair-comparison clarity (Sec 3.4).
- **Qwen3-Embedding-4B / Qwen3-Reranker-4B / Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 / Qwen-Max** — four distinct Qwen-family models used at different pipeline stages; the 4B models are open-source and used without finetuning except for LoRA adapters on the Instruct model; Qwen-Max is a closed-API commercial model used only for final reranking.

### Figure-by-figure analysis

- **Fig. 1 (p. 3) — Conceptual overview.** Diagrams the gap (low recall / wrong coverage) of conventional RAG on colloquial legal queries and motivates MAS + LLM Reranker as the two-stage answer. Pedagogical, not load-bearing.
- **Fig. 2 (p. 6) — Architecture, three rows.** Compares the baseline dense-retrieval + lightweight reranker pipeline (row 1) against MAS + LLM Reranker (row 2) against MAS + GRPO + LLM Reranker (row 3). The point is that the contributions stack additively.
- **Fig. 3 (p. 7) — End-to-end processing flow.** Shows the MAS iterative loop in detail: Planner → Rewrite Agent → RAG (dense + lightweight rerank) → merge into accumulated set → Planner re-evaluates coverage → loop or exit → LLM Reranker. This is the most operationally useful figure; reading it once clarifies how the paper's "agents" relate to one another.
- **Fig. 4 (p. 19) — One-step vs multi-step vs MAS+GRPO.** Compact ablation: one-step MAS Recall@10 = 0.56 / HitRate@10 = 0.96; multi-step MAS Recall@14 = 0.70 / 0.98; MAS+GRPO Recall@14 = 0.75 / 1.00. K differs across the three configurations (10 vs 14 vs 14), which slightly inflates the gap; the paper acknowledges this by noting MAS+GRPO operates "with dynamic candidate pool sizes (approximately 14 on average)." This is one place the Recall delta is less clean than it visually appears.
- **Fig. 5 (App. D, p. 31) — Case-study trajectories.** Two real MAS+GRPO trajectories: a business-license reissue query that requires two reformulations (single-element rewrite → supplementary-element rewrite) to reach Recall@All = 1.0, and a divorce-property query that terminates after one rewrite. Illustrates the adaptive-depth claim; cumulative recall values are diagnostic only (the system does not see them at inference, per the appendix's careful disclaimer).
- **Table 1 (p. 9) — Dataset stats.** Establishes CSAID is substantially harder than STARD on every axis (~4x relevant statutes/query, longer corpus articles, longer queries).
- **Table 2 (p. 11) and Table 10 (App. B, p. 28) — Agent roles.** Table 2 is the conceptual role enumeration; Table 10 is functional summaries of the (Chinese) system prompts in English. Important caveat: the actual Chinese prompts are not disclosed verbatim — only summaries — which limits reproducibility (see Confusions below).
- **Table 3 (p. 12).** Per-query MAS recall over 8 rollouts: max 0.8725, mean 0.8098, min 0.7511. The max-vs-mean gap (~6 points) is the explicit motivation for GRPO.
- **Table 4 (p. 13).** Behavioural categories of MAS rollouts: 763 stable-correct, 186 occasionally-correct-but-unstable, 78 never-fully-correct-but-unstable, 207 consistently-incorrect. GRPO is plausibly useful for categories 2 and 3 (264 queries, ~22% of training queries). Note the denominator is unstated but adds to 1,234 = STARD train size; the categorisation is over training rollouts, not held-out data.
- **Table 5 (p. 16) — STARD test results.** LegalMALR Recall@10 = 0.8195, MRR@10 = 0.7367, vs strongest RAG baseline Qwen3-Original 0.7579 / 0.6736 and strongest non-RAG fine-tuned baseline Dense-STARD 0.6061 / 0.4724. Margins over RAG are real but moderate (~6 points absolute); against non-RAG baselines they are very large but those baselines are reproduced from the STARD publication, not re-run, so the comparison is "trust-the-prior-paper" rather than head-to-head.
- **Table 6 (p. 16) — CSAID test results.** LegalMALR best on every metric. Margin over Qwen3-SFT (the strongest baseline here) is +4.98 in MRR@10, +5.18 in Recall@10, +7.15 in nDCG@10. CSAID is the authors' own dataset, which is the standard double-edged sword: the paper's framework was designed against this distribution.
- **Table 7 (p. 18) — MAS / GRPO ablation.** Isolates MAS contribution from LLM-Reranker contribution. Zero-shot MAS already beats Qwen3-SFT on Recall@14, and GRPO adds another 3-5 points. HitRate@14 hits 1.0000 on CSAID — every query has at least one gold statute in the top-14 candidate pool, which is the structural condition the LLM Reranker needs to perform well.
- **Table 8 (p. 19) — LLM Reranker ablation.** The cleanest causal demonstration: same candidate set, different reranker. Always favours LLM-R. Notably, the lift from Qwen-R to LLM-R is larger under Qwen3-E 60c (+8.7 points Recall@10 on STARD) than under MAS 14c (+4.3 points) — suggesting LLM-R's edge comes partly from filtering irrelevant candidates, which MAS has already largely done.

### Confusions / unresolved points

1. **GRPO formulation is non-standard.** The Eq. 7 objective `L_GRPO(θ) = -1/K Σ R̂_i log π_θ(τ_i)` is written as REINFORCE with group-normalised advantages and no KL penalty to a reference policy, no PPO-style ratio clipping. This is *not* the GRPO of DeepSeek-Math 2024 (which includes a clipped surrogate and a reference-policy KL term). The paper calls this "Generalized Reinforcement Policy Optimization" and cites no GRPO paper. It is unclear whether this is a deliberate simplification, a naming collision with the better-known GRPO, or an oversight. A future-work reviewer would need to see Eq. 7 reconciled with prior GRPO literature.
2. **Reward shaping coefficients are unspecified.** `α` in Eq. 3 (intermediate hit-reward shaping coefficient) is described as "a small shaping coefficient" with no numerical value. Combined with the −0.05 step penalty and −5 fallback penalty, the relative magnitudes of these three reward components determine policy behaviour; without α, the result is not reproducible from the paper alone.
3. **K mismatch in Fig. 4.** One-step is reported at K=10, multi-step and MAS+GRPO at K=14. The paper rationalises this as "dynamic candidate pool sizes" but it conflates two effects (more reformulations + more candidates retained). A K=10 comparison across all three configurations would isolate the MAS-iteration effect more cleanly.
4. **Behavioural-category counts in Table 4 sum to 1,234.** This is the STARD training set, which means the rollout-stability analysis is done on training data, not a held-out split. The paper does not report whether the same categorical distribution holds on the 309-query test set or on CSAID.
5. **Non-RAG baselines on STARD are reproduced, not re-run.** Table 5's lexical-matching, open-domain-PLM, legal-PLM, and fine-tuned-PLM rows are all "taken directly from the STARD publication." Differences in retrieval budget, hardware, and corpus version are not controlled. This affects the headline non-RAG vs LegalMALR comparison but not the RAG-vs-RAG (Qwen3-Original / Infly-Hybrid / Qwen3-SFT) comparison, which is run in-house.
6. **CSAID is constructed and evaluated by the same authors.** Standard concern with author-released benchmarks: the framework's design implicitly co-evolved with the benchmark. The paper partially addresses this by training only on STARD and evaluating CSAID zero-shot, but the dataset's "difficult cases" definition is itself a design choice that may favour the multi-perspective architecture being proposed.
7. **MAS agent prompts are summarised, not disclosed.** Table 10 (App. B) gives English functional summaries of system prompts that were Chinese in the actual experiments. The paper states the summaries "preserve the functional intent ... while omitting formatting patterns and implementation-specific phrasing that do not influence model behaviour" — but for LLM agents, prompt phrasing very plausibly does influence model behaviour. Combined with code availability at github.com/lyxx3rd/LegalMALR (stated in Code Availability), the full prompts may be recoverable, but reading the paper alone leaves prompt sensitivity un-auditable.
8. **No human-evaluation or legal-correctness audit of reranker output.** The LLM Reranker is evaluated only via retrieval metrics against gold statutes; the paper does not audit whether Qwen-Max's "legal reasoning" is actually substantive or whether the reranker is exploiting surface cues. The "no-CoT" choice (Sec 3.4) makes it deliberately uninspectable: the model "conducts internal reasoning implicitly but outputs only the final ranking." This is honest but means the "legal reasoning" claim rests entirely on the downstream metric improvements, not on inspected reasoning traces.
9. **Hedging is generally well-calibrated, with one mild overclaim.** Most claims preserve appropriate hedging ("substantially improves", "consistently enhances"). The strongest claim — "the system that jointly enhances recall, interpretive robustness, and final ranking accuracy" (Sec 5.0.1) — overstates "interpretive robustness," which is a design claim, not a measured property; the paper measures recall stability across rollouts (Table 3) but does not measure interpretability or robustness of the reformulations themselves. Minor.
