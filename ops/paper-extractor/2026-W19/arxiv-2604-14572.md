## Pass 1 — Inspectional reading

### Five Cs

1. **Category** — Empirical system / architecture description with benchmark evaluation. The paper introduces a named system ("Corpus2Skill") and evaluates it on a 10-dataset suite against named baselines. Secondary frame: methods paper (proposes a new distillation procedure for converting corpora into navigable agent artifacts).

2. **Context** — Sits at the intersection of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and agentic / tool-using LLM systems. Positions explicitly against two prior paradigms named in the abstract: dense retrieval (the classical RAG substrate) and agentic RAG (LLM-as-orchestrator-over-retrievers). The "skill directory" framing places it adjacent to the broader 2025-26 trend of treating compiled knowledge as agent-callable skills rather than retrievable chunks. arXiv categories cs.IR + cs.AI + cs.CL + cs.MA confirm the IR / multi-agent crossover positioning.

3. **Correctness (first-glance)** — No first-glance concerns about overreach. The result claim is explicitly hedged to "specific corpus types" — the method achieves highest F1 on some, not all, of the 10 datasets. That qualifier is appropriate honesty in the abstract; whether the body characterises *which* corpus types and *why* is a Pass 2 question. The thinness of the abstract (three sentences) means very little is verifiable at the inspectional level.

4. **Contributions** — Two claimed contributions, both hedged appropriately:
   - **New method:** Corpus2Skill, which distills a document corpus into a "hierarchical skill directory" that an agent navigates.
   - **Empirical result:** "Outperforms dense retrieval and agentic RAG baselines, achieving highest F1 across 10-dataset suite on specific corpus types" — note the scope qualifier on the result. The paper does not claim universal superiority; it claims best F1 on particular corpus types within the suite.

5. **Clarity** — `dense-but-thin`. Title is provocative and aligned with the abstract's framing ("Don't Retrieve, Navigate"). The abstract itself is unusually terse — three sentences that name the system, gesture at the mechanism (hierarchical skill directory + agent navigation), and report a hedged headline number. Key terms ("skill", "skill directory", "navigation") are introduced without definitional anchor at the abstract level. Acronym load is low. Pass 2 will need the body to recover what "skill" means structurally and what differentiates a navigated skill directory from a retrieved hierarchical index.

### One-line summary

Corpus2Skill compiles an enterprise document corpus into a hierarchical agent-navigable skill directory that beats dense retrieval and agentic RAG on F1 across a 10-dataset benchmark suite for specific corpus types.

## Pass 2 — Content grasp

### 1. Main argument and supporting evidence (3-5 sentences)

The paper argues that for enterprise QA over single-domain, atomic-document corpora, the right primitive is not retrieval but *navigation*: a corpus should be compiled offline into a filesystem-shaped hierarchy of LLM-written skill summaries (`SKILL.md` / `INDEX.md` / `documents.json`) that an LLM agent then top-down browses via two tools (`code_execution` for file viewing, `get_document` for full-text lookup), exploiting progressive disclosure so per-turn token cost stays low while the agent retains a corpus-wide map. The central evidence is a 200-query head-to-head on WixQA (Wix customer-support corpus, 6,221 docs) where Corpus2Skill posts F1=0.468, Factuality=0.739, Context Recall=0.673 against the next-best per-metric numbers from Hybrid/Dense/Agentic/RAPTOR (e.g., 23% F1 lift over Agentic at the same $0.089/query after Anthropic prompt caching is enabled), supported by ablations showing (a) the hierarchy is robust to branching ratio choices within ~0.02 F1, (b) max-rounds budget barely matters (F1=0.458 at 5 rounds vs 0.468 at 20), and (c) swapping Sonnet 4.6 for Haiku 4.5 keeps 91% of F1 at 71% of cost — i.e. quality is in the compiled tree, not in the navigator. A 9-subset RAGBench generalization sweep then *qualifies* the headline by exposing three corpus shapes where the method loses (TatQA homogeneous tables, CUAD long extractive contracts, CovidQA/ExpertQA open-domain pools), letting the authors carve out the regime — atomic, topically-coherent, 0.5–3k-character documents — in which navigation outperforms flat retrieval, and explicitly recommending flat retrieval or RAPTOR elsewhere. The hedging-vs-evidence audit shows the paper consistently *characterizes* a regime rather than claiming universal superiority: "complementary design point," "right primitive for…," "We treat FinQA and CovidQA as ties rather than losses" — load-bearing claims are sized to the data.

### 2. The Big Question

How should LLM agents be coupled to large structured knowledge bases when the LLM's own reasoning capabilities exceed what a passive top-k retriever can exploit — specifically, can compile-time investment in corpus organization substitute for, rather than augment, query-time embedding search? More broadly: the paper sits inside the 2025-26 debate over whether RAG should keep adding agentic / hierarchical / graph layers *on top of* embedding retrieval, or whether the embedding retriever itself can be eliminated and replaced with agent-navigable static artifacts (the "skills" framing imported from Anthropic's filesystem-based agent-skills pattern).

### 3. Specific design claims being defended

(This is a systems paper without explicit hypotheses; the design claims it defends are:)

- **DC1.** Corpus navigability is an underexplored dimension of current RAG systems; LLMs currently lack a structured overview of the knowledge base.
- **DC2.** A compile-then-navigate pipeline (iterative bottom-up K-Means clustering + LLM summarization at each level + filesystem materialization) produces a hierarchy whose summaries are themselves the routing signal — no embedding search needed at serve time.
- **DC3.** Progressive disclosure via `SKILL.md` (metadata) → `INDEX.md` (sub-listing) → `get_document(doc_id)` (full text) keeps per-query input tokens bounded while preserving corpus-wide visibility.
- **DC4.** Quality lives in the compiled tree, not in the navigator: serving-model swaps preserve most of the gain (Haiku 4.5 retains 91% F1).
- **DC5.** Navigation beats flat retrieval and beats agentic RAG specifically when the corpus is (i) single-domain, (ii) topically-coherent at the document level, (iii) atomic (each doc covers one feature/entity/how-to), and (iv) document length is in 0.5–3k chars. The reverse claim is explicitly defended too — flat retrieval is the right default elsewhere.
- **DC6.** The dominant failure mode is *top-level routing error* (38 of 62 failures on WixQA stem from choosing the wrong top-level branch), not within-branch search.

### 4. Unfamiliar terms requiring gloss

- **Agent skill (informational vs procedural)** — a reusable filesystem-based module read by an agent on demand. Prior skills encode *how to perform a task*; Corpus2Skill repurposes the format to encode *what a corpus contains*. Formally adopted from Jiang et al. 2026: a tuple (C, π, T, R) of applicability condition / execution policy / termination condition / reusable interface.
- **Progressive disclosure** — Shneiderman-1996 UI pattern adapted for agents: metadata at startup, full instructions on demand, resources as needed. Used here to bound per-step token cost.
- **Compile phase vs serve phase** — offline corpus → skill-tree build (one-time, ~6.5 min on 32-CPU for WixQA, $5–$10 in LLM calls) vs online per-query navigation by the LLM agent.
- **Skill forest / skill tree** — set of K independent top-level skill directories, each a tree of summaries; corpus is partitioned across them via single-path K-Means.
- **Iterative bottom-up K-Means hierarchy** — embed all docs, partition into ⌈n/p⌉ groups (branching ratio p), summarize each cluster with an LLM, re-embed summaries, repeat until ≤K top-level clusters remain.
- **Hard single-path clustering** — each document is assigned to exactly one branch (enables filesystem materialization but creates blind spots for cross-cutting docs).
- **Compact mode** — workaround for Anthropic Skills API's 200-file-per-skill cap: parent `INDEX.md` inlines near-leaf children's content instead of creating subdirectories.
- **Anthropic ephemeral prompt caching** — read-cached navigation prefix billed at 0.1x; cache-write at 1.25x. Drops Corpus2Skill from $0.172 to $0.089 per query by reusing 70% of the prompt across consecutive turns/queries.
- **Token F1 / BLEU / ROUGE-1 / ROUGE-2** — lexical overlap metrics against gold answer. **Factuality** — LLM-as-judge 1–5 score (normalized) of claim-consistency with gold. **Context Recall** — LLM-judge fraction of gold-answer essential claims attributable to retrieved context.
- **RAGBench / WixQA** — RAGBench is Friel et al. 2024's RAG generator-evaluation benchmark, here reformulated as a retrieval-stress benchmark by deduplicating the expanded retrieval pool (train+val+test). WixQA is Cohen et al. 2025's 6,221-article Wix customer-support corpus with 200 expert-written QA pairs.
- **Expanded retrieval pool** — union of train/val/test docs deduplicated by content hash, used as the retrieval corpus to force methods to compete against thousands of distractors rather than per-row gold contexts.
- **Multiplicative information funnel** — N ÷p→ N/p ÷p→ ... ÷p→ N/p^L ≈ 1; the paper's framing of navigation depth scaling as O(log_p N), with WixQA reaching a single doc in L=3 levels.
- **Skill API constraints (Anthropic)** — current limits cited: 8 skills/request, 200 files/skill, 30 MB/skill. The 200-file cap motivates compact mode.

### 5. Figure-by-figure analysis

- **Figure 1 (p.1, schematic).** Retrieve-vs-Navigate side-by-side. Top: classical RAG = embed → vectorDB → top-k → LLM. Bottom: Corpus2Skill = LLM agent reading a forest of skill/idx nodes with a red dashed line showing backtrack from an unproductive branch. Takeaway: makes the load-bearing distinction (passive consumption of top-k vs active branch-pruning with backtrack) visible at a glance. No axes / no data — pure positioning figure.
- **Figure 2 (p.5, system architecture).** Two-phase pipeline. Compile phase shows the embed → cluster → LLM-summarize → re-embed loop materializing `SKILL.md`/`INDEX.md` files plus an external `documents.json` store. Serve phase shows the agent with two tools and an interaction trace. Architecturally honest: the agent is just an LLM + two tools, not a custom retrieval engine. Takeaway: deployment surface is small (LLM + filesystem + Skills API), which is the load-bearing claim for the "LLM only" serve-infra row in Table 8.
- **Figure 3 (p.6, file contents).** Abridged `SKILL.md` (root, 1,513-doc Wix-commerce-monetization skill, 16 subgroups listed) and `INDEX.md` (leaf, 5 documents with titles). Concrete: shows the YAML frontmatter (name/description/level/num_documents) the agent sees. Takeaway: the navigation files are genuinely small (≤2KB) and human-readable; this corroborates the progressive-disclosure efficiency claim.
- **Figure 4 (p.7, Trace 1: direct descent).** WixQA query "switch my business type from sole prop to LLC in order to use an EIN" → 4-step trace (SKILL.md → subgroup INDEX.md → leaf INDEX.md → get_document). Reaches a single doc with Factuality=1.0. Takeaway: when the query topic maps cleanly to a top-level branch, the descent is short and the answer is grounded in one article. The trace is faithfully reported (selected doc id `d56cc797742e`) — useful evidence for the navigation-actually-works claim.
- **Figure 5 (p.7, Trace 2: cross-branch).** Query "change the currency for my course" → 7-turn trace visiting two subgroups within the same skill (online-programs + billing-documents) to combine evidence. Takeaway: documents the cross-branch synthesis behavior that flat retrieval cannot easily reproduce. The trace shows the agent first checking the program-settings branch, finding currency is a site-level setting, then pivoting to billing — an instance of the "the agent can reason about where to look" claim.
- **Table 1 (p.8, WixQA main results).** Six metrics × six methods on 200 queries. Corpus2Skill best on all six quality metrics: F1 0.468 (vs Hybrid 0.360, Dense 0.363, Agentic 0.381, RAPTOR 0.389, BM25 0.342); Factuality 0.739 (Agentic 0.719, RAPTOR 0.675); Context Recall 0.673 (RAPTOR 0.616, Agentic 0.498). $/query 0.089 vs Agentic 0.088 (after caching). Sample size n=200 is moderate but consistent across methods; no error bars or confidence intervals are shown, which is a gap. Takeaway: on this single corpus, Corpus2Skill dominates the frontier; the cost-quality story is "match Agentic on cost, win on every metric." Caveat: WixQA is the *anchor*, not a representative sample — the authors acknowledge this in §4.4.
- **Table 2 (p.9, 9-subset RAGBench generalization).** Per-dataset results across Emanual, DelucionQA, HAGRID, CovidQA, ExpertQA, TechQA, FinQA, TatQA, CUAD. Grouped editorially into (a) atomic-single-domain where Corpus2Skill wins (Emanual, DelucionQA, HAGRID), (b) heterogeneous/table-heavy where flat retrieval is competitive (CovidQA, ExpertQA, TechQA, FinQA), (c) homogeneous-tabular or long-extractive where Corpus2Skill loses (TatQA, CUAD). Notable failure: TatQA $/query 0.969 — ~80× more expensive than Dense (0.005) at lower F1 (0.246 vs 0.304) because the agent hits max_turns repeatedly on indistinguishable financial-statement clusters. This is the most honest dataset in the paper — it documents a catastrophic failure mode rather than burying it. Takeaway: the win is regime-specific.
- **Table 3 (p.10, 10-dataset macro-average).** Per-metric best in bold. Corpus2Skill leads on every lexical overlap metric (F1 0.350, BLEU 0.103, R-1 0.378, R-2 0.177); Agentic wins Factuality 0.660 (judge rewards "I don't know" fallbacks Agentic produces); Dense wins Context Recall 0.540 (judge rewards span overlap that embedding retrieval directly optimizes). Honest framing: macro-F1 win is "narrowly ahead of Hybrid 0.349" — the lead is 0.001. Takeaway: the macro-average doesn't show a uniform sweep, and the per-metric story shows judge-metric artifacts the authors flag.
- **Tables 4 / 5 / 6 (p.10, ablations).** (4) Cluster structure: narrow p=5 / default p=10 / wide p=20 all within ~0.02 F1, default cheapest at $0.089. (5) Agent exploration budget 5/10/20 rounds within ~0.01 F1; budget-5 cheapest at $0.087. (6) Sonnet 4.6 vs Haiku 4.5: Haiku retains 91% F1, 90% Factuality, 71% cost; Context Recall *increases* with Haiku (0.698 vs 0.673), which the authors interpret as Haiku navigating more thoroughly. Takeaway: the design is robust along three orthogonal axes — the compiled hierarchy carries the signal. This is the strongest evidence behind DC4.
- **Table 7 (p.15, appendix).** The 6 WixQA top-level skill labels (business-tools 931, commerce 1513, content-media 1074, platform-guide 1161, marketing-seo 553, platform-overview 989). Useful concrete artifact for understanding what the LLM is naming.
- **Table 8 (p.22, positioning).** 4-row × 5-column matrix vs Scatter/Gather, RAPTOR, GraphRAG, Voyager/SkillX. Distinguishes input modality (Corpus2Skill = documents, like Scatter/Gather/RAPTOR/GraphRAG; unlike Voyager/SkillX which take trajectories), organization (hierarchical skill tree), navigation (LLM agent), serve infra ("LLM only"). The "LLM only" row is the load-bearing positioning claim — no cluster index, no vector DB, no graph DB at serve time.

### 7. Confusions / unresolved points

- **Per-metric statistical significance is not reported.** Table 1's F1 lead over Hybrid in Table 3 is 0.001 macro-average; without confidence intervals or seed-disclosure for the LLM-judge metrics, it is impossible to tell whether Corpus2Skill macro-F1 lead generalizes. The paper does not state random seeds, judge-model identity for the per-row scores, or run-to-run variance on the 200-query samples.
- **The "judge artifact" framing is asserted, not demonstrated.** The paper explains away Agentic's Factuality win as "the judge rewards 'I don't know' fallbacks" and Dense's Context Recall win as "the judge rewards span overlap." These are plausible but not measured — there is no ablation of judge behavior on canonical refusals or on paraphrased gold spans. Treat these explanations as hypotheses.
- **Compilation cost reporting is thin.** The paper says compilation is $5–$10 in LLM calls + 6.5 min wall-clock for WixQA on a 32-CPU server, but does not separate clustering vs summarization vs labeling cost, does not report how many LLM tokens were burned, and does not characterize how this scales sublinearly/linearly with corpus size. For an "invest at compile time" thesis this is a load-bearing number that's underspecified.
- **The Skills-API dependency is structural, not incidental.** Multiple design choices (8-skill cap → top-level K=7, 200-file/skill cap → compact mode) are shaped by Anthropic's current API limits. The authors say this in §5, but the paper does not measure how the design would shift if the limits relaxed. The framework's design space is partly captured by one vendor's product constraints.
- **WixQA-as-anchor concern.** WixQA is co-developed with Wix-internal taxonomy and labels (per the dataset card, Cohen et al. 2025); the cluster summarizer's labels (`wix-commerce-monetization`, `wix-payments-ecosystem`) closely track human-authored Wix product categories. The paper does not test whether clusters generated on a corpus *without* a human-engineered topic structure (e.g., free-form notes) would route as cleanly.
- **Hard single-path assignment "blind spots" are acknowledged but unmeasured.** §5 calls out that docs spanning multiple topics (e.g., billing AND subscriptions) appear under one branch only, and the failure analysis says 38/62 failures are top-level routing errors — but the paper does not quantify how many failures are *specifically* the single-path artifact vs labeling-quality artifact. Soft/multi-parent clustering is floated as future work.
- **Cost number sensitivity to caching.** Headline cost-parity vs Agentic ($0.089 vs $0.088) is *only true with Anthropic ephemeral prompt caching enabled.* Without caching, $/query is $0.172, roughly 2x Agentic. This is honestly disclosed in §5 but does change the "cost-competitive" framing depending on deployment platform.
- **"Don't Retrieve" is a stronger claim than the body defends.** The title's imperative is universal; the body's claim is regime-specific (atomic, single-domain, 0.5–3k char). The hedging asymmetry — title vs §4.4 — is the one place the paper promotes beyond what the evidence carries. This is mild overclaiming at the title level even though the body's hedging is consistently appropriate.
