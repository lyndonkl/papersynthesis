## Headline

Restricting semantic search to nodes structurally reachable from a known anchor gives 100% structural consistency vs 24-32% for vector and hybrid baselines, at the cost of needing a graph and an anchor at query time.

## Summary

Path-Constrained Retrieval (PCR) layers a graph constraint on top of standard semantic search: only documents reachable from an anchor node in a knowledge graph can be returned. The benchmark (PathRAG-6, six domains, 180 nodes / 360 edges) is small but the result is sharp: PCR achieves full structural consistency with full relevance at rank 10 in the technology domain, while vector and hybrid baselines top out at 24-32% structural consistency. Graph distance of returned context drops 78% on average.

For a practitioner: this is research, not production-ready. The benchmark is too small to generalize, and the technique requires a usable knowledge graph and an anchor node at query time — most agent settings have neither. The diagnostic finding (vector retrieval frequently returns structurally disconnected context) is the operational point a practitioner should take away.

## Specifics

- Benchmark: PathRAG-6 (6 domains, 180 nodes, 360 edges).
- Structural consistency: PCR 100% vs baseline vector and hybrid 24-32%.
- Technology domain: full relevance at rank 10 with full structural consistency.
- Average graph distance of retrieved context: 78% reduction vs baselines.
- Operational requirement: existing knowledge graph and anchor-node selection at query time.
