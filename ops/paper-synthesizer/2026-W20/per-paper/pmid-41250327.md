## Headline
A minimal agent-based model argues that asymmetry between talking and listening propensities — coupled to attention and memory — is itself sufficient to give rise to emergent hierarchy and to set the integration-vs-fragmentation balance of a social network (abstract-only).

## Summary
The paper proposes that the explanatory primitive for online-network structure is not opinion content or topology but per-agent asymmetry in communicative role: who tends to speak versus who tends to listen. The mechanism is a three-element feedback loop coupling speaking activity, attention allocation, and memory — a talker who attracts attention is remembered, which sustains future attention, which sustains future speaking. From simulations of this minimal model, the authors report that self-referential communication generates local dominance and hierarchy, that listener engagement maintains cohesion, and that the feedback loop "sets the balance" between an integrated network and a fragmented one (abstract-only; methods and figures could not be acquired — APS is paywalled and no preprint exists).

## Specifics

- **Modeling primitives (named in abstract).** Per-agent **talker propensity** and **listener propensity** are the asymmetric primitives. **Attention allocation** is the rate-limited resource agents distribute across neighbors. **Memory** is the temporal persistence of past attention — the hysteresis element of the feedback loop. **Openness** is a listener-side parameter governing willingness to attend to non-incumbent talkers (likely analog to a bounded-confidence threshold; functional form not stated, abstract-only).

- **The central feedback loop.** Three-element reinforcing loop: *speaking activity → attention allocation → memory → (back to) speaking activity*. The loop's gain — set jointly by listener engagement, openness, and memory — is what determines regime. This is the paper's central conceptual contribution; the precise update rule and time scale of memory are not in the abstract (abstract-only).

- **Emergent-hierarchy claim.** Self-referential communication "generates" local dominance and hierarchy — hedge preserved; the strength of "generates" is heavier than typical model-paper language for a simulation-only result and would need the figures to verify (abstract-only).

- **Cohesion-vs-fragmentation regime.** Increased listening propensity, openness, and memory "enhance cohesion and reduce fragmentation" — a monotone qualitative claim across three parameters. The abstract uses phase-structure language ("sets the balance") but does not name a single control parameter, a critical value, or whether the transition is sharp or smooth (abstract-only).

- **Policy-flavored result.** Enforcing a minimum listening participation across all members suppresses excessive hub dominance — a leverage-point claim about an intervention on the model rather than on data (abstract-only).

- **What the abstract does NOT pin down** (relevant for the run's framing of feedback-loop dynamics and emergent hierarchy): the order parameter used to measure fragmentation; the named control parameter for the integration↔fragmentation regime boundary; the quantitative tradeoff at high listening propensity; the minimum-listening floor that suffices to prevent hub dominance; whether the model is calibrated against empirical data (PRE accepts pure model papers, and no empirical-fit claim is made).

- **Where this lands in the run's frame.** Direct hit on the run's working questions: a formal model of emergent hierarchy (Q-hierarchy) produced by a reinforcing feedback loop (Q-feedback), exhibiting an integration↔fragmentation polarity (Q-resilience/fragmentation), with an explicit policy lever (minimum listening floor) that suppresses hub dominance. The talker/listener asymmetry maps cleanly onto broadcast-vs-attend asymmetry in any hierarchical multi-agent communication structure, though the paper itself does not draw that bridge.
