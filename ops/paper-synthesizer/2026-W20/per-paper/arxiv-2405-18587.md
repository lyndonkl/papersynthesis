## Headline
A spiking-neuron model shows that two functionally distinct inhibitory subpopulations — one Hebbian (rate-controlling), one anti-Hebbian (selectivity-enforcing) — are sufficient for stable modular memory assemblies to self-organize and persist under continuous local plasticity, without any external homeostatic or control machinery.

## Summary
The authors compare three plasticity regimes (anti-Hebbian inhibitory only, Hebbian inhibitory only, and the mixed case) in a spiking QIF network trained on alternating selective stimuli, and find that only the mixed regime produces both a cleanly modular weight matrix and a biologically plausible asynchronous-irregular resting state — Hebbian inhibition forms local feedback E-I loops that hold firing rates in check, while anti-Hebbian inhibition forms lateral connections between assemblies that enforce winner-stays-up competition. After learning, the network settles into a low-rate irregular state punctuated by brief partial-synchrony events the authors interpret as spontaneous memory recalls, which they show coincide with positive intra-modular weight changes that counteract a slow forgetting term, and the paper argues these recalls "turn out to be fundamental for the long-term consolidation of the learned memories." Recovery experiments suggest that memory structure is preferentially carried by the inhibitory scaffold: when excitatory weights are randomised but the inhibitory backbone is preserved, the modules recover partially; when the inhibitory backbone is randomised, structure collapses within roughly 12 hours of simulated time.

## Specifics

- **Architecture and plasticity rules.** QIF spiking neurons with three coexisting STDP kernels: asymmetric Hebbian on excitatory→{E,I} synapses, symmetric Hebbian (Ricker wavelet) on one inhibitory subpopulation's outgoing synapses (motivated as PV-like, citing Refs. 42, 51, 52), and symmetric anti-Hebbian (reverse Mexican hat) on a second inhibitory subpopulation's outgoing synapses (motivated as SOM-like). Soft-bound weight saturation enforces Dale's principle by clipping weights to [0,1] for excitatory and [−1,0] for inhibitory presynaptic neurons.
- **The three-regime ablation (Fig. 1).** Anti-Hebbian only collapses into a pathological winner-takes-all with permanent silencing of one assembly; Hebbian only produces two disconnected feedback E-I loops with brief synchrony bursts; only the mixed case yields coupled-but-competing assemblies with the asynchronous-irregular signature.
- **Resting-state signatures (Fig. 2).** Inter-spike-interval CV ≈ 0.8–1.0 (Poisson-like), Kuramoto order parameter R ≈ 0.2 globally with transient bumps to ≈ 0.4 within a cluster during a recall event, firing-rate distribution peaking ≈ 2 Hz with an exponential tail to ≈ 20 Hz. The weight-change rate K(t) shows discrete positive spikes time-locked to recalls — net reinforcement, not depression.
- **Inhibitory-backbone finding (Fig. 3).** Three randomisation conditions over 24 hours simulated time: randomising excitatory weights only → partial recovery; randomising inhibitory weights only → full recovery (the surviving inhibitory scaffold allows the slower excitatory plasticity to rebuild from it); randomising inhibitory weights → structure collapses by t ≈ 12 h. The paper hedges this as "our analysis suggests that memory items can be encoded in different types of connections with different effects."
- **Capacity claim (Fig. 4).** Stability diagram gives M ≤ N_I / 2, with the apex at M* = N/3 ≈ 0.33N at a 33/66 E/I ratio. Extrapolated to mammalian brain regions (the paper labels this table "hypothetical memory capacity"): cortex (80/20) → 0.1N, hippocampus (90/10 or 95/5 in mouse) → 0.025–0.05N, amygdala (70/30 human / 60/40 mouse) → 0.15–0.2N. The extrapolation assumes each memory is encoded by a *triplet* (one excitatory + one Hebbian-inhibitory + one anti-Hebbian-inhibitory neuron) — an assumption the authors themselves flag as "rather implausible for biological neural networks" because losing one neuron would erase a memory.
- **Robustness at scale (Fig. 5).** N = 1000 neurons trained on M = 10 stimuli; weight matrices and intra- vs. inter-cluster weight means hold flat across 4 hours of simulated post-training time, with recalls remaining temporally separated (only one module recalled within ≈ 0.2 s windows).
- **Hub neurons under overlapping stimuli (Fig. 6).** When two stimuli share excitatory neurons during training, those shared neurons stabilise as hubs with mixed selectivity. The post-learning raster shows three event types — full P_1 recall (with hubs), full P_2 recall (without hubs), and hubs-alone events. The authors frame these hubs as "seeds for hierarchical organization," though the model develops only a single hub layer above two modules rather than a multi-level hierarchy.
- **What counts as "no control mechanism."** The paper's novelty claim is sufficiency of local STDP without homeostasis, weight normalisation, or BCM-style sliding thresholds (contrast with Refs. 31, 38, 50). The soft-bound clipping is framed as Dale's-principle enforcement rather than control; a skeptical reader could disagree with this definitional choice.
- **Forgetting term scaling.** The slow-decay constant *f* is set to f_0/M with f_0 = 0.2, so the forgetting/consolidation balance is tuned to the number of memories rather than parameter-free — acknowledged in the methods.
- **Hedging audit.** The abstract's phrasing that spontaneous recalls "turn out to be fundamental for the long-term consolidation" reads slightly stronger than the body, which preserves the "suggests" framing when generalising to biological brains. The model-internal sufficiency claim is well supported by Fig. 2's K(t) asymmetry and Fig. 3's recovery experiments.
