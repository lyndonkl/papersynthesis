## Headline
A mixed-methods survey of 73 Danish pig veterinarians, analysed with Grounded Theory and rendered as a systems-thinking causal map plus a Meadows-style iceberg model, identifies the structural and mental-model barriers behind antimicrobial-use reduction and surfaces specific leverage points — alongside an unintended substitution effect of legislation that only a systems lens makes visible.

## Summary
The paper argues that Danish pig veterinarians have largely adapted their antimicrobial-use practices over time (87% report doing so) and that the dominant driver of this adaptation has been *legislative restrictions* rather than autonomous professional change. It further argues that progress is bounded by financial constraints, a paucity of effective antimicrobial alternatives, and structural housing limitations veterinarians cannot themselves resolve, while a stable set of on-farm practices (gilt acclimatisation, all-in/all-out, feed quality, ventilation) continues to enable further reduction. A specific empirical observation — that legislative requirements have *unintentionally increased* aminoglycoside and macrolide usage — is offered as evidence that top-down regulation produces system-level side effects detectable only with a systems-thinking lens; the authors emphasise that veterinarians occupy a *pivotal* bridging role between regulatory frameworks and on-farm practice (abstract-only; "pivotal" is the abstract's strongest hedge and is rhetorical work a single-profession survey alone cannot fully license).

## Specifics

- **Design and sample.** Mixed-methods survey of Danish pig veterinarians, n=73, response rate 58%. Quantitative analysis tested the relationship between work experience and continued education against AMU practices; qualitative open-ended responses were coded with Grounded Theory (Glaser & Strauss lineage). The qualitative codes were then assembled into a *systems thinking causal map* and a *Meadows/Senge-style iceberg model* whose figures are the paper's central analytic artifacts (abstract-only; the figures' nodes and the iceberg's four-layer contents are not enumerated in the abstract).

- **Headline finding — adaptation is real but externally driven.** 87% of surveyed veterinarians report having adjusted their AMU practices over time; the abstract attributes this "predominantly" to legislative restrictions (Yellow Card scheme, VetStat / DDDvet-based monitoring are the relevant Danish-context machinery, not named in the abstract but load-bearing for the framing).

- **Named enabling practices (the "events / patterns" layer of the iceberg, inferred).** Gilt acclimatisation, all-in/all-out batch systems, improved feed quality, improved ventilation. These are the on-farm husbandry adjustments the responding veterinarians associate with continued AMU reduction.

- **Named barriers (span the "systemic structures" and "mental models" layers, inferred).** Financial constraints; lack of effective antimicrobial alternatives; housing-infrastructure limitations. The abstract presents these as *perceived* key barriers — preserve the hedge.

- **Named future-strategy leverage points (respondent-identified).** Cost-effective vaccines; better and faster diagnostic tools; implementation of eradication protocols. The abstract states respondents *emphasised the need for* these — preserve hedge. Whether the authors themselves taxonomise these against Meadows' 12-category leverage hierarchy is not determinable from the abstract; by inspection they sit roughly at the *parameter / information-flow / rules-of-the-system* levels rather than at the deeper *goals / paradigms* levels.

- **The unintended-consequence finding — the most policy-actionable result.** Legislative AMU requirements have *unintentionally* increased aminoglycoside and macrolide usage. The abstract gives no magnitude, no time series, no statistical test, and no mechanism (substitution? therapeutic-class switching? selection inside the Yellow Card scoring formula?). (abstract-only) This is the cleanest classical-systems-thinking exhibit in the paper: a balancing intervention at one node of the system producing a reinforcing effect at an adjacent node — visible only because the iceberg-model layer of *systemic structures* was made explicit.

- **What the iceberg model and systems map are *for*, in the authors' framing.** Bottom-up identification of leverage points for change in a socio-veterinary system where the front-line prescribers (veterinarians) have professional discretion but operate inside legislative, financial, and infrastructural constraints they did not design. This is a faithful application of the Meadows tradition: render the events, surface the patterns, expose the systemic structures, and name the mental models — then ask where the highest-leverage intervention sits.

- **Hedging audit.** Calibrated at the abstract level. Authors use "predominantly influenced by," "perceived key barriers," "emphasised the need for" — no causal overclaiming from the survey instrument. The closing recommendation that "future efforts should focus on enhancing the feasibility and effectiveness of AMU reduction strategies, ensuring they are supported by robust scientific evidence and feasibility analysis" is a generic policy peroration rather than a sharp recommendation, committing to no specific intervention or threshold.

- **Limitations (the operator should be aware of when reading this as a systems-thinking exemplar).** Single mixed-methods survey, single country, single profession (veterinarians, not farmers), n=73 with 42% non-response; the systems map and iceberg model are constructed from the responding 73 and the non-respondents may systematically differ. One author has a disclosed conflict of interest (agricultural advisory affiliation). The full text was not accessible — the iceberg model's four-layer contents and the systems map's nodes/edges are inferred rather than extracted (abstract-only).
