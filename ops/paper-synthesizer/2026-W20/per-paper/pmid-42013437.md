## Headline
A JMIR protocol argues that adoption of machine-learning clinical decision support is a *dynamic feedback phenomenon* rather than a static checklist of barriers, and proposes using system-dynamics group model building with hospital staff to produce a causal loop diagram and then a Vensim stock-and-flow simulation that surfaces intervenable "levers" (Meadows-lineage) on adoption.

## Summary
The authors anchor their motivation in a prior null-effect study at Duke (Bedoya 2019; O'Brien 2020): integrating an early warning score into the EHR at three sites "did not change rates of mortality or unanticipated intensive care unit transfer, even when the accuracy of the ML score indicated it was performing well," with frontline nurses ignoring the alert 86% of the time. They argue this kind of failure cannot be diagnosed by static facilitator/barrier inventories because the underlying structure is feedback-driven — perception → use → trust → use, with delays and nonlinearities — and that group model building (GMB), a participatory system-dynamics method, is the appropriate way to elicit that structure from the developers, implementers, and users who live it. The protocol commits to three sequential GMB sessions (variable elicitation, behavior-over-time graphing, causal loop diagram initiating-and-elaborating) at two Duke hospitals using different EWS variants, followed by translation of the qualitative causal loop diagram into a quantitative stock-and-flow simulation in Vensim whose adjustable parameters "will provide an environment to test the implementation of AI-enabled CDS more generally across health systems and identify intervenable levers." The clarity-checker audit on the extraction is preserved: this is a *programmatic* paper — it names the modelling apparatus but defers the concrete loops, stocks, equations, and leverage points to a future results publication, with hedges ("we are translating," "we will define," "we anticipate generating") used consistently throughout.

## Specifics

**Method (canonical Forrester / Sterman workflow, participatory front end):**
- Three GMB sessions, run over Zoom + Miro, following named Scriptapedia scripts: *Variable Elicitation*, *Graphs Over Time*, *Initiating and Elaborating a Causal Loop Diagram*.
- Recruitment: snowball sampling, with a minimum target of at least one developer and one implementer per EWS variant, and at least two users per variant. Session 1 enrolled 10 participants; DUH staff, DRH staff, and developers met separately to surface stratum-specific themes.
- Pre/post Weiner instruments (AIM / IAM / FIM, Weiner 2017) at four timepoints — pre-Session 1 and post each of the three sessions — used as a "proxy for assessing commitment to adopting a new tool."

**The intended modelling apparatus (named, but not yet populated):**
- Software: **Vensim** (Ventana Systems).
- Two stocks are named as examples: **"patient counts"** and **"adoption of CDS."** No specific flows are named, no equations given. The paper commits to *"define each stock and flow relationship with mathematical equations and capture delays, nonlinearities, and feedback effects … using Vensim's built-in functions."*
- Feedback loops are referred to in the abstract, never enumerated by name, polarity, or membership. The simulation will "quantify how feedback loops influence variables over time" and support parameter-sweep scenario analysis.

**Implicit feedback loops the paper describes qualitatively but does not draw:**
- A *reinforcing disengagement loop*: clinicians perceive overabundance of alerts → alert fatigue → ignore alerts → CDS is operationally inert → no observed outcome improvement → users perceive CDS as lacking utility → further disengagement. (Consistent with the 49%-96% override range in the alert-fatigue literature; the protocol does not sign or name the loop.)
- A second implicit loop, from the Use Case section: opacity of the underlying ML score → user perceives lack of validity → user does not act on alerts → no observed benefit → reinforces perception of lack of validity.
- One concrete causal *edge* (not a loop) was elicited in Session 2: participants identified *"quality of data input as a factor affecting use of the score."*

**The natural-experiment contrast (the study's hierarchical-organization angle):**
The two hospitals share the same Epic EHR since 2013 but run different EWS variants, generating within-system variation across five enumerated dimensions:
1. Input variables: NEWS uses 7, interpretable; Duke EWS uses 40+, opaque.
2. Workflow integration: Duke EWS fires continuously in real-time; NEWS fires only at shift change.
3. Target user (the hierarchy axis): NEWS is consumed by staff nurses; Duke EWS by charge nurses.
4. Information displayed: NEWS shows an overall score; Duke EWS a red/yellow/green tri-level risk category.
5. Interpretability: NEWS has no probabilistic interpretation; Duke EWS thresholds chosen by desired sensitivity/PPV.

**"Levers" (Meadows-lineage) — the framing the title carries:**
- The title and Discussion invoke "levers" / "intervenable levers" but do not tie the term to a specific Meadows-typology classification (parameter, feedback-loop strength, delay, paradigm). Leverage-point identification is positioned as a deliverable of the *future* simulation, not the protocol paper.
- No sensitivity-analysis protocol is described; no decision rule for what counts as a lever vs. an uninfluential parameter is given.

**Hedging preserved (per extraction's clarity audit):**
- "we will define," "we are translating," "we anticipate generating," "the resulting interactive simulation model will provide" — forward-tense throughout the modelling claims.
- The Conclusions assert *"these findings will have broader applicability beyond just EWSs"*, but the body's hedging is softer (*"could offer value," "approach could improve"*). Preserve the softer "could" when citing generalisability.

**Gaps the extraction's clarity-checker flags (carry these downstream):**
- The CLD itself is not in the published protocol — it is described as complete and validated (by an independent charge-nurse interview and a group interview with two non-participating nursing leaders) but reproduced only in a future companion results paper.
- The CLD → stock-and-flow translation procedure is underspecified: no rule for how arrow polarities become equation signs, how variables become stocks vs. flows vs. auxiliaries, or how Session 2's behaviour-over-time delays become integration time-constants.
- "Adoption of CDS" as a *stock* is conceptually unusual — Sterman's Bass-diffusion treatment models adoption as a *flow* between stocks of potential adopters and adopters. The protocol does not commit to a formulation.
- "Adoption" is not disambiguated between (a) using the score at all, (b) acting appropriately on a high-risk alert, and (c) not over-acting on low-risk alerts — these have different feedback structures and trade off via alert fatigue.

## Why this matters in this run's context

Outlier (applied systems-thinking practice): argues that adoption of an ML-driven decision system (nurses ignored the alert 86% of the time despite a well-performing model) is a feedback phenomenon, not a static checklist of barriers — directly analogous to "the orchestrator's downstream consumers ignore its outputs because the perception → use → trust → use loop has stalled," and proposes GMB plus a Vensim stock-and-flow simulation as the appropriate diagnostic apparatus.
