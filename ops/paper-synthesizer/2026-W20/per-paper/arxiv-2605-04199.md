## Headline
An 81-page review by core figures in the subfield argues that when the same units interact through multiple qualitatively distinct relation types — a multiplex — collective dynamics emerge that cannot be recovered from any single layer in isolation or from the aggregated weighted graph, and it identifies three structural/dynamical mechanisms that drive those phenomena.

## Summary
The review walks systematically through six dynamical-process families on multiplex networks — percolation, reaction–diffusion, synchronization, epidemic and social spreading, opinion dynamics, and evolutionary games — and in each one points to a concrete result where the layered architecture produces a phenomenon absent from any constituent layer or from any flattened-graph reduction (discontinuous cascading collapse, multiplex fast diffusion, Turing patterns in regimes where neither layer alone could host the instability, relay and inter-layer synchronization, awareness-shifted epidemic thresholds, and structurally induced cooperation). The authors then consolidate this evidence under a three-mechanism taxonomy: structural inter-layer correlations, dynamical interplay between inter- and intra-layer interactions, and dynamically correlated heterogeneous processes running on different layers. Hedging is calibrated — the framework "identifies" and "presents" mechanisms rather than proving them universal, and the Conclusions use "can be identified" — preserve that calibration when reading downstream.

## Specifics

**Framing claim (structure → behavior, but at the architectural level).** The paper's central existence claim is that there are collective phenomena which (a) do not appear on any single layer of the multiplex considered in isolation and (b) do not appear on the weighted aggregated single-layer graph obtained by collapsing the layers. The reviewers flag a definitional caveat: "aggregated" admits several constructions (binary OR, weighted sum, overlay, quotient), and not every multiplex phenomenon is tested against every aggregate — the strong claim is calibrated to the constructions actually compared.

**The three mechanisms (the taxonomy that travels).** The review's portable contribution for a reader bridging into hierarchical multi-agent orchestration:

- *Mechanism 1 — structural inter-layer correlations.* Edge overlap across layers, inter-layer degree correlations, and community alignment across layers systematically alter dynamical outcomes. Concretely: link overlap enables catastrophic cascading percolation; cross-layer degree assortativity controls cooperation in games; community-structure similarity across layers smooths the percolation transition.
- *Mechanism 2 — interplay between inter-layer and intra-layer interactions.* Tuning the relative weight of inter-layer vs intra-layer coupling (a single parameter, often called D, w, or η) produces sharp transitions: superdiffusion above an inter-layer coupling threshold D\*, explosive synchronization in duplex Kuramoto systems, layer-based localization in multimodal epidemic spreading.
- *Mechanism 3 — dynamically correlated heterogeneous processes.* When two different dynamics run on two layers and couple (Kuramoto + biased random walk; epidemic + awareness UAU-SIS; voter + disease), the coupled system can show explosive transitions, hysteresis, multistability, or cyclic cascade collapse that neither dynamics shows in isolation.

**Load-bearing quantitative anchors.**

- *Percolation (Fig. 7, p.17).* Two-layer Poisson multiplex: discontinuous transition at p_c⟨k⟩ ≃ 2.45541, with the mutually connected giant component σ̄⟨k⟩ jumping from 0 to ~1.25643 at criticality. Compare single-layer Poisson percolation: continuous, threshold at ⟨k⟩=1. The doubling of the threshold and the discontinuity are the canonical Buldyrev signature of a genuinely multiplex emergent behavior.
- *Diffusion (Fig. 9, p.23).* The supra-Laplacian's second-smallest eigenvalue λ₂ rises with inter-layer diffusion D in two regimes separated by D\*. Above D\*, λ₂ exceeds that of either single layer — multiplex fast diffusion. Heterogeneity in per-node interlayer constants drastically reduces λ₂ near D\*; removing even a small fraction of interlayer edges drops λ₂ sharply.
- *Cooperation in games (Fig. 25, p.54).* In multiplex weak prisoner's dilemma with ⟨k⟩=3, the cooperator fraction x_c remains substantial up to very large temptation T as M grows, opposite of the aggregated-graph behavior (which approaches well-mixed defection). The per-layer distribution of x_c is heterogeneous — layers diverge.
- *Public-goods game (Fig. 29, p.57).* Critical synergy R_c drops from 3.75 (isolated) to 3.25 (M=2 with maximal edge overlap). Without edge overlap, no enhancement — edge overlap is required for multiplex reciprocity.

**Cataloged failure modes (the part most relevant to a hierarchical-orchestrator reader).**

- *Discontinuous cascading collapse across coupled layers* (Buldyrev-type interdependent percolation): a hybrid first-order transition where macroscopic connectivity vanishes abruptly rather than continuously.
- *Multiplex Braess paradox / "slower is faster" failure* (Fig. 11, p.27): average travel time T can decrease with increasing inter-layer speed-ratio γ when storage buffers are bounded; increasing buffer size from 40 to 10³ can worsen performance.
- *Explosive synchronization induced by an inhibitory layer* (Fig. 16, p.37): a duplex of excitatory + inhibitory phase oscillators where neither layer in isolation supports a first-order transition; inter-layer coupling flips the excitatory layer from second-order to first-order with hysteresis.
- *Topological enslavement* (Fig. 27, p.55): as the number of layers M and inter-layer degree correlation rise, cooperation outcomes become determined by topology (hub aggregation across layers) rather than by game payoffs — a failure mode for "tune the payoff to control behavior" strategies in layered systems.
- *Shattered phase in coevolving multiplex voter model* (Fig. 32, p.61): emerges only when layers have asymmetric plasticity — a multiplex-specific failure mode of consensus dynamics.
- *Multimodal contagion regime shift* (Fig. 19, p.42): for inter-layer contagion ratio η≪1, dynamics is dominated by the layer with larger leading eigenvalue (decoupled behavior); as η grows, secondary susceptibility peaks decay and a single epidemic onset emerges.

**Hypotheses and design claims, preserved with the authors' calibration.**

1. *Existence claim* (defended throughout): there exist collective phenomena that emerge on multiplex networks and do not emerge on any single layer in isolation or on the weighted aggregated graph.
2. *Mechanism-1 claim*: patterns of correlation between layers systematically alter dynamical outcomes (the review cites edge overlap, inter-layer degree correlations, and cross-layer community structure as the concrete control parameters).
3. *Mechanism-2 claim*: relative weight of inter-layer vs intra-layer coupling produces sharp transitions, often crossing from continuous to discontinuous as the inter-layer parameter grows.
4. *Mechanism-3 claim*: coupling two different dynamics across layers can produce phenomena that neither dynamics exhibits in isolation.

**Named tools and concepts a reader should carry forward.**

- *Multiplex network*: N nodes and M layers, each layer an adjacency matrix over the same node set, with replica nodes matched one-to-one across layers.
- *Aggregated network*: single-layer projection (sum / OR / overlay / quotient); the null comparator for any "truly multiplex" claim.
- *Mutually connected giant component (MCGC)*: a node is in the MCGC iff it has a path to the MCGC in every layer it is coupled to. Self-consistently defined; replaces the single-layer notion of connectedness.
- *Supra-Laplacian L*: block-matrix Laplacian over MN nodes governing diffusion across the multiplex; λ₂ controls relaxation timescale.
- *Master Stability Function (MSF), extended to multiplex*: separates dynamics from topology in linear stability analysis of the synchronous manifold.
- *Reducibility (via quantum-Jensen-Shannon divergence on layer Laplacians)*: the principled answer to "when does a system need to be modeled as multiplex vs compressed?"
- *Edge overlap o*: fraction of layers on which a given pair (i,j) is connected; a single scalar that the review repeatedly identifies as a critical structural control.
- *Topological enslavement*: regime in heterogeneous-multiplex games where topology rather than payoffs determines outcomes.

**Scope limits the authors flag explicitly.**

- Higher-order interactions (hypergraphs, group decisions beyond dyadic) are out of scope — pointed to refs 541–542, 574–578 as future work. Real systems with quorum-style group decisions fall outside the framework as presented.
- Temporal-network dynamics is mentioned but not covered in depth.
- Section X (intertwining dynamics on fixed topologies) and Section XI (coevolution with co-evolving topology) are distinguished cleanly in principle but examples bleed across.
- Reducibility methods (Sec. III.E) pull against the rest of the review's thesis: many real multiplexes can be compressed without dynamical loss. The tension is acknowledged but not resolved — when is a system "truly multiplex enough" for these mechanisms to apply?
- Failure-mode coverage is uneven: percolation is rich in adversarial/Braess-style results; social-dynamics coverage is thinner on hostile / adversarial layer interactions (poisoning by a single adversarial layer, manipulation, etc., not covered).

**Why this is a bridge paper for a systems-thinking reader.** The review's three-mechanism taxonomy is a contemporary, mathematically explicit version of the classical claim that *structure determines behavior* — pushed past the single-layer formulation that dominated network science's first two decades. Each mechanism names a structural or dynamical primitive (cross-layer correlation; inter/intra coupling ratio; coupled heterogeneous processes) that is layer-agnostic in formulation and therefore portable to any layered-interaction setting where the same units participate in multiple kinds of interactions at once.
