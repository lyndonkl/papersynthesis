## Headline

Across nine urban rail networks, static topology alone predicts most of the variance in how badly a network fails *and* how fast it recovers — with modularity emerging as the strongest single predictor of fragility under targeted attack, and average degree the strongest predictor of recovery speed.

## Summary

The paper benchmarks five centrality-based dismantling strategies (Domirank, Betweenness, Degree, Closeness, Eigenvector) plus random failure and a geography-ordered flooding scenario across nine subway networks (N = 91 to 463 nodes), measuring robustness as the area under the giant-connected-component (GCC) curve during sequential node removal and recovery. It reports that Domirank-based attacks are *in general* the most damaging and that Domirank- and Betweenness-based recovery orderings produce the fastest GCC regrowth, with Kendall-tau correlations on the nine cities pointing to modularity (τ = -0.65 vs Domirank attack, -0.64 vs flooding) as the dominant fragility driver and average degree (R² up to 0.81 vs Betweenness recovery) as the dominant recovery driver. The authors hedge that larger networks "indicate" both greater vulnerability and faster recovery, attributing the latter to "redundancy and connectivity" — but the analysis is correlational on N = 9, with no partial-correlation control for the size/degree confound.

## Specifics

**Methodology**
- Nine networks: Washington DC, Boston, Chicago, Delhi, Tokyo, Paris, Shanghai, London, New York; static topological inputs in Table 1 (⟨k⟩ 2.02-2.40; ⟨L⟩ 10.64-19.69; modularity 0.76-0.88; clustering 0.0000-0.0294 — rail networks are nearly tree-like).
- Failure phase: sequential removal of nodes (SRN) ordered by each centrality measure (deterministic single sequence), plus random failure (20-trial ensemble mean) and a first-order flooding model (stations ranked by river proximity, removed in order).
- Recovery phase: re-add nodes in centrality-priority order; recovery score = AUC of GCC regrowth.
- Static topology → resilience link: Kendall-tau on nine paired city-level observations across seven attributes × six strategies (42 reported correlations, no p-values, no multiple-comparison adjustment).

**Headline numbers (preserved with hedging)**
- Failure: Domirank attack yields the lowest GCC-AUC in 7 of 9 cities (Boston A_DMC = 0.07 lowest; Tokyo and Chicago are the exceptions where Betweenness or Degree "proved to be the most effective"). Boxplot median Domirank failure-AUC ~0.05-0.09; Eigenvector/Closeness highest.
- Recovery: Domirank-priority recovery yields the highest GCC-AUC in every city (Paris R_DMC = 0.80 maximum); Boston is the only city where Betweenness recovery edges out Domirank (R_BC = 0.60 vs R_DMC = 0.51).
- Critical-fraction claim: removing ~10-30% of the most-critical nodes causes loss of GCC integrity across all nine networks.
- Flooding "behaves similar to a localized random failure" — F_F clusters near F_R in the boxplot; Chicago L worst, Tokyo most resilient.

**Topological-determinants claims (Kendall-tau on N = 9 cities; directional only)**
- **Modularity index** is the strongest single predictor of fragility under targeted Domirank attack (τ = -0.65) and flooding (τ = -0.64); near-zero correlation with recovery (τ = -0.06 to -0.14). More-modular networks are more attackable — cutting inter-module bridges disconnects modules.
- **Average degree**: weak correlation with Domirank-attack failure (τ = +0.09), strong positive correlation with recovery across all three best recovery strategies (τ = +0.74 / +0.73 / +0.82; R² up to 0.81 under Betweenness recovery — the single strongest reported relationship).
- **Average shortest path length**: negatively correlates with both robustness *and* recovery (τ = -0.51 to -0.77 failure; -0.34 to -0.44 recovery) — longer paths hurt in both phases.
- **Clustering coefficient**: positively correlates with recovery (τ = +0.20 / +0.47 / +0.29) but *negatively* with failure-phase robustness under Domirank attack (τ = -0.43). The paper foregrounds the recovery-positive half and underplays the failure-negative half — a within-paper tension on whether more clustering is a net win.
- **Network size (N)**: τ = -0.39 vs Domirank attack, +0.29 vs Domirank recovery — supports the "larger more vulnerable, larger recovers faster" hedge, but N and ⟨k⟩ are themselves correlated across the nine cities, and the paper runs no partial correlation.

**Hedging and limitations worth carrying forward (the paper's own + audit findings)**
- Authors' language stays appropriately cautious — "indicate," "consistently influence," "suggests" — but Figure 6's color-coded heatmap renders nine-point correlations with the visual confidence of large-N data; the words preserve uncertainty, the figure does not.
- Random/flooding curves use 20-trial ensemble means; deterministic centrality curves are single sequences with no variability shading. Close ranks (e.g., A_DMC = 0.07 vs A_BC = 0.09 for Boston) cannot be assessed against sequence noise.
- The recovery model re-adds nodes in centrality-priority order — i.e., it describes an *optimal repair-prioritization policy*, not observed real-world recovery dynamics (which depend on damage geography, crew logistics, parts availability). Not flagged as a model-choice limitation.
- Flooding model is first-order (proximity ranking only); no hydrology, no correlated multi-station spatial events.
- Modularity finding is muted in the Discussion ("varies with context") despite being the strongest single fragility signal — inverse of the clustering issue.

**Why this matters for the run's framing**
This is a clean empirical instantiation of cascading-failure and network-resilience theory on real infrastructure: it puts numbers on *which* static topological invariants (modularity, average degree, path length, clustering) govern the failure-vs-recovery tradeoff, and it surfaces a substantive tension — clustering helps recovery but hurts failure-phase robustness under targeted attack — that any modular hierarchical system has to confront. The N = 9 statistical foundation means the directional findings travel further than the magnitudes.
