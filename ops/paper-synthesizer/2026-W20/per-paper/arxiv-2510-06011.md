## Headline
The paper argues that abrupt size-dependent changes in collective behavior — "critical numerosities" — commonly arise from competing feedbacks that scale differently with the number of constituents N, and that a one-line modification to standard bifurcation analysis (balance b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) against d<sub>n</sub>(x) instead of b<sub>n</sub>(x) against d<sub>n</sub>(x)) locates them where the standard analysis cannot.

## Summary
Building on the exact stationary distribution of any 1-D birth–death Markov chain, the authors define a critical numerosity as an integer n at which the number of stable equilibria of b<sub>n</sub>(x-1) − d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 changes, and use this to unify "discreteness-induced" and "noise-induced" transitions as instances of the same phenomenon. They defend the definition by reworking four collective-behavior models — Föllmer–Kirman ant path selection, Beekman–Ratnieks–Sumpter pharaoh-ant trails, Amé et al. cockroach shelter selection, and Pacala–Gordon–Godfray task allocation — and recover closed-form n<sub>c</sub> in each, including a case (Pacala) where standard ODE bifurcation analysis predicts no transition at all. The methodological pay-off, hedged carefully, is that ODE approximations of stochastic models can fail near these critical numbers with non-monotonic error in n, and that competing published models of the same phenomenon can predict opposite critical-numerosity behavior — suggesting an experimental lever for model disambiguation.

## Specifics

- **Core construct (Eq. 4).** Critical numerosity: 𝒩<sub>c</sub> = {n ≥ 2 : P(n) ≠ P(n−1)}, where P(n) counts stable equilibria of b<sub>n</sub>(x−1) − d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 on [1, n−1]. The shift from b<sub>n</sub>(x) − d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 (standard) to b<sub>n</sub>(x−1) − d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 (modified) falls out of the exact stationary distribution π<sub>n</sub>(x) = π<sub>n</sub>(0)·∏<sub>y=1</sub><sup>x</sup> b<sub>n</sub>(y−1)/d<sub>n</sub>(y) — not an approximation. Fig. 2 carries the whole methodological contribution.

- **Mechanism claim (hedged "commonly arise").** In each of the four worked models, the critical numerosity emerges because two feedbacks scale differently with n — e.g., in Föllmer–Kirman, spontaneous switching is O(1) per individual while recruitment is O(n)/(n−1), so their balance crosses as n grows. The paper exhibits this decomposition case by case; it does not prove a general theorem of the form "any (b<sub>n</sub>, d<sub>n</sub>) with differential scaling has an n<sub>c</sub>." The unifying interpretation is descriptive, not predictive.

- **System-specific critical-numerosity formulas (no universal threshold).** The paper is explicit that there is no single N at which "emergence kicks in"; n<sub>c</sub> is set by ratios of competing rates and an exponent depending on how feedbacks scale:
  - Föllmer–Kirman ant path selection: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ r/s + 1 (Fig. 3, r=1, s=0.02 ⇒ n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 51).
  - Beekman–Ratnieks–Sumpter pharaoh-ant trails: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 2√(s/r) − q/r (Fig. 4 ⇒ n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 50).
  - Amé et al. cockroach shelter selection: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 2√k.
  - Pacala–Gordon–Godfray task allocation: n<sub>c</sub> ≈ (s/min{f<sub>1</sub>, f<sub>2</sub>})<sup>1/α</sup> (Fig. 5 ⇒ n<sub>c</sub> = 50; Fig. 6 ⇒ n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 47).
  - Pavement-ant foraging (Pasteels 1987 vs Deneubourg 1990, re-analyzed): n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 200–230 (numerical, Fig. 7).
  - Referenced empirical anchors not re-derived: ice nucleation "roughly 90 or more" water molecules; pharaoh-ant pheromone trails 700 forage vs ≤600 do not; fire-ant rafts "10 or more."

- **Failure-mode result for ODE limits (Pacala case).** Standard bifurcation analysis applied to the ODE limit of the Pacala model predicts one equilibrium at f<sub>1</sub>n/(f<sub>1</sub>+f<sub>2</sub>) for every n — no transition. The modified analysis recovers n<sub>c</sub> = 50. Fig. 6 plots |μ̂<sub>n</sub> − μ<sub>n</sub>| (absolute error between ODE prediction and stochastic mean) vs n and shows a sharp non-monotonic spike at n<sub>c</sub> ≈ 47 returning toward zero on either side, refuting any "ODE gets monotonically better as n grows" intuition. The y-axis units in the rendered figure are not clearly labeled — flag.

- **Model-disambiguation result (Fig. 7).** For pavement-ant foraging, the Pasteels-1987 and Deneubourg-1990 models both predict a parallel→alternate split at n ≈ 200–230, while the Föllmer–Kirman framing predicts the opposite ordering. The paper is candid (footnote 2, p. 13) that no published experiment confirms which is correct; the claim is *that critical numerosities could disambiguate models given an experiment*, not that they have.

- **Scope honesty.** The 1-D birth–death case is complete; higher-dimensional collective behaviors (schooling polarization, midge swarms) are gestured at via three candidate routes (complex-balanced CRNs, Fokker–Planck, van Kampen system-size expansion) and explicitly deferred to future work. The schooling-fish row of Fig. 1, which motivates the introduction, is not actually analyzed.

- **Load-bearing assumption defended by example, not theorem.** The link between stable equilibria of b<sub>n</sub>(x−1) − d<sub>n</sub>(x) = 0 and modes of the stationary distribution π<sub>n</sub>(x) is asserted at the start of §2.3 and used in Appendix A.1, but no theorem statement bounds the error. This is the paper's most consequential assumption.

- **Conceptual distinction the paper draws.** Critical-numerosity transitions are not density-dependent (Vicsek/Cates–Tailleur-style) phase transitions; they are *number-dependent* and persist even when density and interaction strength are held fixed. The paper anticipates and answers this objection in §5.

- **Why this matters for systems thinking applied to multi-agent collectives.** The paper is a quantitative sharpening of Anderson's 1972 "More Is Different": it converts *that* emergence occurs into *at what n*, with closed-form expressions in terms of the system's competing feedback rates. The methodological warning — deterministic approximations can mislead near critical numbers, and the error need not shrink monotonically with N — is the operationally consequential half of the paper for anyone modeling collectives with mean-field ODEs.
