## Headline
A pre/post evaluation of System Dynamics Group Model Building with 32 community stakeholders found no statistically significant overall gain in systems-thinking quiz scores, with hedged subgroup-level increases that "appeared" among younger participants, those with lived experience, nonprofit staff, and those without prior SD GMB exposure (abstract-only).

## Summary
The paper takes a Forrester/Sterman-lineage participatory modeling method — System Dynamics Group Model Building (SD GMB), framed here as a CBPR-grounded approach — and asks whether co-building a shared model of a community problem with non-modeler stakeholders measurably expands those stakeholders' systems-thinking competence (abstract-only). The supporting evidence is a pre/post assessment of 32 stakeholders using a validated systems thinking scale plus custom knowledge assessments; the baseline mean score was "nearly 45%" and the pre-to-post change "did not demonstrate a statistically significant difference" at the overall-sample level (abstract-only). Subgroup-level increases "appeared" in four named subgroups, and the abstract preserves that hedging rather than promoting the signal to a significance claim (abstract-only).

## Specifics

- **Method (named): System Dynamics Group Model Building (SD GMB)** — the participatory variant of Forrester/Sterman SD developed in the Andersen / Richardson / Vennix lineage, in which a facilitation team leads stakeholders through scripted exercises to co-produce a shared system model. The paper frames SD GMB as "a CBPR-grounded approach" (abstract-only).
- **Sample.** n = 32 community-based stakeholders (abstract-only).
- **Instrument.** A validated systems thinking scale plus custom knowledge assessments; the specific scale name and the custom-item content are not given at the abstract level (abstract-only).
- **Baseline result.** Mean quiz score "nearly 45%" (abstract-only).
- **Primary result (overall).** "Did not demonstrate a statistically significant difference" pre-to-post on the systems-thinking quiz (abstract-only). Hedging preserved: this is a null primary result, not a negative one — the abstract does not quantify the minimum detectable effect, and n = 32 leaves room for an underpowered null.
- **Secondary result (subgroups).** Increases "appeared among" (a) younger participants, (b) those with lived experience, (c) nonprofit staff, and (d) those without prior SD GMB experience (abstract-only). Pre-specification status of the subgroup analysis and any multiple-comparisons correction are not stated at the abstract level.
- **What the paper does NOT report at the abstract level** (load-bearing gaps for the operator's classical-systems-thinking interest):
  - The concrete GMB protocol — number of sessions, session sequence, whether published "scripts" (e.g., Hovmand's *Scriptapedia*) were used, role assignment (modeler / facilitator / recorder / gatekeeper).
  - The artifacts produced — whether the sessions stopped at a causal loop diagram (CLD) or progressed to a stock-and-flow diagram (the computationally simulable artifact the Forrester/Sterman tradition treats as load-bearing).
  - Mechanism of (non-)transfer — whether dose was too small, whether stakeholders learned the *content* of the problem without internalizing systems-thinking *competence*, or whether the quiz instrument failed to detect a differently-shaped change.
- **Funding / access.** NIH grant R01 MD017596 (NIMHD). Unpaywall reports `is_oa: false`; no PMC id exists at extraction time, so the concrete GMB protocol described in the methods section is gated behind the paywall and could not be recovered for this synthesis.
- **Positioning for this run.** The paper is one of the rarer empirical tests of the long-standing capacity-transfer claim in the participatory-SD literature — proponents have asserted for decades that GMB engagement transfers systems-thinking competence to lay participants, but measured cognitive outcomes are uncommon. The null-with-hedged-subgroup finding is itself a useful corrective to the often-assumed transfer claim, even though the concrete GMB step-and-artifact detail the operator's brief calls for is not extractable at the abstract level.
