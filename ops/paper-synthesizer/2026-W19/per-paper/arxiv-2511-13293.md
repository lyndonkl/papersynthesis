## Headline
GHAR proposes a two-agent hierarchical RAG architecture for clinical prediction in which a "primary physician" agent decides when to retrieve over a biomedical knowledge graph and a "consulting" agent summarizes the retrieved subgraph, with both agents jointly optimized under a single multi-agent MDP.

## Summary
The authors argue that prior healthcare RAG systems fail in two specific ways — they retrieve indiscriminately (MedRAG, KARE) and they train retriever and generator in isolation, which the paper calls an "alignment gap" and a "loss-in-the-middle" failure. GHAR's response is to split the work between an Agent-Top that adaptively chooses between parametric knowledge and retrieval along a selected meta-path partition of PrimeKG, and an Agent-Low that summarizes the retrieved subgraph into reasoning history; both share a Qwen2.5-3B backbone (LoRA rank 8) and are co-trained with multi-agent PPO under a composite reward (cost, outcome-supervised match, and pairwise rank against negative reasoning paths). On three MIMIC-style datasets across three tasks, GHAR is reported to outperform 15 baselines on point estimates, with the authors stating the results "demonstrate the superiority" of the approach — a claim the extraction flags as moderately overclaimed because no confidence intervals, multi-seed runs, or significance tests are reported and several headline gaps are within plausible run-to-run variance.

## Specifics
- **Named system and components.** GHAR = Generative Hierarchical Agentic Retrieval. Two role-specialized agents share one LLM backbone: **Agent-Top** ("primary physician") decides whether to invoke parametric knowledge or trigger retrieval, and over which meta-path partition of the KG; **Agent-Low** ("consulting service") performs fine-grained retrieval over the selected subgraph and summarizes it into an intermediate answer fed back as reasoning history.
- **Formalization.** Joint optimization under one multi-agent MDP `⟨Sᵀ×Sᴸ, Aᵀ×Aᴸ, P, Rᵀ⊕Rᴸ⟩` with multi-agent PPO. Composite reward: cost penalty + outcome-supervised reward model (ORM, recipe imitated from DeepSeek-R1) + pairwise rank reward against negative reasoning paths. Optimal ORM weight `η=5` and `|Ō_meta|=3` (tuned on MIMIC-III only — the extraction flags it is unclear whether `η` was re-tuned for MIMIC-IV / eICU).
- **Retrieval surface.** Heterogeneous KG corpus is **PrimeKG**, partitioned by **meta-paths** (ordered node-type / edge-type sequences). Retrieval substrate is **E5 + FAISS**. The paper notes relationships are "primarily sourced from PrimeKG" but does not describe the MIMIC ICD / RxNorm → PrimeKG entity-linking step.
- **Backbone.** Qwen2.5-3B with LoRA rank 8, shared between both agents.
- **Datasets and tasks.** MIMIC-III (33,507 patients), MIMIC-IV (46,178), eICU (8,601). Three tasks: 24h-Decompensation (DEC, binary), Readmission (READ, binary within κ=15 days), Length-of-Stay (LOS, 10-bin multi-class). Splits 6:2:2 via Pyhealth.
- **Headline numbers vs strongest baseline (best per cell).**
  - MIMIC-III: DEC B-Acc 0.8421 vs KARE 0.8114 (+3.1pp); READ B-Acc 0.6272 vs SHAPE 0.6074 (+2.0pp); LOS F1 0.4124 vs Medical-SFT 0.3957 (+1.7pp).
  - MIMIC-IV: DEC B-Acc 0.8279 vs Medical-SFT 0.8110 (+1.7pp); READ B-Acc 0.6484 vs KARE 0.6446 (+0.4pp); LOS F1 0.3678 vs SHAPE 0.3598 (+0.8pp).
  - eICU: DEC B-Acc 0.5990 vs StageNet 0.5294 (≈+7pp); READ B-Acc 0.5908 vs UDC 0.5517 (+3.9pp); LOS F1 0.2610 vs UDC 0.2595 (+0.15pp).
- **Ablation evidence (MIMIC-III, Table VII).** Largest drops come from removing meta-path partitioning (`-NM`, ~4pp B-Acc on DEC) and from removing the shared cross-agent reward (`-NS`, ~3pp across tasks). Removing iteration (`-NI`), Agent-Top (`-NT`), or Agent-Low (`-NL`) each also reduces every metric — this is the strongest internal evidence for the design split.
- **Generalization claims (preserve hedging).** Cross-task and cross-dataset OOD experiments (Fig. 6) report GHAR retains its lead over KARE / Medical-SFT / SHAPE / UDC. Group-wise analysis (Fig. 7) "highlights" an advantage on rare-disease group G1, framed as a fairness signal — the extraction notes no statistical test is reported. On MMLU-Clinical QA and MIMIC-IV-Ext-BHC summarization (Table VIII), GHAR reports ACC 0.7167 vs LightRAG 0.6913, and best ROUGE-1 0.3090 / SARI 0.4307 on summary metrics.
- **Robustness to substitutions.** Swapping E5 for BGE-M3 or Clinical-BERT, or scaling Qwen2.5-3B (rank 8) to rank 16 / BioMistral-7B, yields no significant gain. The authors interpret this as robustness; an alternative read (flagged in extraction) is that the approach is bottlenecked by something other than retriever or backbone quality.
- **Efficiency claim.** Fig. 8 reports lower per-epoch inference time than Search-R1 / MedRAG / KARE, attributed to the adaptive trigger and meta-path scoping — though the comparison conflates these algorithmic choices with Ray distributed inference and async serving.
- **What the paper does not address.** No prospective deployment / clinician evaluation, no calibration analysis of generative predictions, no safety / refusal behavior analysis of the generated rationales, and no costing of meta-path construction when porting to a new domain.
- **Hedging audit.** The paper's verb is "demonstrate the superiority" / "highlighting the potential." Preserved verbatim. The absence of CIs, multi-seed runs, and significance tests means the appropriate downstream phrasing is "consistently outperforms on point estimates" rather than "shows."

## Why this matters in this run's context
Sits in the established "graph-structured retrieval substrates as the agent's primitive" sub-theme as the run's clearest example of co-training the agent's retrieval-trigger policy and its KG-summarization policy under one MDP — the ablation isolates meta-path partitioning and shared cross-agent reward as the load-bearing components.
