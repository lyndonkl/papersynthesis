## Headline
A controlled bake-off of five retrieval strategies inside one biomedical RAG pipeline finds Cross-Encoder Reranking on top by composite score (0.827) but only 0.005 ahead of plain Dense Vector Search, and reports that Multi-Query Expansion *hurts* contextual precision (0.671) — the authors say this "suggests naive query diversification introduces retrieval noise."

## Summary
The paper holds generator (GPT-4o-mini), embeddings (`text-embedding-3-small`), vector store (ChromaDB), chunking (512 tokens / 50 overlap), and top-k (10) fixed, then varies only the retrieval strategy across Dense, Hybrid BM25+Dense, Cross-Encoder Reranking, Multi-Query Expansion, and MMR on the `rag-mini-bioasq` subset (40,181 passages, 250 QA pairs). It evaluates each strategy on DeepEval's four metrics (contextual precision, contextual recall, faithfulness, answer relevancy) with 95% confidence intervals. The headline argument is that retrieval *complexity* does not monotonically convert into retrieval *quality* at this corpus scale: Cross-Encoder Reranking wins on composite and precision, the authors saying this is "confirming that query-document interaction yields measurable retrieval gains" — but Dense leads on recall (0.887), and the two strategies designed to beat Dense (Hybrid, Multi-Query) finish below it.

## Specifics

**Pipeline held constant.** Generator: `gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18` (T=0.1, max_tokens=500). Judge: same model (T=0.0) — the authors flag this circularity as a limitation. Embeddings: OpenAI `text-embedding-3-small` (1,536-dim). Vector store: ChromaDB cosine k-NN. Chunking: 512 tokens / 50-token overlap. Universal top-k=10. Seed=42. n=250 QA pairs from `enelpol/rag-mini-bioasq`.

**Five strategies compared (composite scores, n=250, 95% CIs in Table 2):**
- **Cross-Encoder Reranking** (`cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2` over a 30-candidate dense pool): **composite 0.827**, contextual precision **0.852 [0.818, 0.885]**, recall 0.852, faithfulness 0.902, answer relevancy 0.701. Top on composite and precision.
- **Dense Vector Search** (bi-encoder baseline): composite 0.822, precision 0.809, **recall 0.887** (best of any RAG strategy), faithfulness 0.897, relevancy 0.695. The 0.005 composite gap to Cross-Encoder is well within noise.
- **Hybrid BM25+Dense** (LangChain EnsembleRetriever via RRF, fixed 0.4/0.6 weights): composite 0.820, precision 0.817, recall 0.874. Authors attribute the miss to "fixed weights mistuned for the dataset's synthesis-heavy queries" — they note "weight optimization or query-adaptive fusion could change this outcome noticeably."
- **Multi-Query Expansion** (3 GPT-4o-mini paraphrases + cross-variant frequency dedup): composite **0.772**, precision **0.671 [0.630, 0.709]** — far outside every other strategy's CI. Authors frame this as "suggesting naive query diversification introduces retrieval noise."
- **MMR** (λ=0.5, LangChain inverted convention noted): composite 0.787, recall **0.775** (lowest among RAG), relevancy **0.658** (lowest among RAG). Authors say MMR "sacrifices answer relevancy for diversity."
- **No-context ablation** (lower bound): composite 0.316, answer relevancy 0.287 vs. 0.658-0.701 with retrieval — confirming retrieval, not parametric knowledge, drives answer quality in this setting.

**Where the LLM-utility framing lives.** The composite is the unweighted mean of four DeepEval metrics — three of which (precision, recall, faithfulness) are retrieval/grounding signals and one (answer relevancy) is downstream LLM utility. The paper does not separate "good retrieval" from "good answers" — it scores them together and treats the composite as the decision variable. This matters for the run's topic: the evaluation target is already downstream LLM utility, not classical IR relevance only.

**What the authors say and don't say.**
- The Cross-Encoder precision gap over Dense (0.852 vs. 0.809) sits on the CI boundary (Dense upper bound 0.846 overlaps Cross-Encoder lower bound 0.818 by ~0.028), so the strongest claim is technically true but the practical lift is marginal. Section 6.5 acknowledges this when it advises Dense for recall-critical use.
- The Multi-Query precision collapse (0.671) is robust — its CI does not overlap any other strategy's CI.
- Faithfulness is essentially flat across all RAG strategies (range 0.009, 0.893-0.902); the paper attributes this to a strong grounding prompt ("If the documents don't contain enough information, say 'Insufficient evidence in provided sources'") rather than to retrieval choice.
- The Hybrid and Multi-Query negative results are single-hyperparameter results (0.4/0.6 BM25 weight; 3 variants). Authors flag this; they do not bound the upside of tuning.
- No latency or cost-per-quality Pareto is reported. Cross-Encoder adds a per-query model call; Multi-Query triples LLM calls. Composite-score ranking elides this axis.
- No question-type slicing (factoid vs. synthesis vs. list); the "synthesis-heavy queries" framing used to explain Hybrid's miss is plausible but not directly evidenced.

**Agent angle (relevant to this run's topic).** This is not an agentic-RAG paper — there is no multi-step retrieval, no tool-use, no agent memory. It is a single-turn QA pipeline. Its relevance to the run's topic is as a *primitive-level diagnostic*: it benchmarks the standard menu of retrieval primitives (dense, hybrid, cross-encoder rerank, query expansion, MMR) that an LLM agent would compose, and gives a per-primitive precision/recall/relevancy decomposition with CIs. The Multi-Query result is the most agent-relevant finding — it pushes back on the common agent pattern of "have the LLM rewrite the query a few times and union the hits."
