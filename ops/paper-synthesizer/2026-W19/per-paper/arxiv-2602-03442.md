## Headline
A-RAG reframes retrieval for LLM agents as three hierarchical tools — keyword search, semantic search, and chunk read — and reports that letting the agent itself orchestrate those tools beats graph-RAG and workflow-RAG baselines on multi-hop QA at comparable or fewer retrieved tokens.

## Summary
The paper argues that existing RAG falls into two non-agentic camps — single-shot retrieve-then-concatenate pipelines and predefined multi-step workflows — and that the better move is to expose retrieval to the model as hierarchical tools at multiple granularities so the agent decides what to search, when, and how deep to read. A-RAG operationalizes this with three tools (`keyword_search`, `semantic_search`, `chunk_read`) over a two-stage lightweight index (1000-token chunks, sentence-level dense embeddings, query-time keyword matching with no inverted index), driven by a minimal ReAct-style loop and a context tracker that suppresses re-reads. On four multi-hop QA datasets (HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, MuSiQue, GraphRAG-Bench) under a unified setup with Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B and GPT-5-mini-as-judge, A-RAG (Full) is reported to "consistently outperform" graph-RAG and workflow-RAG baselines — beating every baseline on all 5 columns with GPT-5-mini and 3 of 5 with GPT-4o-mini — while retrieving comparable or fewer tokens than baselines at the Full setting.

## Specifics

- **Three hierarchical tools.** `keyword_search` (exact match, 1-3 word constraint), `semantic_search` (Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B over sentence-level vectors, top-k=5), and `chunk_read` (fetches the full 1000-token chunk a snippet came from). The tool descriptions handed to the agent (Figures 7-8) carry strong inductive biases ("keywords MUST be 1-3 words"; "you MUST use chunk_read").
- **Agent loop.** A deliberately minimal ReAct-style loop (Appendix C pseudocode): at each step the model emits a reasoning thought + tool call or a final answer. A *context tracker* records which chunk IDs have been read; re-reads return a zero-token "already read" notification.
- **Headline numbers (GPT-5-mini, LLM-Acc).** MuSiQue 74.1 vs next-best LinearRAG 62.4; HotpotQA 94.5 vs GraphRAG 82.5. A-RAG (Full) is bold-best on all 10 GPT-5-mini cells and 8 of 10 GPT-4o-mini cells in Table 1; margins are often 5-15 points.
- **Token efficiency (Table 3, GPT-5-mini).** A-RAG (Full) uses fewer retrieved tokens than baselines on HotpotQA (2,737 vs Naive RAG 5,358) and 2Wiki (2,930), comparable on MuSiQue (5,663) and Novel (6,087), and *higher* than the cheapest baselines on Medical (7,678). Within-paradigm, A-RAG (Full) uses 5-10× fewer tokens than A-RAG (Naive) (MuSiQue 5,663 vs 56,360) — the more dramatic efficiency claim is the within-paradigm number, not the cross-paradigm one.
- **Test-time scaling (Figure 4, n=300, single seed implied).** Max-iteration sweep 5→20 lifts GPT-5-mini from ~70 to ~78 on MuSiQue-300 while GPT-4o-mini saturates near 47 — suggests longer horizons help capable reasoners more than weak ones. Reasoning-effort sweep (minimal→high) lifts GPT-5-mini and GPT-5 by ~25 points, framed as evidence the framework "scales alongside model capability."
- **Where hierarchy actually pays.** Ablations (Table 2) show removing Semantic Search hurts most on MuSiQue (74.1→69.4), removing Keyword Search hurts on HotpotQA (94.5→93.0), removing Chunk Read (snippets-only) is the smallest hit — suggesting chunk_read is more a precision/efficiency play than an accuracy lever. Notably, Naive A-RAG (single embedding tool) already beats most baselines, so much of the headline gain is attributed to the *agentic* shift rather than the *hierarchy*; the hierarchy adds a further increment that is "more pronounced when switching to GPT-5-mini."
- **Failure-mode shift (Figure 5, n=100 per dataset, single annotator).** Naive RAG's failures are ~50% retrieval-bound; A-RAG's primary failure breakdown on MuSiQue is Reasoning Chain Error 82%, Judge Error 9%, Corpus Missing 6%, Model Gave Up 3%. The diagnostic claim: failure has shifted from "couldn't find documents" to "found documents, reasoned wrong."
- **Three principles of agentic autonomy (Appendix A, Table 4).** Autonomous Strategy, Iterative Execution, Interleaved Tool Use; A-RAG is the only one of 17 listed methods to satisfy all three. The framework is author-constructed and was designed around A-RAG, so the uniqueness claim is partly definitional.
- **Hedging preserved.** Abstract-level claim is "consistently outperforms existing approaches with comparable or lower retrieved tokens" — assertive but not quantified at the abstract level; "comparable or lower" (not strictly lower). Test-time scaling is *framed as* evidence the framework "scales alongside model capability" — that framing borrows OpenAI/DeepSeek's test-time-scaling terminology while the actual lever is max-iteration count.
- **Notable absences.** No comparison to RL-trained agentic-RAG baselines (Search-R1, R1-Searcher, Graph-R1) despite them being the natural "trained vs prompted" comparison; no seed disclosure or error bars in the main results table; the keyword tool runs O(N) exact match at query time without an inverted index and no indexing/retrieval latencies are reported, so the engineering-scalability claim is undertested.
