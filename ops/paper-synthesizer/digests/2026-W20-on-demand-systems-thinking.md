# Classical Systems Thinking, Reframed for Your Hierarchical Orchestrator (W20, 24-month window)

## Headline

Across 25 papers spanning multi-agent systems, network science, cybernetics, neuroscience, and applied causal-loop practice, one through-line holds: in collectives of interacting individuals, the leverage points are not the loud nodes (the hubs, the loudest agents, the strongest signals) but the *structural shape of the interaction graph* and the *feedback loops on a single per-agent asymmetry*. For your MapReduce-style orchestrator, that means the runtime signals worth instrumenting are graph-topological (modularity, edge overlap across coordination layers, clique-vs-1D structure of the lane-to-lane interaction) and dynamical (the loop that ties realized lane-fitness back to lane respawn / branching factor / barrier policy). The clusters disagree on one substantive point — whether hardening should be applied uniformly or concentrated at a small computed set of medium-load propagators (Cluster 3 says concentrate; Cluster 4 says cross-layer coupling can sometimes save you from needing per-node hardening). No prior digest connects to this run; the W19 retrieval-and-reranking digest is on a different axis and is not continuing here. The applied-systems-thinking outliers carry the run's cleanest *unintended-consequence* exhibit (Pessoa et al.'s Danish pig veterinarians: legislative AMU restrictions unintentionally increased aminoglycoside and macrolide usage) — a Goodhart-style cautionary tale you should hold close when you write any agent-fitness metric the orchestrator will optimize on.

## Themes

### Cluster 1 — Emergence becomes a measurable property when you fix the right interaction-graph topology and look at competing feedbacks at the right scale

The four papers in this cluster collectively argue that "emergent behavior" is not a vague hand-wave — it is operationally diagnosable through a small set of measurements (order parameter turning on, correlation length and relaxation time diverging at criticality, mutual information / transfer entropy / effective-information rising under coarse-graining), and that whether emergence is even *possible* for a given system of interacting parts is decided by the *topology* of their interaction graph and by *how their competing feedbacks scale with N*. Rizi (PNAS Nexus) defines emergence as a many-to-one predictive coarse-graining and bundles the diagnostic signatures; Sacco, Sakthivadivel, Levin (PubMed) prove that clique-and-superclique hierarchies admit an ordered phase while one-dimensional windowed graphs do not — a topology theorem with direct implications for what architectures can hold coherence across scales. Evnin and Krioukov (arXiv) sharpen this with a first-order phase transition that appears when you switch the null model from labeled to unlabeled graphs: below `k_c ≈ log n` the canonical ensemble collapses, and the familiar Erdős-Rényi percolation transition disappears entirely — meaning the "typical coordination pattern" you imagine for interchangeable workers may not exist as a thermodynamic phase at all. Calvert, Richa, Randall (arXiv) close the cluster with closed-form *critical numerosities* `n_c` derived from competing feedbacks scaling differently — and warn that ODE / mean-field approximations of stochastic collectives can fail near `n_c` with *non-monotonic* error.

#### For your orchestrator

- **Classical concept it maps to:** **Phase transition / criticality** (Meadows: the system has regimes, not a single state), made operational via order parameters and correlation lengths. This cluster is the modern empirical face of "structure determines behavior" — at the level of *interaction-graph structure*, not node count.
- **What to observe:** Three things in your orchestrator's trace logs. (i) An order parameter for each lane — the fraction of sub-tasks in that lane reaching the lane's success state — and whether it shows a sharp threshold against branching factor. (ii) The *coarse-grained mutual information* `I(lane_t; lane_{t−τ})` between lane states at lag τ, and whether it spikes near transitions in throughput. (iii) The chromatic / modularity number of the inter-lane communication graph: if your fan-out lanes interact through a topology closer to a 1D chain (each lane only talks to its neighbor in some ordering) than to a clique-of-cliques, Sacco-Sakthivadivel-Levin says you cannot expect global coherence across lanes regardless of how strong intra-lane signaling is.
- **What to tune to improve it:** The *interaction-graph topology between lanes at fan-in*, not the per-lane compute budget. Restructure fan-in as a clique-of-cliques (small cliques of related lanes whose aggregator clique passes to a higher-tier aggregator) rather than a flat MapReduce gather — Theorem 4 + Proposition 3 from Sacco et al. give the topological precondition for global coherence (`ℓ/r > n_max^{n_max}/e`). Also: watch your critical numerosity. Calvert's result says the regime change in `lane_count → n_c` is *not* visible to your ODE-equivalent capacity-planning model.
- **Failure mode this catches early — and the feedback loop:** *Premature convergence under mis-sized population* (the orchestrator runs `N` parallel agents and locks into a degenerate equilibrium that an `N+1`-sized run would not have hit). Early-warning signal: the order parameter's variance across replicates spikes at the same `N` where Calvert-style competing-feedback analysis predicts a critical numerosity (e.g., for an `O(1)` exploration rate versus an `O(N)` coordination cost, expect `n_c ≈ ratio`). Closing the loop: when run-to-run order-parameter variance exceeds a fixed threshold for the current `N`, the orchestrator automatically retries at `N − 1` and `N + 1` (cheaper than a full restart) and compares — if either side is qualitatively better, dispatch future runs at that count.

### Cluster 2 — Useful collective regimes self-organize from a feedback loop on a single per-agent asymmetry, not from designed control of the distribution

These five papers all argue, from very different substrates, that you do not design the steady-state distribution of agent behaviors directly — you build a feedback loop on an asymmetric per-agent quantity and let the population settle. Lesmana et al.'s EvoSK is the cleanest mechanism statement: a Bak-Sneppen-style extremal replacement rule (kill the lowest-fitness agent, respawn with a fresh exploration temperature drawn from a kernel that brackets the underlying critical temperature) drives the system to the edge of ergodicity breaking, with criticality as the *byproduct* of the survival filter rather than a target. Nitti et al.'s SCM does the same trick in continuous time: a noise amplitude σ(t) ratchets up when global consensus C(t) stays near 1 for time τ, with per-agent λₖ(t) damping noise on well-positioned agents — a balancing loop made of two opposed levers. Krebs and Sneppen's talker/listener model shows the same loop running over communication asymmetry (speak-attend-remember), producing emergent hierarchy and an integration-vs-fragmentation regime boundary. Bergoin et al. demonstrate the principle in spiking neural networks: two functionally distinct inhibitory subpopulations (Hebbian and anti-Hebbian) plus spontaneous-recall events maintain modular memory assemblies under continuous local plasticity *with no external homeostasis*. Harré, Drysdale, Ruiz-Serra position Theory of Mind as the asymmetric primitive that distinguishes human social collective intelligence from non-social emergence — a Bayesian belief module that lifts an information-theoretic collective-intelligence proxy from 0 to 1 bit in a worked 3-agent example.

The cluster's internal tension is *what* the asymmetry should be on: exploration temperature (EvoSK, SCM), communicative role (Krebs/Sneppen), inhibitory plasticity rule (Bergoin), or recursive belief order (Harré). The shared move is that the loop is on a *single* per-agent scalar with a *known sign of effect*, not on a vector of tunables.

#### For your orchestrator

- **Classical concept it maps to:** **Balancing loop with a self-regulating respawn distribution** (Meadows' canonical balancing-loop primitive, sharpened by the cybernetic principle that the controller must contain a model of the regime change in the controlled). The kernel from which low-performing agents are respawned must *bracket* the regime transition of the underlying task — EvoSK is explicit that the U[0,2] mutation kernel works because it straddles the SK critical temperature `Tc = 1`.
- **What to observe:** The *empirical distribution of an asymmetric per-agent parameter* across your living population at every fan-in barrier — for an LLM orchestrator this is typically temperature, top-p, max-tool-calls, or context budget. Plot it as a histogram each cycle. Concentrate-on-one-value = collapse; uniform = explosion; concentrated-near-a-broad-band = on-the-edge (EvoSK's α ∈ [0.3, 0.8] band). Also: the *time integral of consensus* (SCM's I(C, τ)): if it stays near 1 for longer than τ, your lanes have stalled and you should ratchet exploration up before they hard-lock.
- **What to tune to improve it:** *The respawn distribution of low-performing agents*, not the mean parameter. Specifically: when an agent in a lane is killed by your stuck-detector, draw its replacement's per-agent scalar (temperature, max-tool-calls, retrieval-k) from a distribution that brackets the suspected regime of the current task. The orchestrator's leverage knob is *the width and centering of the respawn kernel*, not the per-agent values directly. Krebs/Sneppen adds: enforce a minimum *listening floor* — every lane must consume at least k upstream messages per cycle before producing — to suppress hub dominance in the lane communication graph.
- **Failure mode this catches early — and the feedback loop:** *Premature convergence + hub dominance* (one lane's outputs dominate the fan-in aggregator, freezing the population's effective diversity). Early-warning signal: declining entropy of the per-lane contribution distribution at the aggregator, combined with rising self-loop correlation `f̄(s, s')` in agent state transitions (Li et al.'s Cohen's-κ-style diagonal — when an agent's most likely next state is the same as its current state, it has stopped exploring). Closing the loop: when entropy drops below threshold or the diagonal κ exceeds threshold, the orchestrator kills the bottom-quartile agents and respawns them from a *wider* kernel than the current population's std — measured automatically and re-applied every cycle.

### Cluster 3 — Cascade resilience comes from concentrated hardening at medium-load structural propagators identified by local signals — uniform redundancy and classical hub protection misallocate the budget

Four papers agree, across very different topologies (synthetic ER and scale-free graphs, hierarchical command-and-control, urban rail, US air transport), that the load-bearing nodes for cascade propagation are *not* the high-degree hubs that classical centrality measures target. Singh, Chandrasekar, Senthilkumar identify "fragile, medium-degree, well-connected" nodes via a graph-coloring degree threshold using only local fragility — protecting these brings survival probability near 1.0 while highest-degree, closeness, Katz, and betweenness baselines sit at 0.2-0.5 at matched protected-set size. Zhao et al. extend this to asymmetric many-to-one C2 hierarchies and show a hybrid (similarity + residual-capacity + two-layer-centrality) redistribution policy holds resilience 15-20% above conventional baselines, with the largest gains exactly where the network is most stressed. Pal and Ganguly find on 9 urban rail networks that *modularity* is the strongest single predictor of fragility under targeted attack (Kendall τ = −0.65 vs Domirank, −0.64 vs flooding) while *average degree* is the strongest recovery predictor (τ up to +0.82, R² up to 0.81). Liu and Zhao train a GraphSAGE to predict node-level cascade scale and use it to apply +0.3 to the redundancy coefficient β only at the top-ranked nodes — raising a composite resilience metric R from 0.874 to 0.883 at lower cost than uniform β-boost.

The tension is on *which signal*: Singh wants graph-coloring + fragility (purely structural, no training); Zhao wants a hybrid weighted sum (no tuning procedure reported); Pal wants modularity and average degree (N = 9 cities, no partial correlation); Liu wants a trained GNN (single network, no cross-dataset replication). They agree on the architecture of the answer — concentrate the budget — and disagree on the signal.

#### For your orchestrator

- **Classical concept it maps to:** **Cascading failure with leverage-point identification** (Meadows: leverage points often sit at the structure of information flows, not at the highest-flow nodes). The empirical claim is that the cascade-bearing nodes are mid-load nodes whose neighbors are *also* near their fragility threshold — protecting hubs wastes budget because hubs have built-in resilience (`(k−1)/k` stays high when they lose one link).
- **What to observe:** For each lane in your orchestrator: (i) its *fractional load* — current pending work divided by its declared capacity — and a binary "fragile" flag when this exceeds θ (Singh's `(k−1)/k < θ` criterion translated to per-lane utilization); (ii) the *fragility status of its dependent lanes* — how many downstream lanes are also fragile; (iii) the *modularity of the inter-lane dependency graph* — your fan-out structure is implicitly a graph (each lane reads from one or more upstream lanes), and high modularity is a fragility signal under targeted disruption. Compute these three quantities each cycle. Avoid the trap of monitoring only the highest-throughput lanes — they're the resilient hubs.
- **What to tune to improve it:** *The redundancy coefficient* (replica count, retry budget, capacity headroom) applied selectively at *fragile-medium-throughput-with-fragile-dependents* lanes — not uniformly. Liu/Zhao's empirical pattern: a localized +30% capacity boost at the top-k computed-critical lanes beats a uniform +10% across all lanes for the same cost. Pal/Ganguly adds: lower modularity in the fan-out dependency graph by adding a few cross-module dependencies (a few lanes that read from multiple module's upstreams) — this trades some fragility-under-targeted-attack for substantial recovery-speed gain.
- **Failure mode this catches early — and the feedback loop:** *Cascading lane-failure under load spike* (one lane saturates, redistributes work to neighbors, who saturate in turn). Early-warning signal: a count of lanes whose fractional load exceeds θ AND whose dependents are also fragile — Singh's two-condition fragility predicate. Closing the loop: when this count crosses a threshold for two consecutive cycles, the orchestrator automatically increases capacity headroom *only* at those nodes (not uniformly), drawing budget from idle high-degree lanes (resilient hubs). The protection set is recomputed each cycle from local signals — the orchestrator does not need a global view of the cascade trajectory.

### Cluster 4 — A hierarchical orchestrator's leverage lies in a small specialist tier feeding an aggregator with internal recurrence, and in cross-layer coupling that no single layer reproduces

Four papers converge on a single architectural primitive: hierarchies that work well do not have uniform agents reporting to one mean-pool — they have a *small specialist tier* feeding an *aggregator with internal recurrence*, and they exploit *cross-layer coupling* to produce phenomena that no constituent layer can reproduce. Wyart et al.'s zebrafish vsx2+ reticulospinal circuit is the cleanest biological exhibit: rostral r6-r7 "Start" and "Steering" specialist populations feed a caudal r8 hub with local recurrent excitation, and the latency hierarchy (≥60 ms hub-mediated forward versus 10-15 ms direct steering) is the temporal signature of priority-of-control. Zhu, Dorigo, Heinrich's SoNS makes the same architecture artificial: peers self-assemble a rooted graph with a swappable brain that *temporarily centralizes* coordination without losing decentralized fault tolerance, and uses the global-vs-local actuation split to let an LLM write global behavior directly when the swarm gets stuck (17/20 simulation success; the failure mode is always the LLM-returned code removing the brain's stuck-detection pathway). Koifman, Barel, Bruckstein push the principle to its minimal limit: identical memoryless non-communicating agents under four local rules plus an *anonymous-leaders* broadcast layer (where the load-bearing quantity is the *ratio* of receivers to swarm size, not which agents lead) can perform decentralized task allocation by force summation. Battiston et al.'s 81-page multiplex-networks review supplies the cross-layer theory: percolation, diffusion, synchronization, epidemics, opinion, and games all produce phenomena on layered architectures that are absent from any single layer or from the weighted aggregated graph, classified into three structural mechanisms (inter-layer correlations, inter-vs-intra coupling, dynamically correlated heterogeneous processes).

The cluster's internal tension is *how much* hierarchy. SoNS argues for a *temporarily* centralized brain that is interchangeable; Koifman argues for *no* designated leaders at all (leadership emerges per-step from a reception probability); Wyart shows that even biological hierarchies depend on aggregator-internal recurrence rather than pure top-down flow. The shared answer is that the aggregator must have *internal dynamics* — it is not a sum, it is a recurrent integrator.

#### For your orchestrator

- **Classical concept it maps to:** **Modular decomposition with information-flow hierarchy** (Meadows: information flow is itself a leverage point), made concrete as "specialist → aggregator with internal recurrence → broadcaster." The multiplex review adds the cross-layer claim: when your orchestrator runs two coordination layers (e.g., a task-decomposition layer and a tool-call layer), their *coupling ratio* is a sharper transition control than either layer's intrinsic capacity.
- **What to observe:** Three signals at your fan-in barrier. (i) *Aggregator-internal hold time* — the time the aggregator spends combining lane outputs before issuing a downstream command. If this is near zero, your aggregator is a sum and the recurrent integration the cluster identifies as load-bearing is missing. (ii) *Specialist-to-aggregator latency ratio*: Wyart reports ~6 for steering-to-forward; if your fast lanes finish in 10s and your slow aggregator runs in 60s, the ratio is already in the right shape. (iii) *Cross-layer edge overlap* — for any two coordination layers you run (planning vs tooling, for example), the fraction of agent pairs that interact on both layers; Battiston identifies edge overlap as the critical structural control whose absence collapses multiplex enhancements.
- **What to tune to improve it:** Three levers. (i) The *branching factor* at the specialist tier and the *aggregator buffer hold time* together — these are the duals of Wyart's latency gradient. (ii) The *broadcast reception ratio* in your fan-out (Koifman's 20% / 50% / 80%) — increasing the fraction of lanes that listen to the orchestrator's directive (vs. inferring from peer behavior) saves you from connectivity-recovery problems when lanes drop. (iii) The *inter-layer coupling weight* between your two coordination layers — Battiston's "Mechanism 2" says tuning this single parameter often produces sharper transitions than tuning either layer's intra-coupling.
- **Failure mode this catches early — and the feedback loop:** *Cascading collapse of macroscopic coordination when an inter-layer link is overloaded* (Buldyrev-type catastrophic cascade in interdependent networks — discontinuous, not graceful) — combined with *the orchestrator's stuck-detector itself being overwritten by an in-place agent update* (the recurring SoNS failure: the LLM-edited MISSION program removes the stuck-detector). Early-warning signal: a sudden drop in aggregator-internal hold time *while* fan-in queue depth holds steady — the aggregator has stopped integrating and started passing through. Closing the loop: every cycle, checksum the orchestrator's *invariant* code (stuck-detector, kill-switch, escalation thresholds) against a frozen reference and freeze any agent self-update that would touch the protected region. SoNS explicitly identifies static/update-able-code separation as the missing piece.

### Cluster 5 — A handful of local-rule scalars produces a discrete menu of collective regimes with sharp transitions you can locate analytically

Four papers, on very different substrates (numerical optimization, drone-like multi-agent flocking, evolutionary game theory on hypergraphs, autonomous nanorods in low-Reynolds fluid), share one claim: collective behavior across a population settles into a *small finite menu of regimes*, and which regime appears is controlled by one or two scalar dials with a sharp critical value. Biswas and Elliott use particle-swarm optimization as the numerical engine for maximum-entropy reconstruction from up to ~1000 moments — a population of low-capability agents collectively solving a problem no single agent can, coupled through a global-best channel (abstract-only). Jond's minimal flocking model proposes that one sign-changing scalar `ψ` (cohesion below δ, separation above) plus one sign-changing scalar `φ` (alignment below η, divergence above) gives flocking / vortexing / swarming from a single local rule per coordinate, with `η` as the primary phase-transition driver. Li et al. show in a Q-learning evolutionary public-goods game on hypergraphs that conditioning on co-players' past actions produces *two* abrupt transitions (rather than one), separating absence-of-cooperation / medium-cooperation / high-cooperation regions, with the first transition derived in closed form (`r̂₁* ≈ (1 − ε/2) / [(1 − ε/2) + γ(1 − ε/2)^|e|]`). Afsari and Ahmed demonstrate the principle physically: autonomous Au-Ru-Pt trisegmented nanorods couple purely hydrodynamically into a discrete set of locked phase relationships ({0°, 90°, 180°} for co-rotators, none for counter-rotators) and self-organize into stable 3-, 4-, and 5-body clusters.

The cluster's lesson is that you do not get a smooth dial from local rule to collective behavior — you get a staircase. The transition points are computable from the scaling of competing feedbacks (echoing Cluster 1's critical numerosity), and the regime menu is *finite and discrete*, not a continuum.

#### For your orchestrator

- **Classical concept it maps to:** **Bifurcation / phase transition on a small parameter** (Meadows: the system has stable states with finite barriers between them; small parameter changes can jump regimes). The empirical thrust is sharpened: the regimes are *enumerable in advance* and the critical points can be located from competing-feedback scaling rather than discovered empirically.
- **What to observe:** (i) The *regime histogram* across recent orchestrator runs — bin each completed run by an observable like average lane utilization or convergence step count, and ask whether the histogram is multi-modal with stable peaks (the discrete regime menu) or unimodal-broad (between regimes). (ii) The *transition character* — when you change branching factor or temperature by a small amount, does the regime histogram jump to a new peak (true phase transition) or shift smoothly (continuous response). The first is the staircase, the second is the regime-interior. (iii) The *intra-regime dwell time*: nanospinners' locked states last "several cycles" before sudden transitions — your orchestrator's regimes likely also have intermittent dwell, and a dropping dwell time is an early-warning signal of regime drift.
- **What to tune to improve it:** *The single scalar that the discrete-regime analysis identifies as the primary driver*, not the parameter you find most expressive. For Jond's flocking model that scalar is `η` (kinetic offset / equilibrium relative speed). For your orchestrator the equivalents are typically *the per-cycle escalation threshold* (when does a lane request help from a parent) and *the per-cycle exploration probability*. Li et al.'s analytical first transition `r̂₁* ≈ (1 − ε/2) / [(1 − ε/2) + γ(1 − ε/2)^|e|]` translates: long-sightedness γ lowers the cooperation threshold, exploration ε raises it — tune the discount factor (how long the agent considers consequences) before tuning the exploration rate.
- **Failure mode this catches early — and the feedback loop:** *Sudden regime jump on a small parameter nudge* (you tune branching factor from 5 to 6 and the orchestrator's behavior reorganizes qualitatively) — the operational name is *brittle bifurcation*. Early-warning signal: the *finite-size crossing point* across replicate runs (Li et al.'s odd/even-N crossing) — when small variations across replicates produce qualitatively different aggregate outputs, you are near a transition. Closing the loop: maintain a per-week regime histogram across a fixed reference workload; when the histogram's modal peak shifts between weeks, the orchestrator automatically runs a small sweep around the current parameter to locate the new critical point and pins itself to the *broader band* (recall EvoSK's α ∈ [0.3, 0.8]: not knife-edge, broad-band stability is the operational target).

## Outliers — Applied systems-thinking practice surfaces unintended-consequence loops and clarifies which leverage points actually matter, even when the competence-transfer claim itself remains unmeasured

These four papers are honest outliers in *methodology* — they use participatory System Dynamics Group Model Building (GMB), causal loop diagrams (CLDs), and Meadows-style iceberg models with humans in the loop, rather than mathematical or simulation work. They share an argument: rendering events → patterns → systemic structures → mental models explicitly surfaces feedback loops and unintended-consequence dynamics that static checklists miss. Pessoa et al. carries the run's cleanest *Goodhart / unintended-consequence* exhibit — Danish legislative AMU restrictions *unintentionally increased* aminoglycoside and macrolide usage, a substitution effect visible only because the iceberg-model layer of systemic structures was made explicit. Wan et al.'s chlamydia CLD identifies health education as a high-leverage intervention sitting at the intersection of one balancing and one reinforcing loop in a 47-variable map across four subsystems. Sperber et al.'s JMIR protocol anchors on an existing null-effect study (clinicians ignored an ML early-warning score 86% of the time) and argues this kind of failure cannot be diagnosed by static facilitator/barrier inventories — the underlying structure is feedback-driven (perception → use → trust → use) and demands GMB to surface. Lemke et al. is the cluster's honest caveat: a pre/post evaluation of SD GMB with 32 community stakeholders found *no statistically significant overall gain* in systems-thinking quiz scores, with hedged subgroup-level increases only — the long-assumed capacity-transfer claim of participatory SD remains empirically unsettled.

> No clean orchestrator translation as a four-bullet block — these are field-internal methodology papers showing classical Meadows-lineage practice in operation. Read them at the 3K level for two concrete instruments: (i) the iceberg model is the right rendering for any post-mortem on an orchestrator change that produced unexpected downstream behavior (events → patterns → systemic structures → mental models, in that order); (ii) Pessoa et al. is the cautionary tale you should hold close before writing any agent-fitness metric the orchestrator will optimize on — a "reduce X" rule will reliably produce a substitution effect at a node the rule did not name.

## Closing: How this run lands for your orchestrator

If you build only three instruments from this run, build these. First, from **Cluster 3 (Cascade resilience)**: per-lane fractional load combined with the fragility status of each lane's dependents, computed every cycle from local signals only — this is what tells you which lanes to harden, and the answer is *not* the highest-throughput ones. Second, from **Cluster 2 (Self-regulation as leverage)**: the histogram of an asymmetric per-agent scalar (temperature, max-tool-calls, retrieval-k) across your living population, plus the time integral of consensus at each fan-in barrier — these tell you whether your population is collapsing, exploding, or sitting in the broad-band edge regime that EvoSK and SCM identify as the attractor of a working feedback loop. Third, from **Cluster 4 (Hierarchical architecture)**: a checksum of your orchestrator's invariant code (stuck-detector, kill-switch, escalation thresholds) against a frozen reference — this catches the one failure mode that recurred in every Zhu et al. simulation failure, where an in-place agent self-update deleted the very mechanism that would have detected the stuck state.

The failure mode you are most likely to hit first is *premature convergence with stuck-detector overwrite* — the orchestrator's lanes lock into a low-diversity equilibrium and the in-place agent update mechanism rewrites away the safety rail that would have flagged it. In classical terms: a reinforcing loop on agent-self-update closes over the balancing loop that monitors lane diversity (Cluster 2 + Cluster 4 in collision). The single automated feedback loop to build first is the *invariant-code checksum + lane-diversity entropy* pair: every cycle, refuse any agent-self-update that touches the protected region, and when lane-output entropy drops below the rolling-mean's first standard deviation, kill the bottom-quartile lanes and respawn them from a wider parameter kernel than the current population's std — measured automatically. This single loop combines Cluster 4's static/update-able code separation and Cluster 2's broad-band respawn principle, and gives you the most early-warning coverage at the lowest implementation cost.

What this run did *not* cover, and you should not pretend it did: the classical canon itself — Forrester's original *Industrial Dynamics*, Senge's *Fifth Discipline* archetypes (limits-to-growth, shifting-the-burden, tragedy-of-the-commons), Ashby's law of requisite variety stated in its original 1956 form, and Beer's Viable System Model — is not on arXiv or PubMed. The papers here operationalize fragments of that canon empirically and mathematically; they do not replace the canon. You should read Meadows on the twelve leverage points alongside these results, and treat Pessoa et al.'s pig-veterinarians paper as your run-specific reminder that any reward signal you give the orchestrator will produce a substitution effect somewhere the signal did not name.
