# Synthesis style for this run

The reader of this digest is a practitioner / researcher who already knows the standard RAG stack and wants to know **which retrieval and re-ranking ideas from the last 6 months have actually landed, and which recent ones are worth tracking even though they are too new for citations.**

The synthesis is **top-down** — write 30K (what changed across the 6 months) first, then the two tiers (Established / Emerging), then per-paper bullets within each tier.

---

## Layer 1 — 30,000 ft: What changed across the 6-month window

One paragraph, 3-5 sentences, that answers: **what is the cohort of work from the last 6 months that matters, and what is its through-line?**

Required content (in this order):

1. **The through-line across the kept papers.** What is the field collectively doing in retrieval / re-ranking for LLM agents in this window? If no through-line exists, say so — fake coherence is worse than honest scatter.
2. **The split between established and emerging.** Which directions have anchor papers with visible adoption (established) vs which are recent enough to lack citations but show clear signal (emerging)?
3. **What did NOT change.** What is the field still stuck on, still not doing, still disagreeing about? This is high-information.
4. **The disagreement, if any.** When the kept papers argue against each other, surface it here.

### Good 30K paragraph pattern

> Across the last 6 months, the cohort of retrieval/rerank-for-LLM-agents work converges on one move: the optimization target shifts from IR relevance to downstream LLM utility. The established core of this shift is [X, Y, Z] — three approaches with N+ citations each and visible follow-on. The emerging edge — too recent for citation counts to matter — is [A, B], where the move is to remove the retriever entirely and let the agent grep / navigate / skill-tree the corpus directly. What did not change: there is still no shared benchmark for agentic-RAG efficiency, and the field continues to publish against incompatible baselines. The window's main tension is [training-free vs trained rerankers / vector-index vs no-index / cluster-1 vs cluster-2].

### Anti-patterns

- Listing cluster names with one sentence each. That's the next layer.
- Citing individual papers by author in the 30K paragraph. That's the bottom layer.
- Hedging with "various developments occurred" / "several papers explored." Restart with the through-line question.
- Banned vocabulary: delve, unpack, paradigm shift, let's explore, moreover, furthermore, it's worth noting.

---

## Layer 2 — Two tiers: Established and Emerging

This run **replaces the default cluster-by-theme layout** with a two-tier split. Within each tier you may still group papers by theme (sub-clusters), but the top-level structure is:

```
## Established (citations / visible adoption)
  ### Sub-theme A (if it makes sense)
  ### Sub-theme B
  ### Sub-theme C
  ### (etc.)

## Emerging (recent, traction signal but pre-citation)
  ### Sub-theme A
  ### Sub-theme B
  ### (etc.)
```

### Tier rules

**Established tier** — a paper goes here if any of the following are true:
- Semantic Scholar citation count ≥ 10 within the 6-month window.
- The paper is being cited by 2+ other kept papers in this run's kept set (cross-reference signal from extraction).
- The paper is from a recognized group (Anthropic, DeepMind, Meta AI, Google Research, OpenAI, leading academic IR/NLP labs) and has been out ≥ 8 weeks with measurable engagement.

**Emerging tier** — a paper goes here if any of the following are true:
- Published in the last ~8 weeks (after 2026-03-15) and citation count is naturally low.
- Proposes a clear novel mechanism with a named system and a sizable headline number on a named benchmark.
- Is being referenced by other recent papers in the kept set (early-signal cross-reference).

If a paper meets the established bar, it goes in established — even if it is very recent. Ties break toward established when the cross-reference signal exists in the kept set.

### Each tier's sub-themes

Within each tier, group papers into 2-5 sub-themes (the same theme-clustering shape the standard digest uses). Each sub-theme needs:

1. **A claim-shaped header** — "Rerankers shift their target from IR relevance to downstream LLM utility" — not just "Reranking."
2. **A 2-3 sentence summary** that states the sub-theme's collective claim, cites the strongest 1-2 papers inline, and surfaces any tension.

### Layer 2 anti-patterns

- A paper in the wrong tier. If a paper has 30 citations but the digest puts it in emerging because it's "interesting," the tier signal is broken.
- A sub-theme that exists only in established and disappears in emerging (or vice versa) without comment. If the emerging tier is the next generation of an established theme, say so — the connection is itself the signal.
- Sub-themes that span both tiers without acknowledging it. If "rerank-for-utility" has established papers and emerging papers, say it's a maturing theme.

## Layer 3 — 300 ft: Per-paper bullets, within each sub-theme

For each paper, one bullet:

```
- **{Title}** — {first author et al.}, {source}, {YYYY-MM-DD}. {one sentence on
  what it does and why it belongs in this sub-theme.} *Traction:* {citation count
  or traction reasoning}. → [link]({url})
```

The one-sentence description must:

1. Say what the paper *does* (method, result, benchmark, diagnostic) — not what it's "about."
2. Connect to the sub-theme's argument — i.e., why this paper landed here.
3. Preserve the paper's own hedging. If the abstract says "suggests," the bullet says "suggests." Never upgrade hedging to "shows" or "proves."

The *Traction:* line is required. Format examples:

- Established: `*Traction:* 24 citations on Semantic Scholar; cited by 3 other papers in this run's kept set.`
- Established (recognized group): `*Traction:* From a major IR lab; preprint posted 2026-02-04; 17 citations.`
- Emerging: `*Traction:* Posted 2026-04-26; pre-citation but the no-retriever-at-all framing is being referenced by [other paper in kept set].`
- Emerging (no signal yet, kept on methodological merit): `*Traction:* Pre-citation. Kept on the strength of named system + named benchmark + reproducible setup.`

---

## Cross-layer consistency checks (run before writing the digest file)

1. **Upward**: does the 30K paragraph reflect what the two tiers collectively say? If a sentence in 30K is not supported by either tier, delete it or check whether you missed a sub-theme.
2. **Tier integrity**: every paper in Established meets at least one of the established-tier rules (with the citation count / kept-set reference / recognized-group reason on the line itself).
3. **Tier connection**: where emerging is the next generation of an established theme, the digest says so explicitly.
4. **Downward**: each sub-theme's summary matches the papers listed under it.

If any check fails, fix before writing.

---

## Banned vocabulary

Do not use in any text the synthesizer produces:

- delve
- unpack
- paradigm shift
- let's explore
- moreover
- furthermore
- it's worth noting

Do not use vague adjectives without a quantitative or mechanistic anchor:

- "important" (without naming why)
- "exciting" / "interesting" / "novel" (without naming what is new)
- "various" / "several" / "a number of" (use a number when you know it; describe the shape when you don't)
